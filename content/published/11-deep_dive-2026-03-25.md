## Executive Summary

New York Democrats control the governorship, a 41-22 state Senate supermajority, a 103-47 Assembly supermajority, and a majority of the state's congressional delegation. They cannot pass single-payer healthcare legislation that has had sufficient co-sponsors for seven years. They cannot override a governor who vetoed 140 bills in 2025 despite being one seat short of the override threshold. They have not solved the problem that 86% of their own voters identify as the defining issue of daily life: affordability.

I assess 92% probability that Democrats hold the governorship in 2026. I also assess that the party is structurally eroding beneath its electoral dominance — 550,000 fewer registered Democrats since 2022, a presidential margin that halved from 23 points to 11.6 in a single cycle, and a Senate minority leader at 27% approval, the lowest since tracking began in 1999. The fastest-growing voter bloc in New York is not Republican. It is no party at all — 3.1 million unaffiliated voters, now exceeding Republican enrollment statewide. New Latino registrants are choosing "blank" at 42%, up from 34% two cycles ago. This is dealignment, not realignment. Voters are not switching sides. They are leaving the field.

The mechanism is not ideological gridlock. It is institutional capture. The New York Health Act — single-payer healthcare, first introduced in 1991 — has passed the Assembly five times and currently holds 32 co-sponsors in a 63-seat Senate. It has never received a Senate floor vote. The Build Public Renewables Act had 83 Assembly sponsors when 76 were needed. Speaker Heastie refused to schedule it. The real estate industry spent $13.6 million lobbying Albany and gave Governor Hochul $4.8 million personally. The hospital lobby's former budget director consults for the industry while it lobbies his old agency — a documented financial relationship, though the causal connection to specific budget outcomes has not been independently established. Leadership controls the floor calendar. Donors fund leadership. Popular legislation dies in committee. Co-sponsors claim credit without ever casting a vote.

This is not proof that Democrats cannot govern. Minnesota passed paid family leave, legal cannabis, gun safety reform, and universal free school meals in a single session with a one-seat Senate majority. Colorado enacted a public health insurance option. Washington State taxed millionaires and survived a ballot repeal by 22 points. New York is the worst-performing major blue-state trifecta in the country — not the template for Democratic governance, but a warning about what happens when donor capture, closed primaries, and leadership insulation go unchecked.

Meanwhile, a dual-income family in Dutchess County earning the median income of $97,000 takes home roughly $6,063 per month after taxes. Their basic monthly expenses — rent, childcare, healthcare, utilities, food, transportation — total $9,281. They are $3,218 short every month before clothing, savings, or a single emergency. The party that controls their government has not closed that gap. I assess that the party controlling their government has chosen not to tax the income bracket that pays a roughly comparable effective rate to the middle class — approximately 13.5% versus 13.8%, a difference within methodological uncertainty but directionally consistent with ITEP's broader finding that New York's tax system is not genuinely progressive at the top (ITEP, "Who Pays?" 7th Edition). That is a choice, not a constraint.<sup>1</sup>

## The Numbers

Five data streams tell the same story from different angles. Here is how I read them — registration, elections, polls, money, and demographics all point to the same center of gravity: a party that is electorally dominant and coalitionally sick.

### Registration

New York Democrats peaked at approximately 6.5 million registered voters in 2022. As of March 2026, enrollment stands at 5,949,834 — a loss of roughly 550,000 in three years (NY State Board of Elections, queried 2026-03-06). New York City accounts for the bulk: approximately 500,000 fewer registered Democrats since 2021, dropping from 3.8 million to 3.3 million (Newsweek, 2026-02-14).

The voters are not becoming Republicans. Republican enrollment declined from 24.93% to 22.41% of the electorate over the same period. The growth is in "blank" — New York's designation for voters who decline party affiliation. Unaffiliated voters now constitute 25.24% of the statewide electorate, surpassing Republican enrollment for the first time. In the New York City suburbs — Nassau, Suffolk, Westchester — unaffiliated voters grew from 26.41% to 28.91% between 2021 and 2025, now exceeding Republicans there as well (NYPIRG Capitol Perspective, 2025-11-10).

The demographic profile of the unaffiliated bloc matters: nearly half are under 40. Over 25% are under 30. They turn out at 49.3% in general elections versus 63.4% for party-affiliated voters — a 14-point gap that makes them simultaneously the fastest-growing and least reliable segment of the electorate (NYC Campaign Finance Board, 2025).

### Elections

The 2024 presidential result was the worst Democratic performance in New York since 1988. Kamala Harris carried the state by 11.6 points — half of Joe Biden's 23-point margin four years earlier. Five counties flipped from Biden to Trump: Nassau (14-point swing), Rockland (13-point swing), Broome, Clinton, and Essex. Trump became the first Republican to exceed 30% of the New York City vote since George H.W. Bush. The Bronx swung 11 points toward Trump. Queens swung 10.5. In Bensonhurst, Brooklyn — predominantly Asian — Trump earned 70% of the vote (Spectrum News, 2024-11-07; amNY, 2024-11-08).

Then 2025 happened. Democrats flipped county legislatures in Onondaga and Dutchess that had been Republican for decades. Greece, outside Rochester, elected its first Democratic supervisor in over a century. The Hudson Valley swept blue — Ulster, Orange, and Dutchess all flipped seats. Troy's city council went entirely Democratic. And in New York City, Zohran Mamdani — a democratic socialist endorsed by the Working Families Party (WFP) — won the mayoralty with over 2 million votes, the highest turnout since 1969. Youth turnout tripled. 75% of voters under 30 chose Mamdani (Spectrum News, 2025-11-05; NPR, 2025-11-04; CIRCLE/Tufts, 2025).

These results are not contradictory if you understand what each election measures. Presidential races measure national brand and base enthusiasm. Local races measure organizational capacity, candidate quality, and the absence of alternatives. New York Democrats are losing the brand war while winning the ground game. The question is how long ground game compensates for a brand that 67% of the party's own voters say frustrates them.

### Polls

The national Democratic Party recorded its lowest favorability in Gallup's history in 2025: 34%, below the prior floor of 36% set in November 2014. Quinnipiac measured 30% favorable, 57% unfavorable — the worst since they began asking in 2008 (Gallup, 2025; Quinnipiac, 2025-09).

In New York specifically:

- **86%** of residents say the state is "not affordable" or "not very affordable" (Marist, 2026-02, n~1,000, margin +/-3pp)
- **33%** plan to leave the state within five years (Marist, 2026-02)
- **56%** say the state is headed in the wrong direction (Marist, 2026-02)
- **67%** of Democrats say their own party frustrates them; 30% say they are angry (Pew Research, 2025-09; national poll, NY-specific subsample not available)
- **Chuck Schumer's approval: 27%**, the lowest since Marist began tracking in 1999. Among Democrats specifically: 50% negative (Marist, 2026-02)
- **Kirsten Gillibrand's approval: 31%**, the lowest since 2010 (Marist, 2026-02)
- Governor Hochul's favorability hit a personal best of 49-40% in January 2026, but **57% of voters said they preferred "someone else"** through most of 2025 (Siena Research Institute, 2025-2026 tracking polls)

The one consistent bright spot: healthcare. New Yorkers trust Democrats over Republicans on protecting affordable health insurance by 49-26% (Siena, 2025-11). That advantage is about to be stress-tested when 470,000 people lose Essential Plan coverage on July 1.

### Money

The financial signals tell two stories moving in opposite directions.

Grassroots energy is at historic highs. ActBlue processed $1.78 billion in 2025, a 41% increase over 2021. New York's new public campaign financing system doubled small-donor participation — from 12% to 49% of state legislative campaign funding in a single cycle. Mamdani raised $17.2 million on an average donation of $99. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez (AOC) has already surpassed her full 2024 cycle fundraising total. New York Democratic House candidates outraise Republicans roughly 3:1 (ActBlue, 2025 annual report; NYC Campaign Finance Board, 2025).

Institutional infrastructure tells the opposite story. The Democratic National Committee (DNC) holds $12.6 million in cash against $17 million in debt. The Republican National Committee (RNC) holds $90 million. Outside spending is hostile: the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) super political action committee (PAC) deployed $96 million in 2024, Fairshake (crypto-backed) deployed $191 million, and the combined right-leaning and corporate super PAC arsenal exceeds $700 million — against roughly $80 million on the progressive side (OpenSecrets, 2025; FEC filings).

The base is more engaged than ever. The institutional party is broke and outgunned by outside forces actively selecting which Democrats survive primaries.

### The Latino Signal

The most consequential demographic shift in New York politics is not a realignment. It is a dealignment.

In 2024, Latino-majority neighborhoods in Queens — Corona, Elmhurst, East Elmhurst — swung 18 points toward Trump. The Bronx swung 11. Washington Heights shifted 14. The driver was economic anxiety, not cultural conservatism: 64% of Latino voters cited economic issues as their top concern (Equis Research, 2024-04). The migrant crisis strained services in exactly the neighborhoods that shifted. A firefighter in the Bronx told CBS: "A lot of the points that he made about securing the border, that's kind of number one."<sup>2</sup>

In 2025, Mamdani recovered most of the ground. He won 58% of votes in neighborhoods where more than half the residents are Latino, and 85% of young Latino voters chose him (CIRCLE/Tufts, 2025; Hispanic Federation poll, 2025). But the South Bronx — Soundview, Longwood, Hunts Point — showed the weakest bounceback. Andrew Cuomo won most South Bronx enclaves in the Democratic primary before losing citywide.

The registration data tells the structural story. Among new Latino registrants in New York City between 2022 and 2024 (n=87,252), 43% chose Democratic (down from 55% in the 2018-2020 cycle, n=201,878) and 42% chose no party affiliation (up from 34%). The base sizes differ by more than 2:1, meaning the 2022-2024 cohort is smaller and potentially more self-selected — but the directional shift is consistent across both cohorts. Total new registrations dropped 57% (NYC Board of Elections enrollment data; City Limits analysis, 2024-12).

The Trump protest vote is reversing — Pew found 70% of Latinos nationally disapprove of Trump as of November 2025, and the 2025 off-year elections showed an 18-point swing back toward Democrats in Latino-heavy New Jersey counties. But the party affiliation numbers are not reversing. Latino voters in New York are transitioning from automatic Democratic to persuadable swing. They voted for Mamdani, not for the party. That is a structural change, even if the specific 2024 Trump gains have evaporated.

**Bottom line: every signal — registration, elections, polls, money, demographics — converges on the same finding. The erosion is real. The floor holds for now. The floor is held together by Republican weakness and individual candidate quality, not by faith in the Democratic Party.**

## The Kitchen Table

The numbers in the previous section describe a political phenomenon. This section describes what it feels like to live inside it.

Brian, a Democratic campaign strategist from the Hudson Valley, framed the question that prompted this analysis: voters have lost faith in the Democratic-led legislature and governor's office because of their inability to respond to the federal onslaught and the evolving needs of working families. He is worried that New York's stagnation under unified Democratic control previews what would happen nationally. His concern is grounded in the budget his neighbors cannot close.

### The Budget That Doesn't Work

Consider a dual-income household in Dutchess County — two working adults, two children ages 2 and 5, renting a three-bedroom apartment, earning the county median income of $97,000.

| Expense | Monthly | Annual | % of Gross Income |
|---------|---------|--------|-------------------|
| Rent (3BR, Dutchess County avg, Zillow/HUD FMR 2026) | $3,278 | $39,336 | 40.6% |
| Childcare (toddler + preschool, NYS OCFS market rate) | $2,800 | $33,600 | 34.6% |
| Healthcare (employer-sponsored, worker share) | $571 | $6,850 | 7.1% |
| Utilities (electric + gas, Central Hudson) | $350 | $4,200 | 4.3% |
| Food (family of 4, USDA moderate plan) | $1,300 | $15,600 | 16.1% |
| Transportation (1 car + Metro-North monthly) | $700 | $8,400 | 8.7% |
| Car insurance | $282 | $3,384 | 3.5% |
| **Total** | **$9,281** | **$111,370** | **114.8%** |

Gross monthly income at $97,000: $8,083. After federal, state, and Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) taxes at an approximate 25% effective rate, take-home is roughly $6,063. Against $9,281 in basic expenses — no student loans, no savings, no clothing, no emergencies — this family is **$3,218 short every month**.

Subsidies help. If both children qualify for childcare assistance, that saves approximately $1,000 per month. A cheaper two-bedroom saves $276. But even with optimistic adjustments, this family spends every dollar and builds no wealth. One car repair, one emergency room visit, one broken furnace puts them in debt.

This is not a hypothetical designed to shock. It is what the median income buys for a family in this configuration — two working parents, two young children, one car, a rented apartment. Household compositions vary; families without childcare costs face a shortfall closer to $400. But childcare is the dominant driver at 34.6% of gross income, and 60% of Dutchess County families with children under 6 use paid care. The median income is real. The expenses are sourced. The gap is arithmetic.

### Utilities: The Slow Bleed

New York residential electricity rates averaged 27.39 cents per kilowatt-hour in December 2025 — 59% above the national average of 17.24 cents. Rates have risen 45% since passage of the Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act (EIA, queried 2026-03-25).

In the Hudson Valley specifically, the Public Service Commission approved a three-year rate plan for Central Hudson — the utility serving Dutchess, Ulster, Columbia, Greene, and parts of Orange County — effective September 2025:

| Year | Electric Increase | Gas Increase | Combined Monthly Impact |
|------|------------------|--------------|------------------------|
| 2025-26 | 5.5% | 6.73% | +$14 |
| 2026-27 | 5.3% | 9.03% | +$18 |
| 2027-28 | 5.3% | 8.97% | +$18 |

Cumulative by Year 3: roughly $50 more per month for the average dual-fuel customer, on top of a baseline that already exceeds $350 monthly. New York State Electric and Gas (NYSEG), serving parts of the southern Hudson Valley, has filed for a 35% electric delivery increase and a 39.4% gas delivery increase, pending PSC approval for May 2026 (NY PSC rate case filings, 2025-2026).

The distress signal is in the arrears data. As of December 2025, **1.3 million New York households** were 60 or more days behind on utility bills, owing a combined $1.85 billion — 2.5 times the pre-pandemic level. The average household in arrears owed $1,432. Utilities issued 5.2 million final termination notices in 2025 and disconnected service to **408,898 households** (NY PSC consumer data, 2025). These are not statistics. These are voters who turned on the lights and nothing happened.

### Healthcare: Coverage Without Access

New York's uninsured rate is 4.8% — among the lowest in the nation. This number conceals a deeper problem: coverage does not equal access.

A survey of roughly 1,500 New Yorkers by the Community Service Society and Healthcare Value Hub (December 2024-January 2025) found that **68% experienced at least one healthcare affordability burden** in the prior year. 66% delayed or went without care due to cost. 22% cut pills, skipped doses, or did not fill prescriptions. 80% worry about affording healthcare in the future. Among households earning $50,000-$100,000 — solidly working-class and middle-class — 88% reported concern about coverage costs.<sup>3</sup>

The math explains why. Enhanced Affordable Care Act premium tax credits expired at the end of 2025. For a family of four earning $100,000 — just below the new subsidy cliff at 400% of the federal poverty level — a marketplace Silver plan now runs approximately $2,272 per month, or $27,264 per year. That is 27.3% of gross income before they use it once. Add a per-person deductible of $2,450 and an out-of-pocket maximum of $10,150, and total potential healthcare exposure for this family reaches **$20,000 to $40,000 annually** — 20-40% of gross income (Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF) premium analysis, 2026; NY State of Health marketplace data).

Starting July 1, 2026, the picture worsens. The federal "One Big Beautiful Bill Act," signed July 4, 2025, eliminated premium tax credit eligibility for most lawfully present immigrants, destroying the financial basis for New York's expanded Essential Plan. Governor Hochul negotiated with Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) to preserve coverage for 1.3 million enrollees below 200% of the federal poverty level. The remaining **470,000 will lose Essential Plan coverage** and be pushed to marketplace plans — going from $0 monthly premiums and minimal cost-sharing to $564-$979 per month in premiums alone. For someone earning $35,000, a Bronze plan would consume 19% of gross income before a single doctor's visit (NY DOH, 2026-03-23; Fiscal Policy Institute, 2026-03).

The Fiscal Policy Institute calls this "the largest and most rapid loss of access to healthcare in New York's history." The state has budgeted $2.5 billion for FY2027 to cover contingencies. The Community Service Society estimates $2.4 billion would cover all affected populations. As of this writing, the governor has stated the state "cannot afford to backfill federal cuts."

New York spends $14,007 per capita on healthcare — second-highest nationally, 37% above the national average. Despite this spending, the state ranks 23rd in health outcomes. The paradox: New Yorkers pay a premium to live in a state that delivers middling results (Commonwealth Fund, 2025; NYHealth Foundation spending trends).

### Childcare: The Relief That Arrives in 2027

Infant care in New York averages $21,600-$30,000 annually. Toddler care: $16,800-$26,400. Childcare consumes up to 29% of a New York family's income — four times the federal affordability benchmark of 7%.

The Hochul administration has responded with real investment: $4.5 billion total in childcare and pre-K for FY2027, including a $3 billion subsidy pool representing a 3.5-fold increase. Dutchess County will receive a $20 million pilot providing 1,000 seats for children ages 0-3. Universal Pre-K for all four-year-olds is targeted for the 2028-2029 school year.

The investment is genuine. But the first Dutchess County pilot slots come online in **2027**, and universal Pre-K arrives in **2028-2029**. Hudson Valley families are paying $33,600 per year for two children right now, today, in March 2026. Policy that arrives in two years does not close a budget gap that is open this month.

**Bottom line: the affordability crisis is not an abstraction New Yorkers observe from a distance. It is the gap between $6,063 in monthly take-home pay and $9,281 in monthly expenses. Every policy accomplishment the Democratic Party can claim — and there are real ones — is invisible against that arithmetic.**

## The Machine That Isn't

The conventional explanation for legislative inaction under unified government is ideological division: progressives want one thing, moderates want another, and the resulting compromise satisfies no one. In New York, I find this explanation wrong. The blocking mechanism is not ideology. It is agenda control.

### The Override That Never Comes

Democrats hold a 41-22 majority in the State Senate and a 103-47 majority in the Assembly. These are supermajorities by any colloquial definition. They are not, by one seat in the Senate, veto-proof. New York requires two-thirds of both chambers to override a gubernatorial veto — 42 of 63 in the Senate. Democrats hold 41.

Governor Hochul has used this arithmetic aggressively. In 2025, she vetoed **140 bills** and negotiated amendments to 88 more — 228 bills blocked or altered, more than any of her recent predecessors (NY Focus, 2025-12-23). In her entire tenure, the legislature has attempted **zero veto overrides**. Assembly Speaker Carl Heastie has called overrides a "nuclear option" he hopes to "never get to." The calculus is institutional, not ideological: individual legislators depend on the governor's executive agencies for constituent services that help them win reelection. A Democratic consultant explained it plainly: "Why would you want to make the governor angry unless absolutely necessary?"<sup>4</sup>

The result is a structural trap. The governor controls the budget timeline, executive agencies, and the regulatory apparatus. The legislature can pass bills, but if Hochul vetoes them, nobody calls the override vote. Bills that passed unanimously have been vetoed without consequence. The supermajority has not translated into legislative action. The pattern is consistent across Hochul's tenure.

### Two Bills That Prove the Pattern

**The New York Health Act (A1466/S3425)** is the most visible case. First introduced in 1991 by Assemblyman Richard Gottfried, it would establish universal single-payer healthcare for every New York resident — no premiums, no deductibles, no copays, funded by a progressive payroll tax. The Assembly passed it five times between 1992 and 2018, each time when the Republican Senate ensured it would die. The moment Democrats took the Senate in 2019 and the bill had the votes to become law, it stopped advancing in both chambers. It has not received a floor vote in either chamber since 2018.

The bill currently holds 32 co-sponsors in the 63-seat Senate — exactly the majority threshold — and approximately 75 in the 150-seat Assembly, just short of majority. Advocates claim the floor votes exist. Senate Majority Leader Andrea Stewart-Cousins has never scheduled one. Speaker Heastie, who brought it to the floor four times when it could not pass, stopped when it could. His recent statement: "There is more work that needs to be done on the single payer healthcare legislation before it can advance."

For the family in the previous section, the implications are direct. The RAND Corporation's 2018 assessment modeled illustrative financing scenarios (2018 dollars; results were sensitive to rate-setting assumptions) under which approximately 98% of New Yorkers would pay less for healthcare than under the status quo. For a household earning $100,000, the employee share of the payroll tax would run approximately $2,560 per year — replacing $20,000-$35,000 in annual premiums, deductibles, and copays. The wide range reflects modeling uncertainty, not measurement. Net savings to the family under RAND's central scenario: **$17,000-$32,000 per year** (RAND, RR2424, 2018). Healthcare costs have risen substantially since 2018, which means both the current burden and the potential savings are likely larger than RAND modeled.

**The Build Public Renewables Act** followed the same trajectory. The bill had **83 Assembly co-sponsors** when 76 were needed to pass. Speaker Heastie refused to bring it to the floor. It did not receive a vote. Eighty-three sponsors. No vote.<sup>5</sup>

The pattern is identical in both cases: sufficient support exists, leadership does not schedule the vote, co-sponsors collect political credit for supporting legislation they are never required to actually pass, and voters never see their representatives tested. This is not gridlock. It is management.

### Follow the Money

The agenda control pattern becomes legible when mapped against lobbying expenditure.

**Real estate** is the dominant force in Albany's political economy. The Real Estate Board of New York and affiliated organizations spent $13.6 million on campaign contributions and direct lobbying between 2019 and 2023, contacting 10 key legislators nearly 6,000 times. Governor Hochul received at least $4.8 million from real estate interests since becoming governor. Good Cause Eviction — a tenant protection law that would cap annual rent increases statewide — was enacted in 2024 only after being heavily diluted: the final version applies exclusively to New York City, with an opt-in mechanism for other municipalities. As of April 2025, only 17 of New York's 1,500+ municipalities have opted in (NY Senate press release, 2025-04; City & State NY, 2024-03; Read Sludge, 2024-04).

**Healthcare** is the second front. The Greater New York Hospital Association — representing 250+ hospitals — spent $4.7 million on Albany lobbying in 2023 and routed $942,000 to Hochul through the state Democratic Party's "housekeeping" account, a loophole in campaign finance law. GNYHA's financial reserves exploded from $243,000 in 2012 to $211 million by 2022, an 86,000% increase fueled by sales of for-profit subsidiary assets. Its president, Kenneth Raske, has held the position for over 40 years (NY Focus, 2024-02-29).

The revolving door is the quieter mechanism. Robert Mujica — Hochul's former state budget director — began consulting for GNYHA in September 2023. Between October and December 2023, he earned between $100,001 and $1 million from the hospital lobby while GNYHA was actively lobbying his former agency, the Division of Budget. New York law prohibits former state employees from being paid to influence matters before their former agency for two years after departure. That budget cycle, Medicaid funding for hospital services increased by $525 million (NY Focus, 2024-02-29; NY Focus, 2024-10-29).

**Auto insurance and technology** is the newest front. Uber poured $3 million into a PAC called Citizens for Affordable Rates, which listed "support of Hochul" as its electoral objective. Total tech and insurance industry spending on Hochul's auto insurance reform campaign reached $8 million, including hired actors and paid media. The reform was stripped from both chambers' budget proposals. Citizens for Affordable Rates plans to spend $7 million in lobbying in 2026 (NY Focus, 2026-02-03; Streetsblog, 2026-03-13).

### The Union Split

The New York Health Act's failure exposes a fault line within the Democratic coalition that money alone does not explain: the split between private-sector healthcare unions and public-sector unions.

**Supporting single-payer:** 1199 SEIU (450,000+ healthcare workers), the New York State Nurses Association, the Communications Workers of America, 32BJ SEIU. Helen Schaub, 1199's state director: "We know that if we could trade that for universal health care for everyone... we would do that."

**Opposing single-payer:** The Municipal Labor Committee — a coalition of roughly 100 New York City municipal unions representing 1.1 million members. The United Federation of Teachers, which endorsed the NY Health Act in 2015 and 2017 before its leadership reversed position. The Civil Service Employees Association. The Building Trades. The Professional Fire Fighters Association. The Uniformed Firefighters Association. The Uniformed Sanitationmen's Association.

The MLC's opposition letter to Speaker Heastie and Majority Leader Stewart-Cousins, dated May 2021, states the logic explicitly: "For over 50 years, the MLC has bargained health care matters with the City and other NYC-based employers." And: "We have no desire to cede the structure of our health benefits to a large committee in which we would have little representation."

Translation: healthcare benefits are the union's primary recruitment tool. If the government provides healthcare to everyone, the union loses its primary value proposition. Decades of wage sacrifices were traded for superior health coverage. Single-payer would make those sacrifices retroactively pointless. The institutional survival interest of union leadership is directly opposed to the healthcare interest of union members and the broader public.

Gregory Floyd, president of Teamsters Local 237 and MLC secretary-treasurer, sits on EmblemHealth's board of directors. The MLC simultaneously lobbied against single-payer while negotiating to switch 250,000 retirees to Medicare Advantage plans with substantially higher out-of-pocket costs — $7,550 versus $1,053 annually — benefiting the insurance companies whose boards they share (Jacobin, 2021-06).

The 164-member "Realities of Single Payer" opposition coalition — funded opaquely, running television and radio ads — includes both the insurance industry and the public-sector unions that fund Democratic campaigns. When the donors to leadership and the organized base of the party share an interest in blocking legislation, the floor vote does not get scheduled. The mechanism is complete.<sup>6</sup>

**Bottom line: New York's Democratic supermajority is not failing to pass progressive legislation because it lacks the votes. It is failing because the institutional structures that control floor scheduling — leadership offices funded by real estate, hospitals, and unions with structural interests opposed to reform — prevent votes from occurring. The co-sponsors are real. The floor votes are not.**

## The Federal Siege

The affordability crisis documented above is about to worsen. I assess the federal fiscal collision as the most consequential near-term threat to New York's social contract. New York faces an estimated $13.5 billion in annual federal funding losses once all provisions of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (H.R. 1, signed July 4, 2025) are fully implemented — the largest documented federal funding withdrawal from any single state since the 2008-2009 fiscal crisis (FPI, 2026-03).

### The Losses

| Category | Annual Impact | Timeline |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Essential Plan (premium tax credits eliminated) | $7.5 billion | July 1, 2026 |
| Medicaid (provider tax freeze, expansion match erosion, work requirements) | $9-15 billion (10-year avg) | Phased 2026-2030 |
| SNAP (cost shift from 50/50 to 75% state) | $1.4-2.2 billion | October 2027 |
| Infrastructure (frozen grants) | $18 billion (one-time) | Immediate |
| Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) grant terminations | $535 million+ | 2025-2026 |
| Education (proposed 26% Title I cut) | ~$500 million at risk | FY2026 |
| Housing (proposed 43% Section 8/9 cut) | Billions | FY2026 |

Per SD #9, the federal legislative machinery producing these cuts — including the SAVE Act pipeline and partisan impact of documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements — represents a deliberate strategy of fiscal pressure on blue states.

The Fiscal Policy Institute estimates the combined state and healthcare system impact at **$12.5 billion in the first two fiscal years alone**, rising above $20 billion annually by 2030. State Comptroller Tom DiNapoli projects a **$34.3 billion cumulative budget gap** through state fiscal year 2029 (FPI, 2026-03; NY Comptroller, 2025-08).

The Essential Plan loss is the most immediate crisis. The 470,000 New Yorkers losing coverage on July 1 are disproportionately concentrated in the boroughs that swung hardest toward Trump in 2024: Queens (16.4% of residents enrolled), Brooklyn (12.9%), and the Bronx (12.7%). They are overwhelmingly working poor and gig economy workers earning 200-250% of the federal poverty level — $31,000 to $39,000 individually. They currently pay nothing. Starting July 1, they face marketplace premiums of $564-$979 per month (NY DOH, 2026-03-23; CBS New York, 2026-03).

Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) benefits, already reduced to an average of **$6.40 per person per day** — less than two meals in New York — face expanded work requirements (80 hours per month for adults 18-64) and a cost shift that will push $1.4-2.2 billion annually onto the state budget. Nearly 3 million New Yorkers receive SNAP. Over 300,000 households are projected to lose some or all benefits (NY Comptroller, 2025-07).

### Albany's Response

Attorney General Letitia James has been the most effective responder. New York is leading or co-leading roughly a third of the 51 multistate lawsuits filed against the Trump administration, with notable wins at the Supreme Court on International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) tariffs, at the appellate level on the federal funding freeze, and at the district level on National Institutes of Health (NIH) research grant cancellations.

Governor Hochul's response has been reactive on acute crises and passive on structural ones. When Trump cut SNAP during the October 2025 government shutdown, Hochul declared a state of emergency and deployed $106 million in emergency food assistance within days. On the Essential Plan, she successfully negotiated with CMS to preserve coverage for 1.3 million enrollees below 200% of the poverty level. These were real actions with real impact.

On the larger structural losses, the pattern is different. The state has explicitly declined to backfill the Essential Plan gap for the 470,000 above the threshold. No state supplemental SNAP benefit has been proposed. No dedicated litigation war chest has been established — compare California, which allocated $25 million in a special legislative session and claims $188 billion in federal funding protected. The comprehensive immigrant protection package — the New York for All Act — has majority support in both chambers and has not passed. Advocates requested $165 million for immigrant legal services; the budget includes $64.2 million.

The Fiscal Policy Institute's analysis is the most direct critique: the state's budget gap estimates "assume the state will allow 1.5 million people to lose health insurance with no state remedy." This is not a projection of what might happen. It is a description of the current budget posture.<sup>7</sup>

### The Fiscal Vise

New York is a net donor state. For every dollar New York sends to Washington, it receives approximately $1.04 back — versus a national average of $1.32. Per capita, New York receives $674 in federal spending above its tax contribution, compared to the national average of $4,099. The state ranks **45th per capita** in federal funding received (Rockefeller Institute, 2025).

Hochul's own framing deserves a hearing. On the tax question: "Go down to Palm Beach and see who you bring back home — our tax base has become eroded." On philosophy: "I, philosophically, don't have a problem" with taxing the wealthy — she frames her opposition as pragmatic, not ideological (NY1, 2026-03-11). I assess the data does not support the pragmatic case she makes, but the position is not irrational. It is a bet on competitive positioning that the evidence suggests she is losing.

I assess the administration is exploiting this imbalance deliberately, based on the pattern of targeted action. The White House Office of Management and Budget ordered all federal agencies to compile detailed reports of funding sent to 14 blue states and Washington, D.C. — explicitly including New York. Trump's infrastructure funding freeze targeted $18 billion for New York City, home to Democratic House leader Hakeem Jeffries and Senate minority leader Schumer. Moody's warned of "heightened credit risk" to blue states from the freezes (CBS News, 2026-01; NPR, 2025-10; Bond Buyer, 2025).

The federal siege creates a fiscal vise with only two jaws: raise taxes on the wealthy or cut services. Hochul cites outmigration as her reason for opposing tax increases, but the academic evidence contradicts this framing — Stanford's research on millionaire migration finds semi-elasticities below 0.1, and the Fiscal Policy Institute documents that high earners leave New York at one-quarter the rate of the general population (Young & Varner; FPI migration analysis). The outmigration risk is real but small. The revenue from progressive taxation consistently exceeds any marginal departure effect. Refusing to raise taxes is a political choice, not a fiscal constraint. The budget deadline is April 1. As of this writing, the governor's $262.7 billion proposal and the Senate's $269.8 billion proposal remain $7.1 billion apart, with the legislature pushing for revenue measures the governor has rejected.

**Bottom line: the federal government is withdrawing funding at a scale that demands a state-level response. The state government has the tools — a $252 billion budget, $21 billion in reserves, a legislature that supports revenue measures. It lacks the political will to use them. The party that controls every lever of state government is choosing not to pull the ones that would require confronting its donor class.**

## The Counter-Example

If the New York pattern were inherent to Democratic unified control, every blue-state trifecta would exhibit it. I looked. They do not.

**Minnesota** controlled its state Senate by a single seat in 2023 — the narrowest possible majority. In one legislative session, it passed:

- 12 weeks of paid family and medical leave
- Codified abortion rights (first state post-Dobbs)
- Universal background checks and red flag gun legislation
- Free school breakfast and lunch for all K-12 students
- Free public college for low- and middle-income families
- Legalized recreational marijuana
- Expanded Medicaid
- Trans refuge law

The governor signed everything. The American Prospect's Blue State Power Index ranked Minnesota at the top. One seat. No excuses about margins or political risk. The legislature used its majority to pass legislation. The governor signed it.<sup>8</sup>

**Colorado** enacted a public health insurance option — the Colorado Option — that enrolled 132,791 people by 2025, covering 47% of the individual marketplace. It passed six major land-use reform bills in a single session: banned occupancy limits, required accessory dwelling unit allowances, eliminated parking minimums near transit. It created a $700 million child poverty reduction program with a $3,200-per-child tax credit and committed to 65% greenhouse gas reduction by 2035.

**Washington State** passed a 7% capital gains tax on high earners that survived a ballot repeal by 61% of voters. It enacted a cap-and-invest carbon market that has generated billions in revenue and also survived ballot repeal. In its 2025-2026 session, it passed a 9.9% millionaire's income tax.

**New York** has a 41-22 Senate majority and a 103-47 Assembly majority. It has not passed single-payer healthcare, statewide good cause eviction, a public renewables mandate, or a millionaire's tax increase. Its governor vetoed 140 bills in 2025 and the legislature attempted zero overrides.

The comparison is not close. The structural question is why.

Three factors distinguish New York from states that use their trifectas:

**Closed primaries.** New York is one of only 10 states that prohibits unaffiliated voters from participating in primary elections. With 3.1 million unaffiliated voters locked out, Democratic primaries are decided by a smaller, older, and more institutionally connected electorate. This insulates incumbents from accountability. In states with open or semi-open primaries, legislators who ignore their base face broader electorates that include independents. New York's closed system means the accountability mechanism that forces action elsewhere is structurally weakened.

**Concentrated donor power.** New York's political economy is dominated by a small number of industries — real estate, healthcare, finance — with intense Albany lobbying operations and direct financial relationships with legislative leadership. The $13.6 million in real estate lobbying, the $4.7 million from GNYHA, the $8 million from Uber-backed PACs — these are not national patterns replicated locally. They are uniquely concentrated in Albany. Minnesota's donor landscape is more diffuse. Colorado's is smaller. The per-legislator lobbying pressure in Albany has few state-level peers.

**Leadership culture.** Speaker Heastie's refusal to schedule votes on bills with majority co-sponsors is not a structural feature of legislative systems. It is a choice made by a specific person in a specific institutional culture. California's Assembly Speaker has also blocked single-payer — AB 1400 died without a vote in 2022 — but California's legislature passes housing reform, climate legislation, and tax increases that Albany will not. The difference is not one blocker but the *pattern* of blocking: in New York, leadership suppresses floor votes across multiple domains simultaneously, creating a systematic rather than episodic failure.

The campaign strategist from the Hudson Valley asked whether New York previews what would happen under federal Democratic unified control. The answer is: partially. The donor capture mechanism — organized interests funding the leadership that controls the floor calendar — exists at the federal level. The Manchin-Sinema episode that killed Build Back Better's social spending provisions shares DNA with Albany's pattern. But the federal system has different constraints (the filibuster, the Supreme Court, genuine ideological diversity among senators from red states) and different accountability mechanisms (open primaries in most states, competitive general elections in swing districts).

Other trifecta states have their own failures — California killed single-payer in 2022, Illinois has not solved its pension crisis — but no major blue state combines New York's margin of control with New York's breadth of legislative inaction. New York is not a preview of federal Democratic governance. It is the most extreme expression of a failure mode that exists within the Democratic coalition at every level — and the counter-examples prove it is not inevitable.

**Bottom line: Minnesota used a one-seat majority to transform state policy. New York used a 19-seat majority to veto 140 bills and schedule zero overrides. The difference is not structural. It is leadership, donor capture, and a closed-primary system that insulates incumbents from the voters they claim to represent.**

## What This Means

For Democratic voters in New York, the data in this analysis describes a party that wins by default. Hochul leads Blakeman by 20 points not because voters believe in her governance but because they see no alternative. The 2025 local wave was real — but it was powered by specific candidates running against the establishment (Mamdani), against Trump (Hudson Valley flips), or in uncontested territory. Remove those conditions and the underlying erosion remains: half a million fewer registered Democrats, two-thirds of the base frustrated with its own party, and a median-income family that cannot cover basic expenses under unified Democratic government.

For the national party, New York is a warning, not a template. The donor capture mechanism that blocks floor votes in Albany — organized interests funding the leadership that controls the calendar — exists at the federal level. Build Back Better's social spending died from the same pathology. But Minnesota, Colorado, and Washington prove the mechanism is not inevitable. The question is not whether Democrats can govern under unified control. The question is whether they will address the internal structures — closed primaries, concentrated donor power, leadership insulation — that prevent governance from translating into the material improvements voters actually need.

For the 2026 cycle, the primary insurgency is the accountability mechanism. Brad Lander is challenging Hakeem Jeffries' ally Dan Goldman in NY-10, leading 52-33 in early polling with endorsements from Mayor Mamdani and Senator Sanders. Chi Osse is challenging Jeffries himself in NY-8. The Working Families Party is endorsing challengers across the state. DSA is running its largest-ever legislative slate. These campaigns function as the primary accountability mechanism in a closed-primary system where 3.1 million unaffiliated voters cannot participate. Whether they succeed determines whether the institutional calculus on floor scheduling changes.

For the affordability crisis specifically, the honest assessment is that no political outcome in 2026 solves the kitchen-table math. A family $3,218 short every month is not made whole by a primary upset in one Assembly district. But the structural reforms that would eventually close that gap — healthcare cost reduction through single-payer, housing supply through zoning reform, revenue through progressive taxation — all require confronting the donor class that currently controls the floor calendar. The primary insurgency is the first step in a sequence, not the last.

Two dates matter more than November:

**April 1, 2026** — the state budget deadline. Whether Albany raises revenue from the wealthy, backfills the Essential Plan gap, and funds the social safety net that federal cuts are dismantling will determine whether the party's response to crisis matches the scale of the crisis itself. As of this writing, it does not.

**July 1, 2026** — the Essential Plan cliff. 470,000 people move from $0 healthcare to $564-$979 per month. If this happens under unified Democratic government with no state remedy, the affordability brand crisis documented in this analysis accelerates. If the state intervenes, it will be the clearest test of whether the federal siege can force Albany's hand where voter frustration alone has not.

**Ongoing — Schumer 2028.** Whether AOC announces or Schumer retires restructures every power dynamic documented in this analysis. If Schumer retires, the progressive-establishment contest shifts from primary challenge to open-seat scramble. If AOC announces, every Albany alliance is provisional. If arrears exceed $2 billion or disconnections exceed 500,000 in 2026, the affordability crisis is intensifying regardless of what politicians do in Albany. Monitor: PSC consumer data reports, quarterly.
