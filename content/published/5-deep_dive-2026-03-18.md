## Executive Summary

Three shocks are hitting the US economy simultaneously, and the historical record for this combination is unambiguous.

Brent crude sits at approximately $106 as of March 17, up from a pre-conflict baseline of approximately $65 -- a 63% increase driven by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz following the escalation of the Iran-Israel-US conflict.

Effective US tariff rates have risen to approximately 17% following the Supreme Court's invalidation of IEEPA tariffs and their replacement under Section 122 authority -- the highest effective rate since the early 1930s.

The Federal Reserve has held the funds rate at 3.64% for sixty consecutive days while core PCE sits at 3.1% and real GDP runs at 0.7% annualized.

These three vectors -- energy shock, trade disruption, constrained monetary policy -- are arriving simultaneously.

The historical frequency table for this combination is the analytical foundation of this assessment, not narrative. In the modern US record, there are exactly two episodes where all three vectors were present simultaneously: 1973-1975 (OPEC embargo, Nixon price controls, Fed funds at 12%) and 1979-1982 (Iranian Revolution, auto trade disruption, Volcker at 19%). Both produced severe recessions -- GDP contracting 3.2% and 8% annualized respectively, unemployment peaking at 9.0% and 10.8%. The compound shock recession rate is 2-for-2, n=2. Every episode where only one or two vectors were present -- 1990-1991, 2015-2016, 2019 -- produced either a mild recession or no recession, because the Fed retained the ability to respond. That circuit breaker is absent in 2026.

I assess **44% probability of technical recession** (two consecutive negative real GDP quarters, NBER declaration) and **62% probability of growth recession** (GDP below 1.0% annualized for two or more quarters) in 2026. The divergence between these two estimates reflects the single most important structural difference from the 1973 and 1979 analogs: inflation is running at 3.1% core PCE, not 12-15%. The Fed constraint is real but less absolute. Consumer balance sheets remain structurally sound -- household debt service at 11.3% versus 13.2% at the 2007 peak. That combination makes growth recession the more probable outcome: the economy stalls without formally contracting, because the demand-side floor holds even as supply-side costs compress margins and household purchasing power. The central question is not whether growth deteriorates -- the data shows it already has -- but whether the deterioration clears the technical threshold before the compound shock resolves.

The headline number is -92,000 jobs in February 2026 -- total nonfarm payrolls fell from 158,558K in January to 158,466K. Every analysis I've seen leads with that figure. It's the wrong frame.

That's approximately 15,000 jobs per month -- below population growth, below the rate needed to absorb new labor market entrants.

The gap is not rounding error -- it's the difference between a functioning labor market and one running at stall speed. The February -92K print is not an inflection point. It's the plateau cracking. The labor market was already at the edge; February is when it stepped off.

### 2. Unemployment (FRED UNRATE)

Unemployment stands at **4.4%** as of February 2026 (FRED UNRATE, 2026-02-01). The cycle trough was 3.5% in July 2022. The rise since then has been steady: 3.5% in mid-2022, 4.0% in January 2025, 4.4% now -- a 0.9 percentage point increase from the low.

The Sahm Rule gets misread in most coverage of this data. The indicator is not the unemployment rate -- it's the difference between the current 3-month moving average of unemployment and the minimum 3-month average from the prior 12 months.<sup>2</sup> The Sahm indicator stands at **0.267 percentage points** as of February 2026 (FRED SAHMREALTIME).

The trigger threshold is 0.50. We are not there. FRED's own SAHMREALTIME series confirms this -- it shows 0.27 for February 2026. Financial coverage claiming the Sahm Rule has "triggered" at 4.4% unemployment is conflating the unemployment rate with the indicator. They are different numbers.

What the data shows: the labor market is deteriorating at a measurable pace, and we are roughly halfway to the Sahm trigger. If March unemployment prints at 4.5-4.6% while the prior-12-month minimum holds at 4.1%, the indicator crosses 0.50. That data releases in early April.

### 3. Yield Curve (FRED T10Y2Y) -- Featured Finding

The 10Y-2Y Treasury spread is **0.52 percentage points** as of March 17, 2026 (FRED T10Y2Y). That number, read in isolation, looks unremarkable -- a positive spread, historically consistent with expansion. The trajectory is what matters.

From the FRED data: the spread peaked at **0.74** on both January 30 and February 9. It held at 0.74 on February 5 and again on February 9, then broke sharply lower on February 11 (0.66), accelerated through February (0.62 → 0.60 → 0.59), and continued compressing through March (0.58 → 0.55 → 0.52). **22 basis points of compression in 5 weeks.** Roughly 30% of the spread evaporated while oil spiked 41% and payrolls went negative.

In a standard supply shock, you expect bear steepening: long rates rise faster than short rates because markets price in sustained higher inflation. That's the textbook response, and it's not what the data shows. The 10-year yield did rise -- from roughly 3.95% in late February to 4.28% by mid-March, a "war premium" as one Bloomberg-cited piece described it. But the spread still compressed because the 2-year yield rose too, held up by a Fed that markets correctly assess cannot cut into 3.1% core PCE.

This is the stagflation signature in bond market arithmetic. The 10-year is being pulled in opposite directions: growth fears drag it down, inflation fears push it up. The net: it doesn't rise as fast as a pure inflation shock would warrant. Meanwhile the 2-year stays elevated because rate cuts are off the table. The spread compresses. The bond market is not pricing growth or inflation as separate problems -- it's pricing them as a simultaneous policy trap.

The historical analog is 1973-74, not 1990. The 1990 Gulf War oil shock doubled crude from ~$17 to ~$36, but the Fed could cut quickly and the recession lasted 8 months. The current disruption -- Strait of Hormuz accounting for ~20% of global oil supply, closed without ceasefire in sight -- matches the 1973 Arab embargo in structural terms. In 1973-74, the yield curve followed a specific sequence: initial steepening on inflation panic, then compression as the recession became undeniable, then eventual inversion as the Fed held rates high through the contraction. The FRED data puts us at stage 3 of that sequence. The spread compressed from 0.74 to 0.52 in five weeks. If the pattern holds, continued flattening or inversion follows as the market fully prices stagflation rather than treating inflation and growth weakness as separate shocks.

The T10Y2Y at 0.52 and compressing reads as: the bond market assigns roughly even odds that the Fed is trapped and the economy stalls while inflation remains above target. That assessment is priced in real-time yield data, not a sentiment survey or an economist's model. It is the most current recession signal in the dataset.

## Historical Parallels

The most useful thing I can do here is tell you which historical analogs actually apply to 2026 -- and which ones Wall Street is misusing to justify optimism.

The answer requires a framework. Not a feeling.

### The Compound Shock Frequency Table

I've reviewed every US recession since 1970 against three structural variables: an energy price shock, a trade disruption, and a constrained Federal Reserve. The pattern is unambiguous.

### The 1973-1975 Analog (Closest Match)

November 1973: the OPEC embargo drives oil from $3 to $12 per barrel in four months -- a 300% increase. Nixon's wage and price controls, already distorting domestic supply chains, compound the shock. The Fed, facing 12.3% CPI, raises the funds rate to 12% and cannot ease.

The recession runs 16 months. GDP falls 3.2%. Unemployment peaks at 9.0% and does not return below 6% until June 1978 -- four years later.

I assess 2026 as stage 3 of that sequence. Stage 1 was the latent vulnerability: stalled labor market, elevated core inflation, inverted yield curve. Stage 2 was the triggering shock: Hormuz closure, oil +41% in 8 trading days. Stage 3 is the transition now underway -- the market repricing from "inflation shock" to "recognized stagflation." In the 1973-75 cycle, this transition took approximately 60-90 days from the embargo announcement to broad consensus that a severe recession was in progress.

This one is 15 days in.

### The 1979-1982 Analog (The Severe Case)

The second episode is the extreme. The Iranian Revolution cuts 4.8 million barrels per day from global supply. Oil doubles. Paul Volcker, appointed in August 1979, deliberately engineers a recession to break 15% inflation, pushing the funds rate to a peak of 19%.

The result is the worst postwar recession by most measures: a double dip (January-July 1980, then July 1981-November 1982), GDP contracting at 8% annualized in Q2 1980, unemployment reaching 10.8% at the December 1982 trough.

The structural parallel to 2026 is the compound nature: an oil shock severe enough to drive stagflation, paired with a Fed that cannot ease because inflation is already above target. In 1979-82, the inflation was 15% and the Fed chose to tighten aggressively. In 2026, the inflation is 3.1% and the Fed is holding steady at 3.64% -- constrained, but not at war with the economy.

That distinction matters. Return to it in a moment.

### The 1990-1991 Analog (The Bull Case)

This is the outcome Wall Street wants to apply to 2026. The Gulf War drives oil from $17 to $36 per barrel in three months -- a 110% increase. The recession is real: eight months, GDP -1.5%, unemployment reaching 7.8%.

But it is mild. The reason is the third variable. Alan Greenspan's Fed *does not tighten* in response to the oil spike. It cuts rates -- from 8.25% to 6.75% during the crisis, then all the way to 3% by 1992. Oil falls back to pre-war levels by April 1991. The compound shock is two-variable, not three, and the Fed circuit breaker fires.

The 1990-1991 analog is instructive precisely because it shows what saves you: a Fed that can respond, and an oil shock that resolves within quarters, not years.

### Why 2015-2016 and 2019 Fail as Analogs

Both periods circulate as soft-landing precedents. Both fail the compound shock test.

2015-2016: Zero of three shocks present. Oil crashed from $100 to $26 -- a deflationary shock, not an inflationary one. The transmission mechanism is opposite. Cheap oil is a consumer stimulus, not a stagflationary burden. The Fed was below its inflation target and had complete freedom to act. No trade disruption. The manufacturing weakness and credit spread widening that occurred were absorbed because the Fed put was intact. This is not 2026.

2019: One of three. The US-China tariff war added bilateral trade friction, but the effective rate was narrow and the Fed was actively cutting (three mid-cycle cuts). Unemployment was at a 50-year low of 3.5%. There was no oil shock -- WTI was stable at $50-65 with no supply disruption. The single-vector stress was absorbed because the other two variables were neutralized. That is not the current situation.

The reason these episodes are cited as bullish analogs is that they ended without recession. The reason they ended without recession is precisely that the compound shock framework was incomplete. The circuit breaker worked because it existed. In 2026, the circuit breaker -- the Fed's ability to cut -- is degraded.

### The Structural Difference That Could Save 2026

The 1974 and 1980 recessions happened with CPI at 12-15% and fed funds at 12-19%. The constraint was absolute. In 2026, core PCE is 3.1%. The Fed is frozen, but not by the same magnitude.

This is not a small distinction. It means the Fed *can* cut if conditions deteriorate enough -- if GDP collapses, if payrolls fall for three or four consecutive months, if the oil shock transmits into demand destruction rather than sustained inflation. The compound shock model is historically validated at 100% recession rate, but the severity range spans from -1.5% GDP (1990-91, two shocks) to -8% annualized (1979-82, three shocks with extreme monetary tightening).

The question for 2026 is whether Jerome Powell breaks toward growth before or after significant economic damage accumulates. The 1990-91 playbook requires him to move early. History suggests central banks move late.

## Threat and Opportunity Assessment

### Threat 1: The SPR Inventory Cliff (July 2026)

The Strategic Petroleum Reserve release -- 172 million barrels over approximately 120 days, coordinated with 228 million barrels across 31 other IEA member nations -- is the largest emergency release in history by a significant margin.

It is also temporary.

The release began March 16 at an implied rate of 1.43 million barrels per day. US commercial crude inventories, which had already built 24 million barrels since January (a number that puzzled market observers until the SPR pre-positioning became clear), will continue to hold or build through roughly mid-May. The EIA weekly data will show "adequate" supply. This is the window of artificial calm.

Phase 2 begins as the first 86-million-barrel tranche exhausts around mid-May. Delivery rates will slow as pipeline logistics from Bryan Mound, West Hackberry, and Bayou Choctaw bind against physical throughput limits.

Phase 3 -- the cliff -- arrives in mid-July when the 172 million barrels are exhausted and the SPR sits at approximately **243 million barrels**, the lowest level since the reserve was being filled in the early 1980s.<sup>1</sup>

If Hormuz remains significantly disrupted at that point -- 40-45% probability per the duration model below -- commercial inventories begin drawing immediately and aggressively. The 2022 precedent is instructive: when Biden's 180-million-barrel release ended, the market re-priced within weeks. There is no second reserve release on the table. **The compound risk:** July 2026 is the convergence point. SPR exhaustion intersects with the 90-day mark on the Hormuz disruption duration model -- the last point where the historical probability of continued closure remains above 40%. **Phase 4 -- the 2027 replenishment problem -- is already embedded in the release structure.** This was not a drawdown. It was an exchange. A second price pressure point is baked in regardless of how 2026 resolves.

### Threat 2: The Hormuz Duration Model

No Hormuz crisis in history has involved full commercial closure. The 1984-1988 Tanker War -- 451 merchant ships attacked over four years -- never actually closed the Strait because Iran depended on the same sea lanes for its own exports. That self-interest brake was the implicit constraint on Iranian escalation in every prior episode.

It no longer exists. Iran's exports are being destroyed by the strikes. The Supreme Leader has twice rejected US ceasefire proposals. The new Iranian leadership is publicly framing Hormuz closure as a strategic tool rather than a tactical threat. The usual asymmetry -- Iran needing the Strait to sell oil -- has been eliminated by the air campaign.

I model the duration distribution from the available de-escalation pathways:

## Pattern Recognition

### Pattern 1: The Yield Curve-Oil Divergence Is a Stagflation Signature

**What the data shows:** The 10Y-2Y Treasury spread has compressed from 0.73% in late December to 0.52% as of March 17 (FRED T10Y2Y) -- a monotonic 21 basis-point flattening in under three months. Simultaneously, Brent crude has risen roughly 50% from its pre-conflict baseline. These two signals are pulling in opposite directions.

A supply-side inflationary shock -- oil up 50% -- should, under standard conditions, steepen the yield curve. Higher inflation expectations push up long-term yields faster than short-term yields. The curve should be steepening. It is not. It is flattening.

The bond market is pricing demand destruction as the dominant force, overriding the inflationary supply shock. That is the definitional signature of stagflation: inflation rising faster than the central bank can tolerate cutting, growth falling faster than the central bank can tolerate holding. The long end of the curve reflects the growth-destruction outcome. The oil price reflects the inflation-transmission channel. Both can be right simultaneously, and here, they appear to be.

**Evidence quality:** High. FRED data current through March 17. Both series are confirmed, not estimated.

**Invalidation criteria:** If the 10Y-2Y spread begins widening (steepening) while oil remains at current levels, the bond market has revised its view and the demand-destruction read is wrong. Watch the March 19 FOMC reaction in rates for the initial signal. A post-dot-plot steepening would suggest the market sees a policy path out of the trap; a continuation of the flattening confirms it doesn't.

A secondary invalidation: if oil retreats below $80 as supply routes reopen, the divergence resolves without stagflation having materialized.

**When bonds and implied vol disagree, bonds are usually right.** VIX at 23.51 has actually declined from its March 6 peak of 29.49 despite the geopolitical situation worsening. The bond market's steady flattening -- not spiking, not reversing, just compressing -- is the less dramatic but more structurally meaningful signal.

Moody's model and JPMorgan's 40% estimate use NBER dating criteria, which includes "growth recession" scenarios where GDP is positive but below trend, unemployment is rising, and economic activity is broadly contracting. A growth recession (GDP 0-1%) would trigger the institutional estimates but not the Kalshi contract.

I estimate growth recession probability at approximately **62%** and technical recession probability at approximately **44%**. The difference -- roughly 18 percentage points of scenarios where economic deterioration is real but formal technical recession is avoided -- maps closely onto the 15-point institutional-market spread. Both are correctly calibrated for what they are measuring. The "gap" is not evidence of market underpricing or institutional overpricing. It is evidence that they are measuring different things with different contracts and different models.

**Evidence quality:** Medium. Prediction market prices from web research, not real-time feed (Kalshi contract queries returned 404). The definitional analysis is based on published contract terms. The probability estimates are my own synthesis from the full data set.

**Invalidation criteria:** This pattern -- definitional gap explaining the spread -- would be wrong if evidence emerged that Kalshi traders are actually pricing a materially lower probability of broad economic deterioration than institutions. Alternatively, if Moody's revises downward by 10+ percentage points while Kalshi holds at 34%, the definitional explanation weakens.

### Pattern 4: The SPR Release as Severity Signal

**What the data shows:** The IEA announced a coordinated 400-million-barrel emergency release from member-state strategic petroleum reserves on approximately March 14. The US share was 172 million barrels over four months. Oil prices rose 5% on announcement day (*PBS*, 2026-03-14; *CNBC*, 2026-03-14). The largest emergency oil reserve release in history was treated by markets as a fear-amplifier.

This is the rare case where the policy response itself became the signal. Strategic petroleum reserves exist for exactly this scenario -- they are the calibrated emergency response to major supply disruptions. Activating them at maximum scale communicates to markets that the disrupting governments believe the underlying supply problem is severe enough to warrant the largest-ever draw on emergency stocks. Markets processed the announcement accordingly.

The EIA data adds a counterpoint that deserves explicit acknowledgment: US commercial crude inventories built 24 million barrels from January 2 to March 6 (419,056K to 443,103K barrels). That build is partly why the SPR release appears to have had no price effect -- the SPR volumes are flowing into commercial storage while demand destruction from high prices reduces drawdowns. The inventory build is structural camouflage, not genuine supply adequacy. Singapore fuel oil at a record $140/bbl and the suspended Fujairah terminal confirm the physical tightness that US headline inventory numbers obscure.

The SPR has a four-month duration at current drawdown rates. If Hormuz remains closed through Q3, the emergency buffer expires and inventories face a cliff.

**Evidence quality:** High for the price response (documented by multiple sources on announcement day). Medium for the inventory interpretation (EIA data current through March 6, SPR release mechanics are well-documented, but physical market dynamics are inferred from Singapore and Fujairah reporting rather than direct US inventory decomposition).

**Invalidation criteria:** If subsequent weeks show inventory draws despite continued SPR releases -- meaning SPR volumes are not sufficient to offset the supply disruption -- the "artificial build masking genuine tightness" thesis is confirmed and the severity signal strengthens. If inventories continue building beyond the SPR release period, it would indicate domestic production and non-Hormuz imports are genuinely compensating for the closure, and the physical supply shortage framing is too aggressive.

### Pattern 5: Independent Methodological Convergence on Growth Recession

**What the data shows:** A multi-agent economic simulation -- running bottom-up agent-based modeling where simulated manufacturers, consumers, bankers, and policymakers interact under the same macro conditions -- independently arrived at growth recession (GDP 0.5-1.5%, positive but below potential) as its modal outcome. The analysis above used top-down compound shock frequency anchored to historical base rates. Both methods converge on the same non-obvious middle scenario: the economy stalls without formally contracting.

Growth recession was not the expected answer from either framework. Technical recession or soft landing would have been more narratively salient. That two independent methodologies -- one top-down, one bottom-up -- converge on the same outcome increases confidence that the 62% estimate is not an artifact of any single analytical approach.

The simulation also surfaced a qualitative finding worth noting: growth recession is politically harder to address than technical recession because it lacks the clear signals that trigger emergency policy responses. No NBER declaration, no crisis mechanisms. The economy deteriorates while policymakers wait for thresholds that may not be crossed.

**Evidence quality:** Medium. The simulation is a cross-check methodology, not a primary data source. Its value is convergence confirmation, not independent evidence.

**Invalidation criteria:** If the economy either clearly enters technical recession (two negative GDP quarters) or clearly avoids growth recession (GDP above 1.5% sustained), the convergence finding becomes moot -- the methodologies would have agreed on the wrong scenario.
