# Research Brief — Signal Dispatch Issue #7
**Topic:** KXTARIFFREFUND — Will tariff refunds happen after SCOTUS ruled IEEPA tariffs unconstitutional?
**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Market:** Kalshi KXTARIFFREFUND at 66c | Polymarket ~28–36% | Prior estimate: 42%
**Revised estimate: 30–35%**

---

## Executive Assessment

The legal mechanism for IEEPA tariff refunds is real and more complete than most commentary suggests. *Learning Resources Inc v. Trump* (Feb 20, 2026) struck IEEPA tariff authority on statutory grounds — 7-2, on the merits, with universal scope. A CIT reliquidation order followed on March 4. This is what separates the current situation from the Bush steel precedent, where a WTO win without a domestic court order produced no refunds. The mechanism exists.

The mechanism's existence does not mean refunds will happen before June 30.

Three constraints dominate. First, CBP's CAPE refund processing system is incomplete — approximately 70% of the portal and 40% of the reliquidation system as of March 11 — and only 21,423 of 330,566 affected importers (6.5%) have completed portal enrollment. The other 93.5% would be rejected by the system even if it launches on schedule around April 20. Second, the administration has signaled explicit intent to delay: Trump publicly described the situation as litigation "for the next two years," and while Bessent's "no payments until CIT formally orders" condition is now technically met, CBP continues to cite CAPE incompleteness as grounds for stay. Third, the eligible refund pool has an unknown denominator — importers have a 180-day protest window, and no data exists on how many have filed. Rights are being extinguished in real time.

The Kalshi/Polymarket price divergence (66c vs. 28–36%) is primarily a measurement mismatch, not a disagreement about facts. Kalshi may resolve on whether a court *ordered* refunds — which happened March 4. Polymarket resolves on whether importers *actually receive money*. These are different questions. The relevant market for our probability estimate is Polymarket's framing: actual payment to actual importers.

**The prior estimate of 42% is too high.** The defensible range is 30–35%, with the March 19 CIT compliance hearing as the dominant near-term swing event. If CIT imposes a hard deadline with contempt exposure, the bull case activates. If it does not, the bear case — operational slow-walk within the 2-year statutory window — is the path of least resistance.

## Evidence Base

### The SCOTUS Ruling

*Learning Resources Inc v. Trump* was decided February 20, 2026 on a 7-2 vote. The ruling is statutory, not constitutional: IEEPA's "regulate importation" language does not authorize tariffs. This is a narrower holding than a nondelegation ruling would have been — it leaves Section 232 and Section 301 authority intact — but it is not merely procedural. The ruling covers all IEEPA-based tariff orders universally; it is not limited to the specific challenged orders.

Kavanaugh (joined by Thomas) wrote a 63-page dissent arguing the majority read "regulate" too narrowly and warning of remedy chaos. Barrett commented during oral argument (November 5, 2025) that the remedy situation would be "a mess" — she voted with the majority anyway. The majority opinion said nothing about remedy, making a deliberate choice to leave the $160–175B cleanup to CBP and lower courts.

Thomas filed separately arguing tariff delegation is permissible under the foreign commerce power. He is alone. Current Court support for that position: 1-of-9.

### The Enforcement Chain

The Federal Circuit issued its mandate on March 2 (docket 25-1812). CIT issued a reliquidation order in *Atmus v. United States* on March 4, directing CBP to reliquidate entries without IEEPA duties. On March 6, CBP filed an affidavit acknowledging it cannot yet comply and obtained a stay while building the CAPE system, with a target readiness of approximately April 20. CIT accepted the stay on the condition CBP file progress reports, with the next report due March 19.

As of the March 11 Orrick progress report, the CAPE portal is approximately 70% complete and the reliquidation system approximately 40% complete.

### The CAPE Bottleneck

This is the binding operational constraint. Of 330,566 affected importers:

- 327,143 (~99%) have ACH enrollment — bank routing information on file with CBP. This is *not* CAPE portal enrollment.
- 21,423 (6.5%) have completed CAPE portal setup — the separate registration required to receive refund payments through the new system.
- 309,143 (93.5%) have not completed CAPE portal setup and would be rejected even if CAPE launches April 20.

The prior synthesis briefly cited the 99% ACH enrollment figure as evidence that the technical barrier was lower than the political narrative suggested. That was wrong. ACH enrollment and CAPE portal enrollment are distinct systems. High ACH enrollment does not translate to payment eligibility.

The 6.5% figure is a floor, not a ceiling — enrollment is presumably ongoing. The critical unknown is: who are the 21,423? If they are disproportionately large importers with legal counsel (likely), they may represent a substantially larger share of dollar value than their 6.5% headcount suggests.

### The Political Signals

Trump publicly stated the refund situation would be "litigated for the next two years." Treasury Secretary Bessent stated no payments would be made until CIT formally ordered them — CIT has now ordered them, but CBP continues to cite CAPE incompleteness as grounds for non-compliance. Whether this is deliberate obstruction or genuine operational delay is analytically indistinguishable from the outside; the political statements tilt toward intentional.

One important caveat: Bessent's statement predates the March 4 CIT order. It may have been superseded by events. Trump's statements are also notoriously poor predictors of administrative behavior — agencies sometimes comply with court orders that Trump's rhetoric signals otherwise.

### Historical Precedent

Nixon's TWEA 10% import surcharge (*Yoshida International*, 1974) produced refunds after a domestic court ruling. The Harbor Maintenance Tax (*Shoe Corp.*) produced refunds with interest. The common variable: a domestic court reliquidation order. The IEEPA situation now has one.

The precedent establishes that the mechanism can produce refunds. It does not establish:
- Refunds on a 73-day timeline
- Refunds under an adversarial executive
- Refunds when the processing infrastructure doesn't exist yet

Nixon's TWEA refunds occurred over years under a cooperative administration. The analogy supports the mechanism; it does not support the timeline.

### Section 301 Replacement

USTR launched new Section 301 investigations immediately after the IEEPA ruling. Section 301 authority was explicitly preserved by the SCOTUS ruling. This signals administrative intent to maintain tariff-equivalent pressure through IEEPA-immune authority. It does not moot the refund obligation for duties already collected.

### The Section 122 Wildcard

A 24-state coalition led by Oregon filed suit on March 5 challenging Section 122 replacement tariffs on separate statutory grounds. The Liberty Justice Center filed a parallel Section 122 challenge on March 9. If Section 122 falls, refund exposure expands substantially beyond the current $160–175B IEEPA figure. The legal theory is distinct from the IEEPA challenge and the litigation trajectory is separate — this track was largely absent from initial analysis and remains underresearched.

### The 180-Day Protest Clock

Importers must file customs protests within 180 days of tariff payment to preserve their refund claims. The clock runs concurrent with the CAPE system build. No data exists on current protest filing rates. Sophisticated importers with counsel almost certainly filed early; small and medium importers may not have. Rights are being extinguished in real time for importers who don't file.

## Probability Assessment

**Overall estimate: 30–35% that some importers actually receive refund payments before June 30, 2026.**

*Revised down from prior estimate of 42%. The ACH/CAPE enrollment conflation in the prior research overstated the technical readiness of the refund channel. The bull case requires a conjunction of favorable events that deserves lower conjunctive probability than previously assigned.*

---

### Bear Case — No meaningful refunds before June 30
**Probability: 45–50%**

The path of least resistance. CIT does not impose a hard compliance deadline at the March 19 hearing. CBP continues to cite CAPE incompleteness. CAPE either launches late or launches April 20 but processes only a trivial volume before June 30. The administration operates within the 2-year statutory window for protest resolution. The 6.5% enrolled importers may receive acknowledgment of pending claims but no actual payment. Trump's "two years" framing is the operative administrative posture.

**Bear case does not require:** Rule reversal, SCOTUS stay, congressional action, or anything dramatic. It just requires the operational and political friction to play out as currently structured.

**Resolution criteria:** CAPE launches after June 30, or launches before June 30 but processes fewer than a threshold number of actual payments. No hard deadline imposed at March 19.

**Key trigger:** March 19 CIT hearing produces no hard deadline, no contempt threat.

---

### Base Case — Partial, definitional, or platform-split refunds
**Probability: 30–35%**

CAPE launches approximately April 20. The 21,423 CAPE-enrolled importers begin receiving refunds in May–June, with a portion completing before June 30. This likely resolves Kalshi YES (court ordered refunds — already happened March 4) and resolves Polymarket NO or ambiguously (importers "actually receive" money, but whether 21,423 of 330,566 counts as meaningful resolution is platform-dependent). The legal fight over the stay's scope and what constitutes "compliance" continues into Q3. The bulk of refund exposure remains unresolved.

**Resolution criteria:** Partial payments to enrolled importers confirmed before June 30. Platform-dependent whether Kalshi or Polymarket resolution follows.

**Key trigger:** CAPE on-schedule April 20, no mass enrollment drive, slow processing pace.

---

### Bull Case — Substantial refunds before June 30
**Probability: 15–20%**
*(Downgraded from 20–25%; conjunction underweighted previously)*

CIT imposes a hard compliance deadline with contempt exposure at or after the March 19 hearing. CBP expedites CAPE. A mass enrollment drive brings the 309,143 unenrolled importers into the system at sufficient scale before June 30. Political calculation shifts — refund payments become a constituency tool for affected industries. Courts reject further stay applications.

**Why the conjunction matters:** The bull case requires BOTH legal pressure (hard CIT deadline) AND rapid mass onboarding of 300K+ unenrolled importers. These are not independent — they require CIT action AND CBP responsiveness AND importer action within ~10 weeks. Conjunctive probability is lower than either alone.

**Resolution criteria:** CIT imposes hard deadline at March 19. CAPE launches by April 20. Mass portal enrollment drive produces substantially higher completion rates by late May. Payments confirmed before June 30.

## Adversarial Notes

*Challenges worth weighing before publication, sourced from adversarial review.*

**1. The "universal scope" claim is the synthesis's interpretation, not a confirmed holding.** Courts routinely limit relief to parties before them. Has anyone confirmed the ruling explicitly covers tariff orders the plaintiffs never challenged? The Atmus reliquidation order suggests CIT read the ruling broadly, but the administration could challenge scope and seek SCOTUS emergency stay. Two justices think the ruling was irresponsible — what's the probability of a SCOTUS stay being granted on the remedy? The synthesis implicitly assumes zero. That assumption is load-bearing.

**2. The 6.5% CAPE enrollment figure is being applied without knowing who those 21,423 importers are.** If they are the large importers — the Walmarts, the industrial manufacturers — they may represent 60–70% of the dollar value at stake despite being 6.5% of headcount. How Polymarket resolves "importers receive money" — by count or by meaningful participation — determines whether this matters. The highest-priority analytical gap is not just the contract language; it's the composition of the enrolled cohort.

**3. The eligible pool has an undefined denominator.** Importers must file protests within 180 days. No data exists on filing rates. Scenario A (sophisticated importers filed Day 1; actionable pool is concentrated in the enrolled cohort, bull case strengthened) and Scenario B (most importers haven't filed; eligible pool is quietly being extinguished, bear case strengthened for a different reason than slow-walking) are both consistent with "protest filing rate unavailable." The probability range cannot be calculated with a known denominator. The 30–35% range is directionally correct but built on an unknown foundation.

**4. Bessent's condition may be outdated.** His "no payments until CIT formally orders" statement was made before the March 4 CIT order existed. If that condition is now technically satisfied, CBP's continued obstruction is operating on a different and possibly weaker legal basis than Treasury's stated position. This is a medium-impact clarification that could shift the interpretation of the current stay.

**5. Historical precedent is from a cooperative political era.** Nixon complied with Yoshida. The Harbor Maintenance Tax refunds occurred under cooperative executive administration. These precedents establish that the mechanism can produce refunds; they say nothing about the speed or likelihood of refunds when the executive branch treats court orders as negotiating positions. 2026 ≠ 1974.

## What the Market Is Missing

**Kalshi at 66c reflects a near-certain event that may have already happened.** If KXTARIFFREFUND resolves on whether a court *ordered* refunds, the March 4 CIT reliquidation order may already satisfy the resolution criterion. Traders buying at 66c may be pricing "will court orders exist" rather than "will importers receive money." The contract language is the highest-priority unresolved gap — confirm it before treating 66c as pricing actual payment probability.

**The ACH enrollment figure (99%) has been misread in most coverage.** Near-universal ACH enrollment means CBP has bank routing information on file. It does not mean 99% of importers can receive CAPE refunds. These are different systems. Coverage that cites the 99% figure as evidence the refund channel is clear is wrong.

**The protest filing window is a quiet extinguishment mechanism that almost no coverage mentions.** Importers who fail to file protests within 180 days permanently forfeit their refund claims. If the eligible pool is being extinguished in real time, the eventual refund scale may be substantially smaller than the $160–175B headline figure — not because courts prevented refunds, but because importers didn't know they needed to file. This is the bear case that doesn't require administration bad faith; it just requires information asymmetry.

**The Section 122 litigation track is a separate and underweighted compounding risk.** The 24-state coalition challenged Section 301 replacement tariffs — if that challenge succeeds, the replacement tariff structure also falls, compounding refund exposure beyond IEEPA. Virtually no coverage connects the IEEPA outcome to the Section 122 litigation. The administration's post-ruling pivot to Section 301 assumes that authority is durable. It may not be.

**The Kalshi/Polymarket divergence is structural, not informational.** The two markets are not pricing the same question. Treating their prices as comparable signals about the same underlying probability — as most commentary does — is a category error. The divergence is measurement mismatch. The relevant figure for our estimate is Polymarket's framing (importers actually receive money), and Polymarket at 28–36% suggests informed market consensus is below our 30–35% range.
