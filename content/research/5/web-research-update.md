## What Changed Since March 16

**The ground shifted significantly in the last 24 hours. Three developments materially alter the recession calculus:**

### 1. Strait of Hormuz Escalation -- Energy Shock Is Now Real, Not Theoretical

The existing research treated the Iran/Hormuz disruption as a scenario risk. It is now an active crisis. Key developments:

- **Iran's IRGC declared "not a litre of oil" will pass through the Strait.** Hundreds of tankers sit idle on both sides. The IEA has called this "the largest disruption to global energy supplies in history."
- **Brent crude crossed $100/bbl** on Friday March 14 and is now trading around $106. This is a 40%+ increase since the war began on February 28. The pre-conflict baseline was ~$70.
- **Iran's military warned of $200/bbl.** Analysts are not dismissing this -- CNBC reports traders "wouldn't be surprised" at that level if the crisis drags on.
- **The SPR release failed to contain prices.** IEA member states announced a 400 million barrel coordinated release (US share: 172M barrels over 4 months). Oil prices rose 5% *on the day of the announcement.* The release covers ~4 days of global consumption. The market treated it as confirmation of severity, not relief.
- **EIA forecast:** Brent stays above $95/bbl through May, potentially dropping below $80 in Q3 *only if* the conflict de-escalates. If Hormuz stays closed, the floor is $100+.

**Impact on recession calculus:** The existing synthesis estimated +15pp recession probability if oil broke $120. Oil is at $106 and rising. The $120 trigger is no longer a tail risk -- it is within the distribution. Even at current $100 levels, the rule of thumb is each $10/bbl adds 15-20 cents/gallon at the pump. Gasoline is the most visible inflation transmission channel -- it hits consumer sentiment before it hits CPI.

### 2. FOMC Meeting -- Tomorrow (March 18-19)

This is the most consequential FOMC meeting since the Iran war began. The existing research had CME FedWatch at 95% hold probability for April. Now:

- **CME FedWatch shows 92%+ probability of hold** at 3.50-3.75%.
- **This is a dot-plot meeting.** The Summary of Economic Projections will reveal whether the committee still projects any cuts in 2026. The existing research noted the median was one 25bp cut. If the new dot plot shows *zero* cuts, that is a +5pp recession trigger per the existing synthesis.
- **Goldman Sachs projects first cut no earlier than September.** Some economists see no cuts at all in 2026.
- **The "data vacuum" problem:** A partial government shutdown since February 14 (DHS funding standoff) has delayed key data releases including February PPI. The Fed is making this decision with incomplete information. The Conference Board LEI release (scheduled March 19) may also be delayed.
- **Powell's press conference** will be the first where he must address the oil shock, tariff recalibration post-Supreme Court, and the -92K payroll print simultaneously. Any hint of "higher for longer" will confirm the stagflation trap thesis.

### 3. Supreme Court Tariff Ruling -- The Trade Landscape Changed

On February 20, SCOTUS struck down Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs 6-3. This is a *structural* change the existing research does not incorporate:

- **What was struck down:** "Trafficking tariffs" on China/Canada/Mexico and "reciprocal" tariffs (10%+ on most countries).
- **Trump's immediate response:** Invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 to impose 15% across-the-board tariffs for 150 days (through July 24, 2026). Section 232 tariffs remain in place.
- **Net effect on effective tariff rate:** The US average effective tariff rate is now ~17%, the highest since the early 1930s. This is *higher* than the 11.7% cited in the existing research, because Trump escalated in response to the ruling.
- **New Section 301 investigation:** On March 12, Trump launched Section 301 probes into China, Vietnam, Taiwan, Mexico, Japan, and the EU -- laying groundwork for tariffs that would survive judicial review.
- **Trump-Xi summit March 31-April 2:** Limited expectations for breakthroughs, but the meeting creates a window for de-escalation that did not exist before.

## Gap Fill: Energy Shock Transmission

The energy shock is no longer a gap -- it is the dominant new variable. Here is the transmission chain:

**Oil at $100/bbl (current) transmission:**
- Gasoline: +$0.45-0.60/gallon above pre-conflict levels (rule of thumb: $10/bbl = 15-20 cents/gallon)
- CPI impact: +0.5-1.0pp on headline CPI within 2-3 months (existing research estimated this range)
- Consumer impact: At current levels, roughly equivalent to a $500-700/year per-household energy tax on top of the $1,300/year tariff burden (Tax Foundation estimate)
- Total household cost burden from tariffs + energy: approximately **$1,800-2,000/year** -- hitting simultaneously

**Oil at $120/bbl (if Hormuz stays closed through Q2):**
- Gasoline approaches $5/gallon nationally
- CPI acceleration above 4% headline
- Recession probability mechanically increases to 55%+ per existing synthesis trigger table
- Fed is locked in: cannot cut with headline inflation at 4%+
- Consumer confidence likely drops below 50 (would be lowest since pandemic)

**The compound effect:** The existing synthesis correctly identified that the economy can absorb tariffs OR an energy shock, but not both. Both are now active. The tariff effective rate is 17% (up from 11.7% in the synthesis). Oil is $106 (up from unspecified but implied ~$70-80 baseline). These are additive supply-side shocks arriving simultaneously while the Fed cannot respond.

**Critical data point from UMich:** The March preliminary sentiment (55.5) showed that interviews conducted *before* the Iran war showed improving sentiment. Interviews *after* "completely erased those initial gains." The energy shock is already hitting confidence before it hits CPI.

## Gap Fill: Leading Indicators

### Conference Board LEI
- **December 2025:** Declined 0.2% to 97.6 (2016=100). Fifth consecutive monthly decline.
- **January-February 2026:** Not yet released. Next release scheduled March 19 but may be delayed due to government shutdown.
- **Sub-component drivers (December):** Consumer expectations and ISM New Orders were the largest negative contributors. Labor market (rising claims, declining manufacturing hours) also weighed.
- **Signal:** Five consecutive monthly declines historically precede recession within 6-12 months. The LEI is flashing recession; the question is whether the ISM recovery contradicts it.

### ISM PMI
- **Manufacturing PMI February 2026: 52.4** (down slightly from 52.6 in January). Second consecutive month of expansion after breaking a 26-month contraction streak. This remains the single strongest bullish data point.
- **Services PMI February 2026: 54.0** (up from 53.8). 20th consecutive month of expansion. Services sector remains resilient.
- **Key tension:** Manufacturing PMI is expanding, but "hampered by higher input costs and tariff-related uncertainties." The expansion may be fragile -- it was achieved pre-oil-shock. March data (released April 1) will be the first to capture Hormuz disruption effects on input costs.

### Yield Curve
- **10Y-3M spread:** Oscillating between positive and negative since late February. Was negative from October 2022 to December 2024. The instability itself is a signal -- the curve cannot decide whether to invert.
- **10Y yield (March 13): 4.28%.** 2Y yield: 3.73%. 30Y: 4.90%.
- **NY Fed model: 25% recession probability** (12 months forward), up from 18.8% in the existing research.
- **Interpretation:** The yield curve has normalized from deep inversion, which historically signals the recession risk is *imminent* -- not dissipating. Every recession since 1970 occurred after the curve un-inverted, not during inversion. The lag from inversion to recession is typically 12-18 months. The curve was inverted October 2022 - December 2024. We are now 15 months post-first-inversion, 3 months post-normalization. Right in the historical window.

### Why Markets Are Below Institutional Estimates

The 15-point spread between Moody's ML model (49%) and Kalshi (34%) demands explanation. Several hypotheses:

1. **Definition mismatch.** Kalshi defines recession as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP. The institutional estimates often use NBER dating, which is broader. A "growth recession" (GDP 0-1%) would trigger the institutional estimates but not the Kalshi contract. This is likely the largest source of divergence -- the existing synthesis puts "growth recession" at 55% probability, but that does not pay off on Kalshi.

2. **Market composition.** Prediction markets skew toward active traders who tend to be more optimistic about economic outcomes. Institutional estimates are produced by economists who weight downside scenarios more heavily.

3. **Oil price lag.** The Kalshi spike from <25% to 34% was triggered by oil crossing $100 on March 9. But the full Hormuz implications (duration, supply destruction, second-order effects) may not yet be priced in. The market is reactive, not forward-looking on geopolitical supply shocks.

4. **Historical model limitations.** The NY Fed model at 25% relies on the yield curve, which has normalized. But this normalization occurred in an unprecedented context (post-pandemic rate cycle, tariff regime, active military conflict affecting energy supply). The model may be structurally underweighting non-cyclical shocks.

**Information content:** Prediction markets are useful for detecting when *consensus* shifts, not for calibrating tail risk. The jump from <25% to 34% is the signal -- the direction, not the level.

## Contrarian Case Update

The strongest bull case against recession has both strengthened and weakened since March 16:

**Strengthened:**
- **ISM manufacturing sustained expansion.** February's 52.4 is the second consecutive month above 50. Services at 54.0 is robust. Hard production data is not contracting.
- **Tax cuts passed over summer 2025** are starting to flow through -- corporate tax cuts and consumer spending power.
- **AI infrastructure spending** is driving extraordinary capex. Data center buildout is a real economic engine that is partially insulated from trade/energy shocks (domestic construction, long-term contracts).
- **Trump-Xi summit (March 31-April 2)** creates a concrete de-escalation window on tariffs.
- **Supreme Court ruling** removed the worst tariffs (IEEPA-based). The replacement Section 122 tariffs are capped at 15% for 150 days and expire July 24. This creates a *defined timeline* for tariff relief that did not exist before.
- **Wealthy consumer resilience.** Upper-income households, buoyed by investment gains and housing wealth, account for more than half of consumer spending. Baby boomer retirement spending is a structural tailwind.

**Weakened:**
- **Oil shock overwhelms fiscal stimulus.** The $500-700/household energy burden largely offsets or exceeds tax cut benefits for middle-income households.
- **ISM expansion is pre-oil-shock.** February data does not capture $100+ oil hitting input costs. March PMI (April 1 release) will be the real test.
- **Government shutdown creates data vacuum and direct GDP drag.** Now in its fifth week. If it extends into April, Goldman estimates it could subtract 0.1-0.2pp from Q2 GDP -- on top of already-weak growth.
- **Zandi's formulation captures it:** "Nothing else can go wrong. Like, nothing. We're pretty much on the edge." Since March 16, something went wrong (Hormuz escalation, shutdown continuing, data delays).

**Net assessment on the contrarian case:** The bull case requires ISM expansion to survive the oil shock, the Hormuz crisis to resolve by Q2, and the Trump-Xi summit to produce tariff de-escalation. Each is individually plausible. All three happening is a lower-probability scenario than even one failing.
