## Signal Convergence

**1. Powell will avoid the R-word — near-universal finding across all research vectors.**
Every historical lens points the same direction. Worker 4 (rhetoric analysis) found five distinct evasion patterns Powell deploys when backed into a corner. Worker 7 (case studies) identified the March 2020 analog — the most damning precedent: Powell held a historic emergency meeting, slashed rates to zero, announced QE, and still did not use the word "recession" in the press conference. The language followed six weeks later. Worker 1 found that even when Powell used the word in June 2022 ("a possibility"), it was in a congressional hearing where deflection was harder, not a presser where he controls the frame.

**2. The macro backdrop is objectively bad — no worker disputed the data severity.**
Workers 2, 3, and 5 all confirmed: -92,000 February NFP, Q4 2025 GDP at 0.7% (second estimate), unemployment at or above 4.4%. Goldman Sachs has raised recession probability to 25%. Multiple outlets called it the worst jobs report "in years." The data is not ambiguous. The question is what Powell *says* about it, not what the data says.

**3. Iran war + oil at ~$100 gives Powell an external attribution buffer.**
Workers 4 and 5 independently identified the geopolitical attribution pattern. When external shocks are present (Iran war, energy price spike), the Fed chair can frame deterioration as "transitory external pressure" rather than structural failure — a rhetorical escape hatch. Worker 4: "Tariffs + weak jobs = singular event, not structural failure." This framing makes recession language *less* likely, not more, because it provides an alternative causal narrative.

**4. This is Powell's penultimate meeting. Stability messaging is the dominant incentive.**
Workers 4, 5, and 6 all converged on this. Fortune (March 16): "Don't expect many hints." Pepperstone: "Powell On Pause In Penultimate Meeting." A chair in their final weeks does not blow up markets with inflammatory language. The institutional incentive structure is strongly anti-R-word.

**5. Rate hold is consensus. No cut, no hike.**
Workers 5 and 6 agree: the FOMC holds. Stagflation dynamics (oil shock + cooling labor) make cutting dangerous. The dot plot is the market focus, not the rate decision itself. This removes one pressure vector — Powell isn't justifying a dramatic policy move that might require recession framing.

**2. Resolution criteria: verbatim "recession" vs. derivatives — critical and unverified.**
Worker 0 got HTTP 429 from Kalshi (rate-limited) and could not confirm the 28¢ price or pull the contract's exact resolution language. The key unknown: does "recessionary" trigger resolution? Does "recessionary pressures" count? This is contract-specific and not publicly searchable. If the contract requires verbatim "recession" only, the probability drops. If derivatives count, it rises — reporters might drag "recessionary" out of Powell under questioning even when he avoids the noun.

**3. Market "recession odds" signals conflict — but they're measuring different things.**
Worker 6 found Kalshi News headlines saying recession odds "surged" AND "fell to all-time low." This isn't contradictory data — it's two different Kalshi markets: one on 2026 recession occurrence (Polymarket puts this at an elevated level), another on the March 18 *mention* specifically (28¢). The market-on-mention is the relevant instrument. The divergence suggests that even traders who believe a recession is coming don't expect Powell to *say so* tomorrow.

**4. The 27-point gap: market at 28¢, our pre-synthesis estimate at 55%.**
Post-synthesis, our estimate is revised down to ~38%. The initial 55% overweighted data severity and underweighted the rhetorical base rate. Even at 38%, there is a 10-point gap above the market price. This is either a genuine edge or it reflects liquidity/information asymmetry on a low-volume contract. Worker 0 noted ~$2.8M in comparable conferences — enough volume to take seriously, not enough to trust blindly.

## Preliminary Assessment

### Base Case: NO mention — 62%
Powell holds the line. Iran war provides external attribution. Penultimate meeting incentivizes stability. He frames -92K as "significant softening" or "considerable headwinds" without crossing into recession framing. The March 2020 playbook repeats: the language defers 4-8 weeks while the data is acknowledged. Reporters press; he deploys the epistemic disclaimer ("no one can predict") and geopolitical attribution ("extraordinary external circumstances"). Resolution: NO.

### Bull Case (for YES): 28%
A reporter asks the direct question under impossible-to-deflect conditions. The -92K NFP is the hook — "Chair Powell, with the worst jobs report in years, are we heading into a recession?" Under direct questioning with sub-1% GDP and negative payrolls confirmed, Powell's reflex is to deny ("I do not think the US is currently in a recession") — and that denial itself *uses the word*. The 2022 pattern: Powell said "recession" in congressional testimony by defending against the premise. The same mechanism could trigger at a presser.

Probability hinges on: (a) whether a reporter frames the question in a way that forces the word into the denial, and (b) whether the contract's resolution criteria count defensive usage ("we are not in a recession").

### Bear Case (deep NO): 10%
Conditions are bad but not 2020-bad. The Iran external shock framing holds completely. Powell pivots to dot plot discussion and inflation dynamics. Reporters don't land the direct recession question cleanly. Market is right at 28¢. No edge.
