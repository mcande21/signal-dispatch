# F8: Greenlink West and Greenlink Nevada -- Deep Investigation

**Research date:** 2026-05-15
**Sources:** 18 primary sources including BLM NEPA filings, PUCN rate case orders, NV Energy press releases, WECC progress reports, and investigative journalism from Nevada Independent, Nevada Current, Las Vegas Review-Journal, NNBW, Utility Dive, and regulatory filings.

---

### 1. Project Scope and Route

**The Greenlink System:**
Greenlink Nevada is a three-sided high-voltage transmission triangle across the state, built on the existing One Nevada Transmission Line (ONL, operational since 2014, connecting Las Vegas to Ely). Two new segments complete the triangle:

- **Greenlink West:** ~472 miles (originally described as 351 miles in the 2020 announcement; the 472-mile figure includes all ancillary segments and is the BLM NEPA figure), 525 kV series-compensated AC line from Northwest Substation in northwest Las Vegas to Walker River Substation near Yerington.
- **Greenlink North:** ~235 miles, 525 kV line from Robinson Summit Substation near Ely to Walker River Substation near Yerington, along the Highway 50 corridor.

**Greenlink West Route Detail:**
- **Southern terminus:** Northwest Substation / Harry Allen Substation area, northwest Las Vegas (near the Apex Generating Station, a natural gas plant northeast of Las Vegas)
- Runs north along the Highway 95 corridor from Las Vegas through Indian Springs, Amargosa Valley, past Beatty (Sagebrush Substation), north through Tonopah area (Esmeralda Substation), to Yerington
- **Northern hub:** Walker River Substation (f.k.a. Fort Churchill Substation expansion), near Yerington in Lyon County -- a ~360-acre site, described as among the largest substations in the world when completed
- From Walker River, three 345 kV lines extend to the Reno area: two into the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center (TRIC) and one to Mira Loma Substation
- **Northernmost node:** Comstock Meadows Substation at the south end of TRIC, behind Google's data center complex, currently being expanded for Greenlink interconnection

**Voltage and Technology:**
- Primary backbone: 525 kV AC, series compensated (not HVDC)
- Walker River substation handles four voltage levels: 525 kV, 345 kV, 230 kV, and 120 kV
- Two 525/230 kV collector substations along the line (Sagebrush near Beatty, Esmeralda near Tonopah) for renewable energy interconnection
- The 120 kV portion of Walker River substation was energized October 2025

**Capacity:**
- Greenlink West will raise Northern Nevada's import capacity from the current 1,275 MW ceiling (fully allocated) to ~2,000 MW
- Greenlink North will further boost the limit to ~2,800 MW
- Combined Greenlink North + West: up to 5,000 MW of renewable energy zone access
- NV Energy has committed up to 4,000 MW to data centers (primarily in the Reno area)
- The line stays within NV Energy's balancing authority; it does not directly connect to CAISO. However, NV Energy already has interconnections to neighbors and will join EDAM (see Section 5)

**Counties/Terrain:**
Clark, Nye, Esmeralda, Mineral, and Lyon counties. Terrain ranges from flat desert (southern segment, dust mitigation is primary challenge) to hilly and rocky terrain in the north. The route passes near active military training grounds for Nellis Air Force Base and Naval Air Station Fallon, requiring FAA and DOD coordination.

[Sources: BLM POD Rev1 (2021); BLM Final EIS; WECC 2026 Annual Progress Report; NNBW 2026-04-08; NV Energy OATIS posting (2022)]

### 2. Construction Status (as of May 2026)

**Current state:** Under active construction in southern Nevada; on track for mid-2027 completion.

**Key milestones achieved:**
- BLM Record of Decision issued September 6, 2024 (also constitutes DOE/NNSA approval for crossing Nevada National Security Site)
- NEPA process complete (DOI-BLM-NV-0000-2022-0004-EIS, status: Completed as of 2/2/2026)
- PUCN construction permit approved (exact date in 2024/2025 rate case proceedings)
- Walker River substation 120 kV portion energized October 2025
- Two 343,000-pound transformers delivered to Walker River substation in October 2025
- ~800,000 work hours completed by MasTec as of April 2026
- ~150 transmission line structures erected, moving north along Highway 95 from Las Vegas to Beatty
- Four substations under construction: Walker River (main hub), Sagebrush (near Beatty), Esmeralda (near Tonopah), and Comstock Meadows expansion

**Timeline:**
- Line stringing (by helicopter) scheduled to begin summer 2026
- **Projected in-service: after summer 2027** (the April 2026 NNBW article says "after the summer of 2027," which is a slight slip from the original "May 2027" target -- this is a soft delay of a few months)
- NV Energy senior director Shazad Lateef: "Right now, Northern Nevada has no transmission import capacity for new load growth. Greenlink West alone doesn't give us the same reliability benefits once Greenlink North is in place, but it will give us additional capacity into Northern Nevada and transmission won't be a bottleneck for growth."

**Contractor:**
- **General contractor: T&D Power, Inc.** (a subsidiary of **MasTec, Inc.**, Coral Gables, FL)
- 100% union job (IBEW 1245 secured the worksite agreement; workers drawn from IBEW Locals 396, 1245, 47)
- Equipment sourced globally (conductors from Bahrain; transformers from multiple countries; some lead times exceed 3.5 years)

**Environmental/Legal Challenges:**
- **Friends of Nevada Wilderness + Basin and Range Watch** filed lawsuit against BLM in May 2025 alleging NEPA violations in the Greenlink West review process
- **Department of Defense** raised concerns about 24+ transmission towers in restricted military airspace near Nevada Test and Training Range (Nellis AFB); FAA obstruction analysis ongoing; mitigation agreement in negotiation
- Environmental groups argue the line will open pristine desert to industrial-scale solar development
- Construction coordination required with Nellis AFB and Naval Air Station Fallon

**Greenlink North Status (for context):**
- Still in permitting phase; BLM issued Draft EIS in September 2024
- In January 2026, BLM's Nevada State Director ordered supplemental environmental analysis on sage grouse impacts after agreeing with protest letters from environmentalists, sportsmen groups, and Lander County
- Final Record of Decision now expected May 15, 2026 (per BLM project website as of early 2026)
- Construction start: January 2027 target
- In-service target: December 2028
- Governor Lombardo raised concerns about overly restrictive sage grouse protections that could limit construction to 45 days/year per segment

[Sources: NNBW 2026-04-08; BLM ePlanning; NVBEX 2025-09-04; Nevada Current 2026-01-12; LV Review-Journal 2026-01-06; IBEW 1245 2025-07-01; Pizen Switch Times 2025-11-12]

### 3. Cost and Funding

**Total cost:**
- **$4.24 billion** for the combined Greenlink Nevada project (West + North)
- Original 2020 estimate: $2.5 billion; cost doubled by 2024
- NV Energy has not publicly broken out the individual cost of Greenlink West vs. North

**Cost escalation timeline:**
- July 2020: NV Energy announces Greenlink, estimates ~$2.5 billion combined
- March 2021: PUCN partially approves (full approval for West, permitting-only for North); commissioners noted cost magnitude was "extraordinary"
- June 2021: SB448 passes Nevada Legislature, mandating construction of entire project, bypassing PUCN's partial denial; **no cost caps were included in the legislation**
- September 2024: Federal approval; cost now at $4.24 billion
- Former CEO Doug Cannon stated in 2021 that NV Energy would "bring $2.5 billion to the table" and "Nevadans will not be asked to pay for this investment until at least five to six years down the road"

**Funding mechanism:**
- **Rate base:** Primarily ratepayer-funded through NV Energy's general rate case
- PUCN designated Greenlink as a **"critical facility"** -- this allows NV Energy to request funding during construction (CWIP: Construction Work in Progress) rather than waiting until the project is in service
- September 2025: PUCN approved NV Energy's general rate case including Greenlink cost recovery:
  - **50% of costs recorded to date** approved for inclusion in rates
  - **Southern Nevada customers: 70% of total Greenlink cost**
  - **Northern Nevada customers: 30% of total Greenlink cost**

**Ratepayer impact:**
- Southern Nevada: **$4 per month increase** specifically for Greenlink West construction costs (original estimate $4.35-$4.42/month; reduced after PUCN approved only 50% of recorded costs)
- Statewide: **$25+ per month increase** from new demand-based billing structure (also approved in same rate case, September 2025)
- The demand charge shifts billing from total energy consumption to peak 15-minute usage, a structure no other U.S. investor-owned utility has applied to all residential customers
- PUCN order: "The Commission makes this decision with the unfortunate realization that representations made by NV Energy's former chief executive officer... were either misleading or factually inaccurate" (referring to Cannon's statements about ratepayer timing)
- Bureau of Consumer Protection protested the funding method, warning it will "create significant upward pressure on the general rates"

**Federal funding:**
- Greenlink was **NOT selected** for DOE's Transmission Facilitation Program (TFP). The TFP's first-round selections were: Southwest Intertie Project-North (Idaho/Nevada), Cross-Tie 500kV (Nevada/Utah), Southline (Arizona/New Mexico), Twin States (NH/VT). Second-round selections: Aroostook, Southern Spirit, Cimarron Link, Southline. Greenlink is absent.
- No evidence of IIJA grants or DOE loan guarantees for Greenlink specifically
- DOE involvement limited to NEPA co-lead status (due to NNSA land crossing)
- The project is essentially a privately-funded, ratepayer-backed NV Energy capital investment under Berkshire Hathaway Energy

[Sources: Nevada Independent (cost doubling investigation); Nevada Independent (rate case approval); PUCN rate case order Sept 2025; Nevada Current 2026-01-12; DOE TFP selections page; NV Energy press releases]

### 4. Capacity Allocation

**The 4,000 MW / 5,000 MW figures:**
- **5,000 MW:** The combined Greenlink North + West system will provide access to up to 5,000 MW of renewable energy zones along the transmission corridor. This is the generation interconnection capacity, not transfer capacity.
- **4,000 MW:** NV Energy has "promised Switch, Novva, and others up to 4,000 MW for a complex of massive AI data centers in the greater Reno area" (Nevada Current, Jan 2026, citing LV Review-Journal reporting from Sept 2025)
- **Total data center demand requests: 21,000-22,000 MW** -- Jeff Brigger (NV Energy director of business development) stated at a September 2025 NAIOP panel that companies want to add this to NV Energy's ~9,000 MW peak. NV Energy has agreed to serve 3,000-4,000 MW so far.
- NV Energy's 2024 IRP shows 12 data center projects in Northern Nevada could drive 5,900 MW of new demand by 2033
- Data centers already accounted for 22% of Nevada electricity consumption in 2024; projected to reach 35% by 2030

**Known data center commitments at TRIC and Northern Nevada:**
- **Switch:** 130 MW facility operational since 2017 at TRIC; expanding; Steven Roberts (VP construction development) spoke at NAIOP panel
- **Google:** Data center complex at south end of TRIC (adjacent to Comstock Meadows Substation); 115 MW enhanced geothermal PPA from Fervo Energy through NV Energy; Google also testified in support of NV Energy's EDAM application
- **Apple:** Land acquired at TRIC; facility planned/under construction
- **Microsoft:** Land acquired at TRIC; facility planned
- **Novva Data Centers:** 60 MW facility completed at TRIC (300,000 sq ft, expected online 2026); also planning 100 MW facility in North Las Vegas; targeting 1 GW total capacity by 2028 across five data centers
- **Tract, EdgeCore, Vantage, PowerHouse, Blockchains:** All acquired land at TRIC and began building data centers
- **Redwood Materials:** 120 kV line out of Comstock Meadows serving this customer (initial 82 MW, ultimate 140 MW by 2027; Phase 1 energized November 2024 at ~3 MW)
- **Mark IV Capital:** Planning 1,800 MW natural gas generating station in Fernley specifically to serve data center demand (plans to transfer plant to NV Energy)

**Nature of commitments:**
- The 3,000-4,000 MW figure appears to be based on interconnection agreements and load service commitments, not mere letters of intent. Brigger said NV Energy "has agreed to dole out" this capacity.
- However, NV Energy emphasized they "cannot impact our existing customer base" -- suggesting prioritization of existing ratepayers in principle, even as data centers consume the new capacity.
- Jeff Brigger: "I don't think we intended to build that [Greenlink] for data center load, but it's certainly beneficial."

**Liberty Utilities capacity:**
- Liberty does NOT have reserved capacity on Greenlink. Liberty's current arrangement is a full-requirements wholesale contract with NV Energy (75% of Liberty's supply), not a transmission service agreement.
- Once Greenlink comes online, Liberty can use NV Energy's transmission system to procure from third-party suppliers (the wires stay; only the supply changes)
- Liberty Utilities President Eric Schwarzrock said the utility will be **"first in the waiting line"** when Greenlink opens -- meaning Liberty will have access to the expanded transmission to reach a broader pool of wholesale suppliers, but must compete in the open market for actual supply
- Liberty has 38 interconnection points with NV Energy and sits entirely within NV Energy's balancing authority (not CAISO)
- Liberty filed an advice letter with CPUC in March 2026 seeking authorization for an expedited RFP process; formal RFP expected summer 2026

[Sources: LV Review-Journal 2025-09-18 (NAIOP panel); Nevada Current 2026-01-12; Energy News Beat 2026-05; WECC 2026 APR; Server Country; Novva press releases]

### 5. EDAM Connection

**EDAM approval:**
- Nevada PUCN approved NV Energy's plan to join CAISO's Extended Day-Ahead Market (EDAM) on **April 3, 2026**
- Target go-live: **Fall 2028**
- EDAM launched May 1, 2026 with PacifiCorp and Portland General Electric as initial participants
- Future EDAM participants: LADWP, BANC, Turlock Irrigation District, Public Service Co. of New Mexico, Idaho Power (leaning toward), and NV Energy (fall 2028)

**Economic case:**
- NV Energy estimates **$93.1 million/year in savings** from EDAM (lower production costs, increased market sales, higher wheeling revenues)
- Brattle Group study found SPP Markets+ would have produced net costs of ~$7.3 million/year -- EDAM was clearly more favorable
- Google testified in support of EDAM, citing access to "low-cost solar from California and significant wind resources from the Pacific Northwest and Idaho" that peak at different times from NV Energy's solar production

**Is Greenlink a prerequisite for EDAM?**
- Not technically a prerequisite -- NV Energy already participates in CAISO's Western Energy Imbalance Market (WEIM) and has existing transmission interconnections with neighboring utilities
- However, Greenlink strengthens NV Energy's position as a **"critical hub connecting the Northwest, Southwest, and Interior West"** (Advanced Energy United) -- the additional transmission capacity makes NV Energy a more valuable EDAM participant and enables greater power transfers
- Utility Dive: "NV Energy also owns key transmission assets connecting it to neighboring states. Further, it is developing the $4.2 billion Greenlink project in Nevada, set to be fully operating by 2029"
- The TransWest Express project (separately) will deliver 3 GW of Wyoming wind into CAISO via Eldorado substation near Las Vegas -- another EDAM-relevant connection

**Implications for Liberty Utilities:**
- EDAM participation would expand the pool of wholesale suppliers accessible through NV Energy's transmission system
- Liberty could potentially procure from EDAM participants (PacifiCorp, PGE, LADWP, etc.) through the day-ahead market optimization
- Google's EDAM testimony highlights the resource diversity: "Aligning with its most direct neighbors through EDAM helps ensure that external transfers are not limited to emergency conditions, but are arranged as part of a daily, optimized process"
- **Critical timing gap:** EDAM goes live for NV Energy in fall 2028, but Liberty's NV Energy supply contract ends May 2027. Liberty must secure replacement supply 18 months before EDAM benefits materialize.
- However, Liberty can still access third-party suppliers via NV Energy's transmission (bilateral contracts) before EDAM -- EDAM just makes the optimization more efficient

[Sources: Utility Dive 2026-04 (PUCN EDAM approval); CAISO EDAM launch announcement; NV Energy EDAM filing; Google PUCN testimony]

### 6. History and Justification Evolution

**Timeline of justification shift:**

**July 2020 -- The Clean Energy Pitch:**
NV Energy announces Greenlink Nevada. CEO Doug Cannon: "Greenlink Nevada is essential to unlocking Nevada's clean energy potential and meeting Nevada's renewable and de-carbonization goals by providing access to new in-state solar and geothermal resources." The project is framed entirely around:
- 50% RPS mandate
- Carbon reduction objectives
- COVID-19 economic recovery (jobs, tax revenue)
- Rural economic development
No mention of data centers.

**July 2021 -- Plan of Development to BLM:**
NV Energy states: "Development of the Greenlink West Transmission Project facilitates access to renewable energy zones and is necessary to accommodate decommissioning of conventional fossil fuel generation resources."
Still pure clean energy framing.

**March 2021 -- PUCN partial approval / SB448 legislative override:**
PUCN approved Greenlink West but balked at Greenlink North's cost. Two months later, SB448 mandated the full project through the legislature, bypassing the regulatory process. Former Sen. Chris Brooks (D-Las Vegas) championed the bill, introduced on the 102nd day of session (14 days before sine die).
PUCN engineer Adam Danise testified: NV Energy "proceeded to go to the Nevada Legislature... to request that it mandate construction of the Greenlink Nevada Project not only for the portions of the project the Commission rejected, but for the entire Greenlink Nevada Project, which NV Energy had not even presented to the Commission for approval."

**2024-2025 -- The Data Center Pivot:**
By late 2024/2025, the justification has shifted dramatically:
- NV Energy's 2024 IRP shows 75% of major-project load growth attributed to data centers
- 12 data center projects driving 5,900 MW of new demand
- NV Energy business development director Jeff Brigger (Sept 2025): "I don't think we intended to build that for data center load, but it's certainly beneficial."
- NV Energy spokesperson says Liberty's departure was "a planned transition for many years, not a reaction to recent developments"

**January 2026 -- The Full Picture Emerges:**
Nevada Current investigation reveals the northern terminus of Greenlink West is at Comstock Meadows Substation, "tucked in behind Google's data center complex at the south end of Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center."
Mark IV Capital announces plans for 1,800 MW natural gas generating station in Fernley to serve data center demand -- the opposite of decommissioning fossil fuel generation.
Scott H. Williams (Nevada Current): "Contrary to the purpose stated in NV Energy's Plan of Development, Greenlink will not result in decommissioning of fossil fuel power generation. Instead, Nevada can expect an increase in power generated from fossil fuels due to the sharp spike in demand caused by these data centers."

**Key quotes documenting the evolution:**
- 2020 Cannon: "Essential to unlocking Nevada's clean energy potential"
- 2021 POD: "Necessary to accommodate decommissioning of conventional fossil fuel generation"
- 2025 Brigger: "I don't think we intended to build that for data center load, but it's certainly beneficial"
- 2025 NV Energy: Data centers as "unprecedented" demand
- 2026 Basin and Range Watch: "Greenlink West has always been about bringing existing natural gas megawatt generation from [Apex] to data centers"

[Sources: NV Energy press release 2020-07-20; BLM POD 2021; Nevada Independent (SB448 investigation); Nevada Current 2026-01-12; LV Review-Journal 2025-09-18]

### 7. Northern Terminus and Data Center Proximity

**Physical geography:**
- Walker River Substation (main Greenlink hub) is in Lyon County near Yerington, ~60 miles southeast of Reno
- From Walker River, 345 kV lines extend northwest into the Reno metro area
- **Comstock Meadows Substation** is the northernmost node of the Greenlink system, located at the south end of TRIC in Storey County
- Comstock Meadows sits directly behind Google's data center complex -- literally adjacent to the data center corridor
- TRIC is described as "roughly three times the size of San Francisco" and lies along the Truckee River between Sparks and Fernley, accessible via USA Parkway from I-80

**Substations serving the data center corridor:**
- **Comstock Meadows Substation:** Currently being expanded with 345 kV additions (two 345/230 kV transformers); also has a 120 kV line serving Redwood Materials (82-140 MW)
- **West Tracy -- Comstock Meadows 345 kV line:** Under development per WECC 2026 report
- **Walker River Substation:** Four-voltage hub (525/345/230/120 kV), handles all Greenlink interconnections; two 345 kV lines run from here to TRIC

**Data centers physically present at TRIC:**
Switch (operational since 2017), Google (operational, expanding), Novva (60 MW facility completed), Tract, EdgeCore, Vantage, PowerHouse, Apple (planned/under construction), Microsoft (planned), Blockchains

**Proximity to Liberty's service territory:**
Liberty's territory is along the California-Nevada border around Lake Tahoe. Liberty connects to NV Energy at 38 points and sits within NV Energy's balancing authority. There is no direct transmission line over the Sierra Nevada to CAISO; building one would cost "hundreds of millions of dollars" (Schwarzrock). Liberty is therefore entirely dependent on NV Energy's transmission grid for any supply it procures.

[Sources: Nevada Current 2026-01-12; WECC 2026 APR; NNBW 2026-04-08; Tahoe Daily Tribune; Server Country]

### KEY FINDINGS FOR THE ARTICLE

1. **The justification bait-and-switch is documented.** Greenlink was sold to regulators, legislators, and ratepayers as essential clean energy infrastructure to meet Nevada's 50% RPS and decommission fossil fuel plants. The northern terminus literally sits behind Google's data center. Mark IV Capital is building a 1,800 MW gas plant specifically to feed the same data centers Greenlink serves. NV Energy's own business development director admitted: "I don't think we intended to build that for data center load, but it's certainly beneficial." This is the strongest narrative thread for SD #12.

2. **The legislative override is a smoking gun for regulatory capture.** When PUCN balked at the cost, NV Energy went around regulators to the legislature. SB448 was introduced two days after the PUCN's final ruling, on the 102nd day of session, with no cost caps. The PUCN later found former CEO Doug Cannon's statements about ratepayer impact "either misleading or factually inaccurate."

3. **The timing is not coincidental -- it is structural.** Liberty's supply contract ends May 2027; Greenlink West comes online mid-2027; NV Energy joins EDAM fall 2028. Every milestone is sequenced to prioritize data center load first, with Liberty told to find its own supply using the same transmission system that serves those data centers. Schwarzrock's "first in the waiting line" statement means Liberty competes on the open market against deep-pocketed data center operators for the same megawatts.

4. **Cost socialization: residential ratepayers fund data center infrastructure.** Southern Nevada customers pay 70% of Greenlink's $4.2B cost, including a $4/month Greenlink surcharge plus $25+/month from the unprecedented demand-based billing change. The data centers that consume the capacity negotiate their own generation arrangements. NV Energy is using ratepayer capital to build transmission that primarily serves industrial load -- a classic monopoly utility dynamic.

5. **Northern Nevada's import capacity is fully allocated at 1,275 MW.** Greenlink West raises this to 2,000 MW, North to 2,800 MW. But data center demand requests total 21,000-22,000 MW. Even with both Greenlink segments online, the system will be capacity-constrained almost immediately. Liberty's 49,000 customers compete for scraps against hyperscale demand in a market where the utility has already committed 4,000 MW to data centers.

### GAPS AND UNCERTAINTIES

- **"First in the waiting line" source:** The exact quote appears in Energy News Beat (May 2026) attributed to Schwarzrock. Have not located the original primary source (filing or hearing transcript). The claim's precise legal meaning is unclear -- is it a contractual commitment or a conversational assurance?
- **Federal funding:** Found no evidence of DOE loans or IIJA grants for Greenlink. The absence is notable for a project this large, but could not confirm NV Energy never applied. The DOE TFP selected other Nevada projects (Southwest Intertie, Cross-Tie) but not Greenlink.
- **Individual cost breakdown:** NV Energy has not publicly split the $4.24B between Greenlink West and Greenlink North. The 472-mile West segment is significantly longer than the 235-mile North, but substation costs (Walker River is shared) complicate allocation.
- **Firm vs. indicative commitments:** The 4,000 MW promised to data centers -- whether these are binding large customer service agreements, interconnection queue positions, or indicative commitments is not clear from public reporting.
- **Greenlink West lawsuit status:** Friends of Nevada Wilderness / Basin and Range Watch filed in May 2025; no resolution reported as of May 2026. Construction has continued despite the suit.
- **NNBW "after summer 2027" vs. original "May 2027":** The April 2026 article describes completion "after the summer of 2027." This appears to be a quiet delay of 3-4 months from the originally cited May 2027 date. NV Energy has not publicly acknowledged a delay.
