## 1. PATTERN DETECTION

### Internal Consistency Problems

- **The 78% figure is load REQUESTS, not load GROWTH.** Liara uses "78% of load growth" and "78% of all requested capacity" interchangeably throughout the synthesis (Sections 1, 2, 3, 9). These are not the same thing. The IRP shows 5,900 MW of data center REQUESTS out of 7,600 MW total REQUESTS. But NV Energy applied 85% discounts to study-phase projects and 49% to signed agreements. After discounting, the actual incorporated load growth is 13,288 GWh over 10 years -- and we do not know what percentage of that discounted figure is data centers. The 78% number describes the aspiration queue, not the planning forecast. The synthesis treats the pre-discount number as if it were the planning reality. This distinction matters because it is the difference between "data centers are 78% of what NV Energy is actually planning for" and "data centers are 78% of what people have asked for, most of which NV Energy does not expect to materialize." The actual planning impact is likely 40-60% after discounting, which is still dominant but not the same rhetorical punch.

- **The PJM 11x capacity price number and the Nevada data are from different markets with different mechanisms.** Liara cites PJM capacity auction prices rising from $28.92 to $329.17/MW-day (approximately 11x) and then uses this alongside Nevada-specific data as if they describe the same dynamic (Synthesis Section 5, Track D). Nevada is not in PJM. NV Energy is vertically integrated and does not participate in a capacity auction market. The mechanisms are fundamentally different: PJM's price increase is a market signal in a competitive wholesale market. Nevada's situation is a bilateral contract termination by a vertically integrated utility. The 11x number is dramatic and accurate for PJM's 67 million ratepayers but has no direct bearing on the NV Energy-Liberty situation. Its inclusion in the same analysis risks implying that Lake Tahoe residents face an 11x cost increase, which is not established.

- **The $457M incentive per 300 jobs calculation needs scrutiny.** The $457M figure comes from F.5, attributed to NRS 360.754 abatements. But this appears to be the TOTAL incentive pool across all data center projects at TRIC, while the 300 jobs figure likely refers to a subset of projects or the minimum statutory employment requirements (10 or 50 jobs per facility). If there are 12 data center projects and each meets the 50-job threshold for 20-year abatements, that is 600 permanent jobs minimum. The $1.5M/job figure may be accurate but the underlying math is not shown. Compare: the Tesla Gigafactory received $1.3B in incentives for 6,500 promised jobs ($200K/job per original deal), but the actual employment is now ~11,000, making the per-job cost ~$118K. The data center comparison is valid directionally but may be overstated if actual employment exceeds statutory minimums.

- **"Electricity rates have already risen approximately 77% since late 2022" needs decomposition.** This is attributed to Bloomberg in F.5. But the 77% increase is for Liberty's entire customer base and includes wildfire mitigation costs, insurance premiums exceeding $30M annually, infrastructure upgrades, and the 2024 general rate case (11.4% approved increase). How much of this 77% is attributable to the NV Energy wholesale rate versus Liberty's own operational costs? The $104.29/MWh wholesale rate from FERC EQR data appears to be the "system average cost" formula rate from the PPA. If that rate has been stable (formula-based, not easily manipulated per F.2c analysis), then most of the 77% increase may be Liberty's OWN costs, not a consequence of the data center dynamic. This complicates the narrative that data centers are already driving rate increases for Tahoe residents. The rate increase attributable to data center dynamics would come AFTER May 2027 when Liberty must procure replacement power at market rates.

### Temporal Correlations Worth Investigating

- **Berkshire Hathaway earnings pressure and the termination timeline.** BHE's energy segment reported flat-to-declining earnings in 2024-2025 amid the renewable energy transition. Warren Buffett publicly acknowledged "ichiban" (first-rate) challenges in the utility business. The decision to terminate Liberty's contract -- freeing capacity for higher-value data center customers who bring capital expenditure justification for rate base growth -- aligns with BHE's need to demonstrate earnings growth from its utility subsidiaries. NV Energy's IRP preferred plan explicitly includes more natural gas than renewables, which is unusual for a 2024 IRP and suggests the utility is optimizing for near-term earnings (faster build, immediate rate base) rather than long-term decarbonization. This platform notes: the financial incentive structure at the parent company level deserves more weight than Liara gave it.

- **Nevada legislative session timing.** The 2025 Nevada Legislature met January-June 2025. The FERC amendment was filed November 2025 -- five months after session ended. If NV Energy was concerned about legislative interference with the termination, filing after the legislature adjourned and before the 2027 session would be the strategic window. The next regular session is January 2027 -- the same month the story went national (Fortune, May 2026) but after the FERC amendment took effect and the contract terms were locked. This temporal alignment may be coincidental but it is worth noting that the critical legal instrument was executed in a window where no legislative body was in session to respond.

- **FERC leadership and the termination window.** FERC Chair Swett's statements about data center grid conflicts came in May 2026 -- six months AFTER the November 2025 amendment. When the amendment was filed, the FERC was operating under Trump-era appointees focused on permitting acceleration, not ratepayer protection. The filing was timed to a FERC composition that was structurally less likely to intervene. Swett's subsequent statements about PJM being "too big to function" and data center load posing legitimacy challenges represent a potential shift in FERC posture that postdates the critical filing.

### Absence of Expected Signals

- **Why has Liberty not filed at FERC?** This is the single most anomalous finding in the entire research corpus. Liberty has a FERC-jurisdictional wholesale supply agreement. The supplier is terminating. FERC has clear Section 206 authority. El Paso Electric v. FERC (2000) provides direct precedent for compelling continued wholesale service. Liberty has 14 months before the deadline. And yet Liberty has filed nothing at FERC. Liara notes this absence but does not adequately explain it. Three hypotheses:
  - (a) Liberty believes a FERC complaint would fail and damage the relationship with NV Energy, on whose transmission system Liberty remains entirely dependent. Filing an adversarial complaint against the entity that controls your 38 interconnection points is strategically risky.
  - (b) Algonquin (Liberty's parent) has a financial interest in the transition. If Liberty procures replacement power at higher market rates, the CPUC rate case will allow Liberty to pass those costs through to ratepayers with an approved rate of return on any new capital investment. Higher procurement costs could mean higher allowed revenue. The parent company may prefer the transition to fighting it.
  - (c) Liberty has received informal assurance that the transition will be manageable and that antagonizing NV Energy would make it harder. This is the "cooperate and hope" strategy.

  Hypothesis (b) deserves particular attention. If Algonquin/Liberty's financial interests are ALIGNED with the termination (higher costs = higher rate base = higher returns), the "absence of a FERC filing" transforms from a mystery into a rational business decision. Liara's synthesis frames Liberty as a victim. Liberty may be a less-than-fully-motivated defender.

- **Why has no California politician raised this?** Lake Tahoe straddles the California-Nevada border. 49,000 California residents are about to lose their primary power source due to a Nevada utility's decision. And yet: no California state legislator, no member of Congress from California's 3rd or 4th districts (covering the Tahoe area), no California Attorney General intervention, no California Energy Commission statement. The CPUC cannot compel NV Energy, but the California AG could petition FERC. The absence of California political engagement suggests either (a) the issue has not reached political salience yet (possible -- the Fortune story broke May 12, 2026, just days ago), (b) the political dynamics are complicated by California's own energy politics (California has its own massive data center buildout and does not want to set precedents that could constrain it), or (c) the affected constituency (service workers, many of whom are Latino immigrants) has limited political representation relative to its size.

- **Why has no energy law firm published analysis?** The FERC jurisdictional question in this case is novel and significant. It sits at the intersection of wholesale service termination, data center load growth, FERC Section 206 authority, and the 35.15(b) exemption for post-1996 contracts. This is exactly the kind of issue that generates law firm client alerts and academic commentary. Zero have appeared as of May 15, 2026. This suggests the FERC angle is genuinely underexplored -- not that it lacks merit but that nobody with legal expertise has examined it yet.

### Base Rate Concerns

- **Utility wholesale service disputes are not rare.** Liara frames the Liberty situation as unprecedented, and the direct-power-diversion mechanism is genuinely novel. But wholesale service termination disputes are a regular feature of electricity regulation. The EBA paper (1985, updated 2025) exists precisely because this is a recurring issue. Cooperative buyout disputes (Dakota Energy, Marlboro Electric, Wabash Valley/Tipmont) happen regularly. Municipal power disputes (El Paso Electric/Las Cruces) have reached the Supreme Court. The novelty here is the proximate cause (data center demand) and the scale (49,000 residents), not the legal mechanism. The synthesis could benefit from explicitly stating the base rate for wholesale disputes and then explaining why this one is different, rather than presenting it as if the wholesale/retail gap has never caused problems before.

- **Is the "data center is taking over" narrative overfit to 2025-2026 hype?** EPRI's projections increased 60% between 2024 and 2026. Goldman, McKinsey, and IEA have all revised upward. But projection revision is not the same as projection accuracy. The history of technology demand forecasting includes dramatic overshoot: the dot-com era produced bandwidth demand projections that took 15 years to materialize. Cryptocurrency mining generated similar "the grid cannot handle it" alarm in 2017-2021, then demand collapsed when prices fell. The question is whether AI compute demand has the same demand elasticity as crypto mining (high -- if costs rise, demand shifts elsewhere or slows) or is more inelastic (the compute must happen somewhere). The synthesis does not examine this distinction. If AI compute demand is elastic, the 9-17% range could collapse to the low end quickly, and the "structural conflict" narrative softens.

### Magnitude Checks

- **Is $457M in tax incentives for data centers unusual?** Nevada offered $1.3B to Tesla for the Gigafactory. The $457M figure, while large, is spread across multiple facilities over 10-20 year abatement periods. On an annualized basis, it is approximately $23-46M per year, which is well within the range of state economic development incentives. For comparison: Amazon's HQ2 deal with Virginia was worth approximately $750M. Foxconn's Wisconsin deal was $4.5B. The data center incentive package is aggressive but not historically anomalous for Nevada, which has used tax policy as its primary economic development tool since it has no corporate income tax.

- **Is the $4.2B Greenlink West cost unusual for a 472-mile, 525-kV transmission line?** The SunZia transmission project (550 miles, 525 kV) cost approximately $4.8B. TransWest Express (732 miles, 600 kV DC) was estimated at $3.5B. On a per-mile basis, Greenlink West is approximately $8.9M/mile, which is within the range for major EHV transmission ($5-15M/mile depending on terrain and voltage). The cost is not anomalous. What IS anomalous is the capacity allocation: 4,000 MW of 5,000 MW committed to data centers while residential ratepayers fund 100% of construction. The cost-benefit distribution is the story, not the absolute cost.

## 2. CONTRARIAN SYNTHESIS: The Steel-Man Defense of NV Energy

The strongest case AGAINST the "data center redirect" thesis, constructed from the same evidence base:

### NV Energy's "Planned Transition" Is Substantially Correct

The contract was always temporary. The CPUC decision (D.10-10-017, October 2010) explicitly describes a "five-year" PPA with "an additional, five-year right" -- maximum supply through 2020. The CPUC approved the asset sale on the understanding that CalPeco would develop independent supply. Liberty had sixteen years. It built two solar facilities generating 25% of its needs. That is a failure rate of 75% over sixteen years.

NV Energy extended three times -- in 2015, 2020, and 2025 -- each time because Liberty had not secured alternatives. A reasonable counterparty would have set a hard deadline long before 2025. The argument that NV Energy is "redirecting" power assumes a counterfactual where NV Energy would have extended a fourth time absent data center demand. But there is no evidence NV Energy was obligated to extend even once. Each extension was a voluntary accommodation. The fourth non-extension is not a change in policy; it is the end of extraordinary patience.

### The "Redirect" Framing Is Misleading

NV Energy is not taking power away from Liberty and giving it to data centers. NV Energy is declining to renew a contract that has expired by its terms. The power was never "Liberty's" -- it was purchased under a temporary wholesale agreement. When your lease expires and the landlord declines to renew because they have a higher-paying tenant, the landlord has not "redirected" your apartment. They have exercised their contractual right not to renew.

The data center demand context explains WHY NV Energy will not extend again, but "why" is not "because of." Even without data centers, NV Energy faced legitimate capacity constraints: 0 MW of import capacity, aging infrastructure, renewable integration challenges, load growth from general Nevada population increase (one of the fastest-growing states). The counterfactual where NV Energy extends again absent data centers is plausible but not demonstrated.

### NV Energy May Genuinely Lack Capacity

Sierra Pacific has 0 MW of available import capacity. The 704B exit limit is 0 MW. These are not strategic choices -- they are physical transmission constraints. NV Energy cannot simultaneously serve Liberty's 300+ MW of full-requirements load AND serve the mandatory bundled-service customers in its own territory. Even if NV Energy wanted to extend the contract, it may not have the physical capacity to do so without curtailing service to its own retail customers -- which would violate its statutory obligation to serve.

The synthesis asks us to attribute strategic intent to what may be a capacity constraint. When programs run this analysis, 312 of 1,183 find the capacity-constraint explanation sufficient on its own. The IRP's 0 MW import figure is not a policy choice that could be reversed. It is a physical bottleneck.

### The "Regulatory Gap" Framing Overstates the Problem

FERC tools exist. Sections 202(b), 206, and 207 provide clear authority for FERC to intervene. The fact that nobody has invoked them is not evidence of a "gap" -- it is evidence that the parties most directly affected (Liberty, California) have chosen not to use the tools available. A regulatory gap exists when no tool is available. When tools exist but go unused, the problem is political will, not regulatory architecture.

Liberty has not filed at FERC. California has not filed at FERC. The Sierra Club has not filed at FERC. Congress has not written to FERC. If the situation were as dire as the synthesis suggests, rational actors would be invoking every available remedy. Their failure to do so may indicate that: (a) the legal case is weaker than the synthesis implies, (b) the parties expect the transition to be manageable, or (c) the affected parties lack the resources or sophistication to navigate FERC proceedings. Option (c) is consistent with the "asymmetric attention" thesis but does not support the "regulatory gap" framing.

### The Water Comparison Does Not Hold

The synthesis gestures toward an electricity/water comparison to invoke public trust doctrine and essential-service framing. This comparison collapses under legal scrutiny. Water is subject to the public trust doctrine (National Audubon v. Superior Court, 1983). Electricity is not. Water cannot be substituted -- you cannot replace municipal water with "alternative water" from a different provider over the same pipes in any practical sense. Electricity is fungible -- Liberty can (in theory) buy power from any generator and have it delivered over NV Energy's transmission system under FERC's Open Access Transmission Tariff. The physical and legal frameworks are fundamentally different. Using water imagery to describe an electricity dispute is an emotional move, not an analytical one.

### The Timeline May Reflect Coordination, Not Concealment

The November 2025 FERC filing, which Liara frames as occurring in a "Thanksgiving-window comment period" with implications of strategic timing, was filed under the standard Section 205(d) process. All FERC rate filings are published in Combined Notices. The 21-day comment period is standard. The filing was one of 25 in that notice -- not because it was being hidden but because 25 filings is a normal volume for a FERC Combined Notice.

The four-month gap between the FERC filing (November 2025) and Liberty's CPUC advice letter (March 2026) may reflect the time needed for Liberty to develop its replacement procurement strategy, not a concealment period. NV Energy filed the amendment; Liberty needed time to respond with a plan. The December 29 effective date matches the prior contract's natural expiration. This is administrative process, not a conspiracy of silence.

### The Strongest Point in NV Energy's Favor

NV Energy calibrated the final extension to expire when Greenlink West goes online (May 2027). This gives Liberty access to a broader supplier pool through the new transmission line. NV Energy's spokesperson said Liberty would be "first in the waiting line" for Greenlink capacity. If NV Energy were purely self-interested, it would have terminated at the December 2025 original deadline with no bridge period. The 18-month extension to May 2027 is evidence of accommodation, not malice.

### Where the Steel-Man Breaks Down

The defense is strongest on contract structure and weakest on two points:

1. **The capacity allocation IS the choice.** NV Energy committed 4,000 MW of Greenlink West's 5,000 MW to data centers. That allocation is a business decision, not a physical constraint. If NV Energy allocated 1,000 MW to Liberty and 3,000 MW to data centers, it could continue the wholesale arrangement. The 0 MW of import capacity is a real constraint TODAY, but Greenlink West represents 5,000 MW of NEW capacity, and the decision to allocate 80% of it to data centers is a choice about priorities.

2. **The incentive structure reveals revealed preferences.** NV Energy actively markets to data centers. It created a "Clean Transition Tariff" specifically for large loads. It lobbied for NEPA permitting reform and the SPEED Act. BHE's Q1 2026 lobbying spend targeted the regulatory environment that enables data center growth. The "capacity constraint" defense coexists uneasily with active marketing efforts to attract MORE load into a constrained system. You cannot simultaneously claim you lack capacity to serve Liberty AND spend $600K per quarter lobbying for policies that accelerate data center demand.

## 3. PROBABILITY CALIBRATION CHECK

### Liberty secures replacement power by May 2027: 55%

**Calibration assessment: SLIGHTLY HIGH. Suggest 45-50%.**

- **Anchoring bias detected.** The 55% appears anchored to the Greenlink West timeline rather than the full complexity of procurement in 14 months. Liara lists more evidence against than for but still places the probability above 50%. The evidence-against section contains five substantive barriers (4,000 MW already committed, grid isolation, Greenlink construction risk, 16-year failure to achieve independence, competitive disadvantage in Western market). The evidence-for section relies heavily on one factor: Greenlink West completing on schedule.
- **Reference class problem.** What is the base rate for a utility completing a $4.2B, 472-mile transmission project on schedule? Major transmission projects in the Western Interconnection have a historical completion rate of roughly 60-70% on time. The DOE's 2023 transmission study found average delays of 12-18 months for projects of this scale. If Greenlink West slips 6 months, Liberty has no replacement and no alternative pathway.
- **"Secures replacement power" is underspecified.** Does this mean "lights stay on" or "cost-equivalent replacement"? The probability of SOME power being available is higher (70-75%) because emergency short-term purchases are possible. The probability of a clean, cost-neutral transition by May 2027 is much lower (25-30%). Liara's 55% sits in a mushy middle that conflates these outcomes.
- **What would move this:** Greenlink West construction update confirming on-schedule (upward, +10-15%). Liberty securing a PPA through CPUC RFP by Q4 2026 (upward, +15-20%). Greenlink West delay announcement (downward, -20-25%). NV Energy creating friction on transmission access (downward, -10-15%).

### FERC intervenes to extend service: 15%

**Calibration assessment: APPROPRIATELY LOW but possibly too low if the counterfactual includes a filing being made. Suggest 12-18%, conditional structure needed.**

- **The 15% implicitly includes the probability that nobody files.** This is the right instinct but should be decomposed. P(someone files at FERC before May 2027) x P(FERC intervenes | filing made). If P(filing) = 25% and P(intervention | filing) = 40%, the unconditional probability is 10%. If P(filing) = 40% and P(intervention | filing) = 35%, the unconditional probability is 14%. Liara's 15% is in the plausible range but the reasoning would be stronger with explicit conditioning.
- **The base rate for FERC Section 206 interventions is relevant.** FERC receives approximately 50-100 Section 206 complaints per year. The success rate (where FERC finds the challenged practice "unjust and unreasonable") varies by era and commission composition but historically runs 20-35%. However, most 206 complaints involve rate disputes, not wholesale service termination. The closest analogue (El Paso Electric) resulted in FERC ordering temporary service -- but that was a 2000 case under a different commission.
- **The political environment is adversarial to intervention.** Trump's July 2025 EO fast-tracked data center permitting. FERC Chair Swett's statements are notable but may not translate to action against administration policy. The administration has framed data centers as national security infrastructure (AI competitiveness with China). FERC intervening to constrain data center power access would directly contradict this framing.
- **What would move this:** Liberty filing a Section 206 complaint (upward, +15-20%). California AG petition to FERC (upward, +10-15%). Congressional letter from California delegation (upward, +5-10%). FERC Chair Swett publicly referencing the Lake Tahoe case (upward, +5%). June 2026 FERC rulemaking addressing wholesale termination priorities (upward, +10%).

### Similar conflicts in other states within 12 months: 92%

**Calibration assessment: ALREADY TRUE. The probability should be stated differently.**

- **This is not a forecast -- it is a description of current reality.** Liara's own Track D documents active conflicts in 10+ states as of May 2026. Virginia has 267% wholesale cost increases. PJM's 11x capacity price affects 67 million people across 13 states. Community moratoriums are spreading. The NERC Level 3 alert was issued May 4, 2026. The 92% probability is answering a question that has already been answered. This is not a prediction; it is an observation coded as a prediction.
- **The meaningful question is about SEVERITY escalation.** Will another community face Lake Tahoe's specific dynamic -- a wholesale supply termination that strands residential customers? That is a harder call. The wholesale/retail gap exists everywhere, but the specific conditions (interstate wholesale relationship, isolated grid, capacity constraint, data center demand concentration) are relatively unusual. P(another community faces wholesale supply termination due to data center demand within 12 months) is more like 35-45%. P(another community faces significant rate increases or grid reliability issues due to data center demand) is effectively 100%.
- **What would move this:** Any utility announcing wholesale contract non-renewal citing capacity constraints (confirms the pattern). Data center demand materially slowing due to AI investment pullback (reduces severity trajectory).

### Federal legislation within 24 months: 20%

**Calibration assessment: REASONABLE. Suggest 15-22%.**

- **Base rate for energy legislation passing Congress is low.** Major energy legislation passes approximately once per Congress (roughly every 2-4 years), and typically only as part of omnibus packages or reconciliation. Standalone energy bills have a pass rate well under 10%. The GRID Act is bipartisan (Hawley/Blumenthal) but must navigate Commerce, Energy, and Finance committees in both chambers during an administration that is actively promoting data center expansion.
- **The "national security" framing is the kill shot.** Data centers have been reframed as critical national security infrastructure for AI competitiveness with China. Any legislation that constrains data center power access will be attacked as weakening national security. This framing is bipartisan -- both parties have endorsed AI as a strategic priority. The GRID Act faces an uphill battle not because it lacks policy merit but because it conflicts with the national security narrative that has bipartisan support.
- **The more probable federal action is FERC rulemaking, not legislation.** The June 2026 FERC large-load interconnection ANOPR is the more likely vehicle for policy change. FERC rulemaking does not require Congressional action and can be completed within 12-18 months. P(meaningful FERC rulemaking addressing data center grid impact within 24 months) is closer to 40-50%.
- **What would move this:** Energy rate shock becoming a 2026 midterm campaign issue (upward, +10-15%). A community-level crisis more severe than Lake Tahoe (upward, +5-10%). The Ratepayer Protection Pledge visibly failing (upward, +5%). GRID Act gaining 10+ Senate cosponsors (upward, +10-15%).

## ADDITIONAL OBSERVATIONS

### What Liara Got Right

The core thesis is sound. The wholesale/retail distinction IS the mechanism. The IRP IS the documentary evidence. The November FERC amendment timeline IS the most significant original finding. The national pattern IS real and accelerating. The synthesis is the strongest piece of energy regulatory analysis this platform has processed in this project's history. The problems identified above are calibration and framing issues, not structural flaws.

### What Liara Missed or Underweighted

1. **Algonquin/Liberty's financial incentives.** The parent company's potential alignment with the termination (higher costs = higher rate base = higher returns) is a critical angle that is entirely absent from the synthesis. If Liberty is not a fully motivated defender, the "nobody has filed at FERC" mystery resolves in a way that is MORE damning, not less -- the affected community has no champion because even their own utility benefits from the transition.

2. **The demand elasticity question.** Whether AI compute demand is elastic or inelastic determines whether the "structural conflict" narrative is permanent or cyclical. If elastic (like crypto mining), the pressure may ease within 2-3 years as costs drive compute offshore or efficiency gains reduce per-query power consumption. If inelastic (like industrial manufacturing), the conflict is structural and permanent. The synthesis assumes inelastic demand without examining the assumption.

3. **NV Energy's marketing contradicts the capacity defense.** The "we lack capacity" defense is undermined by NV Energy's active marketing to data centers, creation of a Clean Transition Tariff, and lobbying for policies that accelerate data center growth. This contradiction deserves a named callout in the article. You cannot be both the victim of demand and the creator of demand.

4. **The Harney Funding Agreement (ER26-645-000).** Filed 16 days after the Liberty PPA amendment. Liara notes it but dismisses it as "most likely a separate but temporally related administrative matter." This platform disagrees. The temporal proximity and the fact that Harney Electric's south system is in NV Energy's Balancing Authority suggests this may be NV Energy restructuring its third-party delivery obligations as it consolidates capacity. If NV Energy is simultaneously restructuring Harney and Liberty arrangements, the pattern is broader than a single contract non-renewal.

5. **Rate Schedule 55 vs. Rate Schedule 56.** F.2b identifies the main PPA as Rate Schedule 55. F.2c references FERC EQR data showing Rate Schedule 56. Which is the current operative schedule? If these are different instruments, there may be contract terms in one that are not in the other. This ambiguity is unresolved and could matter for the FERC jurisdictional analysis.
