## NV Energy IRP Findings

### The 2024 Joint IRP: Capacity Allocation in Black and White

NV Energy's 2024 Joint IRP (filed May 2024, approved December 2024) is the foundational planning document. The load forecast section (Volume 6) contains the critical numbers:

**Total load requests:** 39 bundled-service projects requesting approximately 7,600 MW of capacity additions by 2033.

**Geographic concentration:** Nearly 6,500 MW at Sierra Pacific (northern Nevada), 1,180 MW at Nevada Power (southern Nevada). 85% of all new load is in Sierra Pacific's service territory.

**Data center dominance:** 12 of the 39 projects are "bundled-service high load factor data centers" requesting 5,900 MW of capacity alone. Data centers represent ~78% of all requested capacity.

**Scaling methodology:** NV Energy applied heavy discounts to these requests:
- Study-phase projects (25 of 39): 85% reduction (only 15% of requested load included)
- Signed Rule 9 agreements (14 of 39): 49% reduction
- Even after discounts, the IRP incorporates 13,288 GWh of load growth over 10 years

**Sierra Pacific growth rate:** 4.4% annually over the 2025-2044 planning period. Annual energy demand projected to increase 64% from 2023 to 2030, and more than double from 2023 to 2045.

**The "open position" (capacity gap):** After existing resources, the IRP shows the gap increasing by 100-500 MW per year. The Preferred Plan adds:
- 1,000+ MW paired solar and storage
- 400+ MW hydrogen-capable natural gas combustion turbines at North Valmy
- NV Energy's CEO stated the "preferred plan" will add more natural gas than renewables

**Import capacity constraint (critical):** Sierra Pacific's 704B annual limit was set to 0 MW during the 2024-2027 action plan period due to severe import capacity constraints. Sierra had 681 MW of import transmission requests in queue with no available capacity. This means: no large customer can exit NV Energy in northern Nevada because there is literally no transmission capacity to serve them independently.

### The 2025 First IRP Amendment: Liberty Shows Up Explicitly

PUCN Docket No. 25-10028 (filed October 28, 2025) -- a single-item amendment that directly connects the Liberty situation to capacity planning:

**Load forecast adjustment:** "The Liberty Utilities LLC load servicing agreement, previously scheduled to end in December 2025, has been extended until May 2027." This is listed as a change to the approved 2024 IRP load forecast.

**Why the amendment was needed:** "Due to the cancellation and delay of certain approved resources in recent months, combined with adjustments to the load forecast, a need for additional capacity for Northern Nevada starting in 2027 has arisen."

**Resource failures:** 144 MW of approved northern solar, battery, and geothermal resources were cancelled or delayed. Distributed and demand-side resources not approved in the 2024 IRP were removed.

**The fix:** A 150 MW Dodge Flat Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) was selected from an all-source RFP (200 bids received) with a July 1, 2027 commercial operation date.

**Key implication:** The IRP amendment explicitly treats the Liberty load extension as a planning variable -- one that creates additional capacity need alongside data center growth. The utility is filing amendments to manage the capacity crunch, and Liberty's contract end date is being coordinated with resource availability.

### The April 2026 IRP Filing (Draft, Upcoming)

NV Energy announced it will submit a new triennial IRP in April 2026 -- one year early -- because:
- The utility needs 47% more energy than forecast just two years ago
- The queue now exceeds 15,600 MW beyond existing system capacity
- The "preferred plan" calls for 4,000 MW new renewables, 4,200 MW battery storage, but leans more heavily on natural gas (1,200 MW over 20 years)

## Sierra Club Testimony

### Jeremy Fisher / Synapse Energy Economics Expert Testimony

Fortune reports reviewing Sierra Club expert testimony filed with Nevada regulators that identified:

**75% figure:** "In NV Energy's own 2024 resource plan, about 75% of major-project load growth is attributed to data centers, according to Sierra Club expert testimony filed with Nevada regulators." This is the key statistic that quantifies the data center share of load growth.

**Rose Anderson (Synapse Energy Economics) testimony:** Filed with Nevada regulators in October 2024, warning that NV Energy's major-project load forecast is "highly uncertain" and that existing customers could end up paying for infrastructure built to serve industrial demand that never materializes.

### Sierra Club National Policy Position (Jeremy Fisher, Principal Advisor)

Jeremy Fisher authored a January 2026 Sierra Club policy guide specifically addressing data center utility impacts nationwide. Key points relevant to Nevada:

- "We're already seeing the costs of data center growth reflected in customers' bills"
- "Utilities are pushing data center costs and speculative risks onto ratepayers"
- Recommends: separable rate classes for data centers, protective large load tariffs, transparency requirements, clean energy mandates

### Sierra Club Toiyabe Chapter (Local Nevada)

Olivia Tanager, Sierra Club Toiyabe Chapter Director, has been the most vocal local voice:

- Called for a pause on data center development in northern Nevada
- Warned that "a voluntary handshake won't protect Nevada families from higher utility bills"
- On behind-the-meter generation: "This is exactly the sort of thing we've been concerned about... this is a massive scale gas power plant"
- On behind-the-meter: "locks the public out of the process" because regulators lack oversight
- Sierra Club has found utility rates increased for residents in states with data center rushes

## PUCN Proceedings

### Key Dockets

- **25-10028:** Joint Application for approval of First Amendment to 2024 Joint IRP (Liberty load extension + Dodge Flat BESS)
- **24-05 (IRP):** 2024 Joint IRP, approved December 2024
- **23-02011/23-02010:** Large Customer Market Price Energy (LCMPE) schedule revisions for Energy Supply Agreements
- **25-05010:** Rate misclassification investigation (NV Energy overcharged tens of thousands of multi-family customers -- $65M+ restitution, separate from data center issue but shows regulatory environment)

### Rate Proceedings

**September 2025 Rate Approval:**
- PUCN approved rate hike specifically to recoup Greenlink West construction costs
- Southern Nevada: ~$4.35-4.42/month increase for residential customers
- Statewide: Peak usage rate increasing bills by over $25/month for most customers
- Cost split: 70% Southern Nevada, 30% Northern Nevada

### Regulatory Gap Identified

No PUCN order was found that explicitly addresses how NV Energy allocates generation between existing wholesale obligations (Liberty) and new data center load. The decision to terminate the Liberty agreement appears to be a business decision by NV Energy, not a PUCN directive. This is a significant regulatory gap -- the commission approves IRP resource additions but does not appear to have ruled on the question of whether existing wholesale customers should be prioritized over new industrial customers.

## Greenlink West Cost Allocation

### Project Overview
- **Cost:** $4.2 billion (up from $2.5B initial estimate in 2021)
- **Scope:** 472 miles, 525 kV transmission from Las Vegas to Yerington (Comstock Meadows Substation at TRIC)
- **Northern terminus:** Comstock Meadows Substation -- "currently undergoing an expansion to become the northernmost node of Greenlink West" -- located directly behind Google's data center complex at TRIC
- **Southern terminus:** Apex Generating Station northeast of Las Vegas (enables natural gas power to flow from south to north)
- **Status:** Construction underway, expected operational May 2027

### Who Pays

**Residential ratepayers bear the cost:**
- 70% funded by Southern Nevada customers
- 30% funded by Northern Nevada customers
- Already designated a "critical facility" allowing NV Energy to request additional construction funding
- September 2025: PUCN approved 50% of recorded costs for inclusion in rate-change requests

### Who Benefits

**Data centers are the primary beneficiaries:**
- NV Energy has promised Switch, Novva, and others up to 4,000 MW of the combined 5,000 MW Greenlink capacity
- Nevada Current: "NV Energy acknowledged a suite of tech companies will be allowed to access electricity transmitted via Greenlink lines"
- The line was originally pitched as necessary for clean energy/decarbonization goals but is now primarily serving data center demand
- "As it becomes apparent that the electricity demand of new data centers in northern Nevada will eclipse the transmission capacity of the new Greenlink lines before the project is completed, Nevadans have now joined the ranks of ordinary working people across the country subsidizing Big Tech's AI infrastructure buildout through their local electric utility" (Nevada Current)

### The Paradox for Liberty

NV Energy's own spokesperson said Liberty would be "first in the waiting line" when Greenlink opens in May 2027. But:
- 4,000 MW of 5,000 MW capacity is already committed to data centers
- The May 2027 Greenlink operational date matches Liberty's contract termination exactly
- Liberty still depends entirely on NV Energy's transmission infrastructure
- Building independent California transmission: hundreds of millions, years of construction

## Behind-the-Meter Bypass

### Fleet Data Centers / Tract Capital: Peru Ridge and South Valley

**Filed April 20, 2026 with Nevada state energy regulators:**
- **Peru Ridge Self-Generation Project:** 218 MW natural gas, powering data center campus at TRIC
- **South Valley:** 144 MW temporary natural gas + diesel plant
- **Total:** 362 MW behind-the-meter generation
- **Developer:** Fleet Data Centers (subsidiary of Tract Capital Management)
- **Tenant:** Unnamed "AA- investment grade rated tenant with a market cap in excess of $3 trillion" (narrows to Apple, Microsoft, or Alphabet)

**Why behind-the-meter:**
- NV Energy cannot provide power for at least two years
- "Data center growth at TRIC is outpacing the ability of NV Energy to provide enough power"
- The 350 MW total would be "more than one quarter the amount of natural gas power NV Energy expects to develop over the next few years"

**Regulatory bypass:**
- Behind-the-meter customers are NOT subject to Nevada's Renewable Portfolio Standard
- 704B customers (who leave NV Energy but stay on grid) must comply with RPS
- BTM customers escape both utility oversight and clean energy mandates
- TRIC's master plan allows "over the counter" permits -- no county commissioner approval needed for core area projects
- Peru Ridge is in the core area: permitted by right

**Environmental concerns:**
- Sierra Club: "locks the public out of the process"
- Nevada fell short of GHG emission reduction goals in 2025
- Behind-the-meter gas at this scale undermines state climate targets
- Air quality permits submitted to Nevada Division of Environmental Protection but with minimal public review

### National Pattern (Cleanview Data)

Per Cleanview's comprehensive report:
- 46 data centers nationally are building 56 GW of behind-the-meter power
- Represents ~30% of all planned US data center capacity
- "Nearly 75% of the generation equipment we could identify (23 GW) was natural gas-powered"
- "Press releases for these projects say one thing. But the 35+ permit documents, site plans, and equipment deals we found tell a much different story."
- Renewables, where committed, are scheduled for 2028 or later; nuclear is a decade away

## SMOKING GUN ASSESSMENT

### Does the IRP explicitly trade Liberty for data centers?

**Evidence level: STRONG CIRCUMSTANTIAL -- approaching documentary proof**

The IRP does not contain a sentence that reads "we are terminating Liberty to serve data centers." However, the documentary evidence creates an overwhelming inferential case:

**What the documents prove:**

1. **The IRP explicitly plans for data center load as the dominant driver.** 75% of major-project load growth is data centers per Sierra Club testimony. 5,900 MW of the 7,600 MW requested is data centers. This is not disputed.

2. **The IRP Amendment explicitly links Liberty's termination to capacity planning.** Docket 25-10028 treats the Liberty contract extension to May 2027 as a load forecast variable that creates additional capacity need. The amendment was filed specifically because resources were insufficient for northern Nevada starting in 2027 -- the same date Liberty's contract ends.

3. **Sierra Pacific has ZERO import capacity.** The 704B annual limit for Sierra is 0 MW. There is no spare capacity in northern Nevada. Every MW allocated to data centers is a MW not available for other purposes, including wholesale service to Liberty.

4. **Greenlink is ratepayer-funded but data-center-allocated.** $4.2B paid by residential/commercial ratepayers. 4,000 MW of 5,000 MW promised to data centers. The line terminates at a substation behind Google's TRIC campus.

5. **NV Energy's own statements confirm resource constraints.** The utility cited "growing demand and its own resource constraints" for ending the Liberty agreement. It needs 47% more energy than forecast two years ago. The queue exceeds 15,600 MW beyond current system.

6. **Liberty's filing to CPUC said NV Energy cited data centers.** Liberty told California regulators that NV Energy cited "data centers in the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center area and northern Nevada transmission constraints" among reasons for ending full-requirements service.

**What prevents this from being a full "smoking gun":**

- NV Energy's defense is temporally accurate: the Liberty arrangement was always described as temporary since 2009, extended in 2015, 2020, and 2025. The utility frames it as a planned transition, not a data center reaction.
- No PUCN order directs the reallocation. This appears to be a business decision within NV Energy's discretion.
- The Synapse economist's caution about forecast uncertainty cuts both ways -- the data center load may not fully materialize, but neither might Liberty's alternatives.

**The narrative synthesis:**

NV Energy did not wake up one day and decide to dump Liberty for Google. But the data center boom -- 5,900 MW of requests concentrated in the exact same service territory that feeds Lake Tahoe -- created a capacity environment in which extending a "temporary" wholesale agreement became untenable. The utility is legally obligated to serve bundled customers in its territory. Data centers at TRIC are bundled customers. Liberty is a wholesale customer on a voluntary agreement. When capacity is constrained (and it is -- 0 MW of available import capacity, 15,600 MW in queue), the wholesale customer is the one who gets shed.

This is not a conspiracy. It is a structural outcome of utility obligation hierarchy colliding with unprecedented industrial demand. The regulatory framework produced exactly the result the data center developers needed without anyone having to explicitly choose between Lake Tahoe grandmothers and Google servers.

**For article framing:** The story is not "NV Energy chose data centers over residents." The story is: "the system is designed so that when capacity runs out, voluntary wholesale customers lose to mandatory bundled-service industrial customers, and nobody in the regulatory chain has the authority or mandate to protect the 49,000 people caught in between."
