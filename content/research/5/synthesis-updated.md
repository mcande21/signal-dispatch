### 3. Yield Curve (FRED T10Y2Y)

**Raw data:** 10Y-2Y spread at 0.52% as of March 17, down from 0.72% on January 2. The spread has been compressing steadily: 0.72 in early January, 0.62 in mid-February, 0.55 in early March, 0.52 now. This is a controlled flattening, not an inversion -- but the direction is clear.

**Cross-confirmation:** The NY Fed yield curve model has risen to 25% recession probability (from 18.8% in the existing research). **Direction:** Bearish, with important nuance. The curve is positive (not inverted), which mechanistic models read as "no recession." But the post-inversion normalization-then-flattening pattern is historically the signal that the lagged recession is arriving.

1. **ISM manufacturing expansion (52.4) vs everything else.** This remains the single strongest bullish signal. Two consecutive months of manufacturing expansion after 26 months of contraction. Services PMI at 54.0 is robust. But critically: February ISM data was collected before the oil shock hit $90+. The March PMI (released April 1) is the test. 3. **Prediction markets (31-34%) vs institutional estimates (25-49%).** The 15-point Moody's-Kalshi spread demands explanation. March/April prints will capture the energy and tariff transmission. The lag between when inflation is felt (now) and when it appears in data (April-May) is the window in which policy paralysis deepens.

### Which Signals Are Stronger

The bearish convergence dominates because:

- **The oil shock is exogenous and accelerating.** It does not depend on interpretation or model choice. Oil is at $95-106. Hormuz is closed. These are facts, not forecasts.
- **The labor data is hard, revised, and confirmed.** The -92K payroll print is not a sentiment survey. The benchmark revision cannot be un-revised.
- **The Fed's paralysis is structural, not tactical.** They cannot cut with inflation at 3.1%+ and rising. This is not a judgment call they can change quickly.

The bullish signals are either **lagging** (ISM pre-shock), **artificial** (inventory build via SPR), or **definitional** (prediction market contracts vs broader deterioration).

If PE funds are facing redemptions, we will not see it until it hits bank balance sheets.

2. **CRE extend-and-pretend breaking point.** $936B in maturities are due, but the recognition timing is controlled by banks and regulators. A decision to stop extending could cascade fast -- SVB showed the 48-hour failure timeline. We have no visibility into that decision-making.

3. **China's actual response.** Chinese energy companies resuming Russian oil purchases (Reuters via Telegram) signals opportunistic realignment. But China's response to Section 301 probes and the Trump-Xi summit dynamic is the biggest near-term wildcard for tariff trajectory.

4. **Consumer spending in March.** We have February data (stalling). March data -- the first full month of $4+ gasoline and shutdown anxiety -- will not arrive until late April. The gap between when consumers feel the shock and when we measure it is approximately 6-8 weeks.

5. **The government shutdown's full data impact.** Which releases are delayed? How does the BLS handle March employment data collection during a shutdown? The data vacuum itself may prevent us from seeing deterioration in real time.
