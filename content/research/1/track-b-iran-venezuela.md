# Track B: Iran-Venezuela Bilateral Axis

**Research date:** 2026-03-06
**Classification:** Deep Dive -- Issue 1
**Scope:** Iran-Venezuela bilateral relationship, sanctions evasion infrastructure, and disruption following US intervention

---

## Executive Summary

The Iran-Venezuela bilateral axis -- built over two decades as a mutual sanctions-evasion lifeline -- has been catastrophically disrupted by two events in early 2026: the US military capture of Nicolas Maduro on January 3 (Operation Absolute Resolve), and the US-Israeli strikes on Iran beginning February 28 (Operation Epic Fury / Operation Roaring Lion). This research documents the axis as it existed through late 2025, its rapid unraveling, and the residual infrastructure that may persist or be repurposed.

Iranian investments in Venezuela are estimated at $4.7B-$7.8B, with $2B+ in outstanding debts now likely unrecoverable. The 299 bilateral agreements, the Free Trade Agreement finalized in June 2025, and the 20-year cooperation framework signed in 2022 are all functionally dead. What remains is a case study in how sanctioned states build parallel economic systems -- and how quickly those systems collapse when the political protection disappears.

## 1. Oil and Energy Cooperation

### The Condensate-for-Heavy-Crude Swap

The core of the energy relationship was a barter arrangement: Iran shipped gas condensate (ultra-light crude) to Venezuela, which needed it to dilute its extra-heavy Orinoco Belt crude into exportable grades. In return, Venezuelan fuel oil was loaded onto the same NITC (National Iranian Tanker Company) tankers for delivery to China.

**Volume data:**
- Since 2020: approximately 36 million barrels of Iranian gas condensate delivered to Venezuela (valued at roughly $2.5B)
- 2023 alone: over 12 million barrels of crude oil and gas condensate shipped
- Iran's total crude + condensate exports in 2025: 1.5-1.7 million bpd (6% higher than 2024, 25% above 2023 levels)
- UANI documented 99 vessels that transported both Iranian and Venezuelan oil as of January 2025

### Tanker Fleet and Logistics

Iran operated a shadow fleet estimated at 200-300 vessels. These ships employed systematic deception: AIS transponder spoofing, frequent flag changes, ship-to-ship transfers in open waters, and false documentation. NITC vessels ran the bilateral route -- discharge Iranian condensate in Venezuelan ports, load Venezuelan fuel oil for the return leg to Chinese refineries.

The December 2025 seizure of the Motor Tanker Skipper -- carrying 1.8 million barrels of crude -- demonstrated the scale: a single vessel in this network carried cargo worth approximately $130M at prevailing prices. The DOJ forfeiture action revealed systematic location spoofing and false-flag operations.

### Refinery Infrastructure

Iran provided technical assistance and engineering support for Venezuelan refinery operations, particularly for processing the heavy crude that requires diluent. Iranian engineers maintained a presence at key PDVSA facilities. The relationship was not just trade -- it was infrastructure dependency.

### Current Status (March 2026)

The energy axis is effectively severed. Following Maduro's capture on January 3, US-aligned interim authorities under acting President Delcy Rodriguez have pivoted PDVSA toward Western market engagement. Commodity traders Vitol and Trafigura now lift Venezuelan crude, with proceeds deposited in US Treasury-managed accounts. On January 15, Rodriguez proposed reforms to Venezuela's hydrocarbons law, signaling a fundamental reorientation away from sanctioned-state partnerships.

The US-Israeli strikes on Iran beginning February 28 have further disrupted Iranian export capacity. The dual-pressure squeeze has collapsed the bilateral energy architecture that took a decade to build.

## 2. Military and Defense Cooperation

### Drone Technology Transfer

Venezuela became the first Latin American country to operate armed drones, built on nearly two decades of Iranian UAV technology transfer. The program centered on Iranian Mohajer-series platforms assembled and maintained at facilities within Venezuela.

**Confirmed systems:**
- **Mohajer-6:** Medium-altitude, long-endurance (MALE) drone. 12-hour flight endurance, 200+ km range. Carries up to four precision munitions (Qaem missiles). Electro-optical/infrared sensors, laser rangefinder, target designator. Confirmed operational at El Libertador Air Base in photographs from December 30, 2025.
- **Zamora V-1:** Loitering munition introduced in 2024, modeled on the Iranian Shahed-136 (the same design Russia employed in Ukraine).
- **Orlan-10:** Russian-origin reconnaissance drone, separate supply chain but part of the same defense diversification strategy.

### Manufacturing and Training Infrastructure

El Libertador Air Base housed a drone manufacturing facility where Iranian military personnel supervised assembly, maintenance, and doctrinal integration. Venezuelan personnel were trained to operate advanced models. This was not arms sales -- it was technology transfer with embedded advisory presence.

### OFAC Response (December 30, 2025)

Treasury designated 10 individuals and entities across Venezuela and Iran for weapons proliferation:
- **EANSA** (Empresa Aeronautica Nacional SA) -- Venezuelan firm assembling and maintaining Iranian drones
- **Jose Jesus Urdaneta Gonzalez** -- EANSA chairman
- **Mostafa Rostami Sani, Marco Klinge, Majid Dolatkhah** -- Iran-based procurement agents
- **Rayan Fan Group, Rayan Roshd Afzar** -- Iranian UAV-affiliated companies

These designations came four days before the US military operation that captured Maduro.

### Broader Defense Relationship

The 20-year cooperation agreement signed in June 2022 covered defense alongside energy and industrial sectors. Venezuelan Transport Minister Ramon Celestino Velasquez coordinated military equipment and drone shipments from Iran while planning visits to Tehran. Internal US documents reported by The Washington Post indicated Venezuela was seeking expanded military help from Russia, China, and Iran -- including missile support -- in response to the growing US military buildup in the Caribbean through late 2025.

### Current Status (March 2026)

Iranian military advisors and IRGC-affiliated personnel at Venezuelan bases are displaced. The manufacturing infrastructure at El Libertador is under de facto US control or oversight. The drone program's future depends entirely on the post-Maduro government's orientation, which currently trends strongly toward US alignment.

## 3. Sanctions Evasion Networks

### Maritime Evasion Architecture

The Iran-Venezuela sanctions evasion system was among the most sophisticated globally. Key mechanisms:

**Ship-to-ship (STS) transfers:** Cargo transfers between vessels on the open ocean, outside territorial waters, to obscure origin and destination. The Skipper seizure revealed multi-layered STS chains where crude changed hands multiple times before reaching its final destination.

**AIS manipulation:** Systematic transponder spoofing -- vessels broadcasting false positions while conducting sanctioned trade. Frequent flag-state changes to evade jurisdiction.

**Shadow fleet scale:** By October 2025, analytics firm Kpler reported that over 60% of vessels loading Iranian crude in the previous 12 months had been sanctioned by OFAC (up from 38% one year prior). The US sanctioned 29 additional vessels in a December 2025 crackdown on the shadow oil fleet.

### Cryptocurrency and Financial Architecture

The financial layer evolved significantly in 2024-2025:

**PDVSA USDT payments:** By Q1 2024, PDVSA required new oil spot-deal clients to use digital wallets and pay in Tether's USDT. By mid-2025, over 50% of shipments were settled in USDT. By late 2025, an estimated 80% of PDVSA oil revenue was collected via USDT.

**Iranian IRGC crypto operations:** The IRGC moved over $1B in stablecoins through two UK-registered front companies (Zedcex and Zedxion), linked to Babak Zanjani -- one of Iran's most notorious sanctions evasion financiers. The Central Bank of Iran acquired at least $507M in USDT to support the rial and settle international trade.

**Tether enforcement:** Tether blacklisted approximately $3.3B in funds between 2023-late 2025. In January 2026, Tether froze $182M in USDT in its largest single asset freeze, targeting Venezuelan-linked wallets.

### Front Companies and Third-Party Intermediaries

The bilateral network employed layered corporate structures across multiple jurisdictions. OFAC enforcement actions in 2025 included:
- **Fracht** (Texas-based freight forwarder): $1.6M settlement for violations involving Venezuela and Iran sanctions related to car parts transportation
- **Unicat** (Texas-based catalyst seller): $3.9M settlement for Iranian and Venezuelan sanctions violations

These cases illustrate how deeply the evasion network penetrated legitimate commercial infrastructure.

### Current Status (March 2026)

The maritime evasion network is severely degraded. The US maritime blockade of sanctioned tankers servicing Venezuela (five tranches of designations in December 2025 alone), Maduro's capture, and the pivot of PDVSA toward US-managed oil sales have collapsed the primary trade route. The cryptocurrency channel -- while more resilient than physical infrastructure -- faces increasing enforcement from both Tether and US authorities.

The residual risk is that the financial expertise and corporate shell networks persist beyond the bilateral relationship. These capabilities are transferable and may be repurposed for other sanctioned trade flows, particularly any remaining Iran-China routes.

## 4. Diplomatic Timeline (2023-2026)

### 2022 (Foundation)
- **June 2022:** Iran and Venezuela signed 20-year strategic cooperation agreement covering energy, healthcare, science, technology, transportation, and education during Maduro's visit to Tehran.

### 2023-2024 (Deepening)
- **February 2024:** Iranian and Venezuelan officials held energy and trade discussions, expanding cooperation frameworks
- **October 2024:** Maduro met Pezeshkian on sidelines of BRICS summit in Kazan, Russia -- signaling continued alignment within the broader anti-Western bloc
- **November 2024:** Iran's Defense Minister Nasirzadeh traveled to Caracas for 10th Joint Economic Cooperation Commission. Multiple cooperation documents signed, including visa exemption MOU and AI technology transfer pact. Mining sector investment agreements for steel, cement, gold.
- **By end 2024:** 289 active bilateral projects; 299 total sectoral agreements

### 2025 (Peak and Collapse)
- **June 2025:** Free Trade Agreement finalized -- eliminated tariffs on agricultural, fishery, and manufacturing products. Revived a dormant 2004 trade pact.
- **September 2025:** US Southern Command began striking vessels in the Caribbean, alleging drug trafficking on behalf of Venezuela
- **November 2025:** Washington Post reported Venezuela seeking expanded military aid from Iran, Russia, China in response to US Caribbean buildup. Maria Corina Machado promised to open Venezuela's oil/gas reserves at Miami business meeting attended by Trump.
- **December 2025 (critical month):**
  - US seized Motor Tanker Skipper with 1.8M barrels
  - US sanctioned 29 shadow fleet vessels
  - Mohajer-6 confirmed at El Libertador Air Base (Dec 30)
  - OFAC designated 10 Iran-Venezuela weapons trade entities (Dec 30)
  - Pezeshkian called Maduro: "Venezuela is a true friend and ally of the Islamic Republic"
  - US struck first land target in Venezuela (Dec 26) -- marine facility
  - Iranian government directed media to avoid narratives critical of Venezuela

### 2026 (Destruction)
- **January 3, 2026:** Operation Absolute Resolve -- US captures Maduro and Cilia Flores. Over 150 aircraft from 20 bases. Estimated 80 casualties.
- **January 4, 2026:** Iran condemned US strikes, expressed support for Maduro
- **January 5, 2026:** Maduro and Flores arraigned in Manhattan federal court
- **January 15, 2026:** Acting President Delcy Rodriguez proposed hydrocarbons law reforms
- **February 28, 2026:** US-Israeli strikes on Iran begin (Operation Epic Fury / Roaring Lion). Approximately 2,000 strikes by March 1. Supreme Leader Khamenei killed.
- **March 2026:** PDVSA pledges "reliable" oil supplies to US market amid Iran war

The bilateral axis that took 20 years to build was destroyed in 60 days.

## 5. Economic Dependencies Beyond Oil

### Construction and Housing
Iran invested in low-income housing developments in Venezuela, cement plants, and automobile manufacturing facilities. These were structured as barter or deferred-payment arrangements rather than conventional investment, reflecting both countries' dollar-shortage constraints.

### Mining
Discussions between Iran's Defense Minister and Venezuela's Minister of Mines covered investments in steel, cement, gold, and mineral extraction. The mining cooperation exploited Venezuela's Arco Minero (Mining Arc) in Bolivar state -- a resource-rich region also linked to environmental and human rights controversies.

### Technology and AI
The November 2024 agreements included specific provisions for AI technology transfer and training of Venezuelan personnel. This represented a newer dimension of the relationship, moving beyond traditional energy and military domains.

### Power Infrastructure
Iran provided maintenance and equipment supply for Venezuelan power plants. Iran's domestic experience with sanctions-era infrastructure maintenance was directly applicable to Venezuela's chronically failing electrical grid.

### Financial Architecture
The two countries discussed establishment of a joint development bank -- an institution designed to facilitate bilateral trade outside the dollar system. This never reached operational status.

### Scale of Integration
Iranian exports to Venezuela reached approximately $40M in 2024. While modest in absolute terms, this understates the relationship's value: much of the economic activity operated through barter, deferred payment, and off-books arrangements that conventional trade statistics do not capture. The leaked document figure of $4.7B in Iran-linked Venezuelan projects is a better measure of total economic entanglement.

### Current Status (March 2026)
Iranian investments of $4.7B-$7.8B face probable total loss. Fixed assets (refineries, manufacturing facilities) are vulnerable to seizure or nationalization by post-Maduro authorities. Outstanding debts exceeding $2B are likely unrecoverable given Iran's own sanctioned status and the priority of Western creditors in any debt-restructuring process. Iran's Chamber of Commerce chief indicated that funds had already been withdrawn where possible, but physical infrastructure and contractual claims remain stranded.

## 7. Signal Interpretation Framework

### Ghost Market Data Sources -- Analytical Lens for Iran-Venezuela Axis

**OONI (Open Observatory of Network Interference)**
OONI measures internet censorship through volunteer-run probes, tracking measurement count per hour, test diversity, and connectivity disruptions in Iran. For the Iran-Venezuela axis, OONI is a leading indicator of regime stability and internal security operations in Iran. A measurement count drop below 200/hr (baseline: ~1,400/hr) historically precedes significant regime action within 48 hours. In the current context -- post-February 2026 strikes -- OONI data reveals whether Iranian domestic internet infrastructure is functioning, which directly indicates the regime's capacity to coordinate any remaining overseas operations, including residual Venezuela-linked activities. Signal threshold: sustained drop below 500/hr for 6+ hours = significant disruption. Recovery above 1,000/hr = reconstitution of control infrastructure.

**Bonbast (Iranian Rial Black Market Rate)**
Bonbast tracks the unofficial rial-to-dollar exchange rate, reflecting real economic sentiment and capital flight pressure in Iran. The rial spread (gap between official and black market rates) is a direct measure of sanctions pressure and economic stress. For the Venezuela axis, rial depreciation correlates with increased urgency to secure alternative revenue streams -- historically, this drove acceleration in Venezuelan oil barter arrangements. With that avenue now severed, rial stress post-Maduro and post-strikes indicates the degree to which Iran is economically cornered. Signal threshold: spread widening beyond 25% (baseline: 8-12%) = severe stress; narrowing below 10% = stabilization or capital controls taking effect. A sustained spread above 30% in the current environment would indicate that loss of the Venezuelan revenue channel is compounding with strike damage.

**TEDPIX (Tehran Stock Exchange Index)**
TEDPIX captures Iranian domestic economic sentiment and institutional investor behavior. Volume spikes indicate panic or regime-directed market intervention. For the Venezuela axis, TEDPIX anomalies following Maduro's capture would reflect Iranian market assessment of economic losses from the collapsed bilateral relationship. The $4.7B-$7.8B investment exposure in Venezuela is material relative to Iran's GDP. Signal threshold: volume spike above 200% of 30-day average = significant market stress; sustained index decline above 5% in a week = structural reassessment of economic outlook. In the current March 2026 context, TEDPIX behavior reflects the compounded impact of Venezuela losses, strike damage, and sanctions tightening.

**Cloudflare Radar (Traffic Anomaly Detection)**
Cloudflare Radar tracks internet traffic patterns by country, detecting disruptions, rerouting, and anomalous traffic flows. For Iran, it serves as confirmation/complement to OONI data -- where OONI measures censorship, Cloudflare measures actual traffic volumes. For the Venezuela axis specifically, Cloudflare data for Venezuelan traffic patterns can indicate whether residual Iranian-linked communication infrastructure (VPNs, encrypted channels used by IRGC advisors and business operatives) is still functioning. Cross-referencing Iranian and Venezuelan traffic anomalies can reveal coordinated communication disruptions. Signal threshold: traffic drop exceeding 30% from baseline for either country = major disruption; simultaneous anomalies in both countries would indicate coordinated action or shared infrastructure failure.

**OFAC (SDN List Changes)**
OFAC SDN list updates are the most direct signal of US policy escalation or de-escalation against the Iran-Venezuela network. Each designation wave has a targeting pattern -- vessels, individuals, entities, sectors -- that reveals enforcement priorities. The December 2025 wave (29 shadow fleet vessels + 10 weapons-trade designations) preceded the January intervention by days. For forward-looking analysis, new OFAC designations targeting Venezuelan entities or residual Iran-linked infrastructure would signal continued enforcement, while absence of new designations might indicate the US considers the axis sufficiently disrupted. Signal threshold: any batch of 5+ designations targeting Iran-Venezuela entities = active enforcement campaign; individual designations = routine maintenance. Designations targeting cryptocurrency wallets or digital asset facilitators = escalation into the financial layer.

**USAspending (Federal Procurement Data)**
USAspending tracks federal contract awards and spending patterns. For the Venezuela axis, relevant signals include: DoD contracts related to Caribbean operations and Southern Command buildup; State Department/USAID contracts for Venezuelan transition governance; Treasury/FinCEN contracts for sanctions enforcement technology. Procurement pattern analysis reveals where the US government is investing enforcement resources, which indicates strategic priorities. Signal threshold: new DoD contract awards exceeding $50M for Caribbean theater = sustained military posture; Treasury/FinCEN technology procurement = enhanced sanctions enforcement capability. Spikes in Venezuela-related procurement indicate the depth of US commitment to the post-Maduro transition.

**EIA (US Petroleum Data)**
EIA data on US petroleum inventories, imports, exports, and Strategic Petroleum Reserve levels provides the macro energy context for the Iran-Venezuela axis. With both Iranian and Venezuelan supply now disrupted simultaneously, EIA data reveals the actual supply impact. Key metrics: Venezuelan crude import volumes to the US (historically near zero under sanctions, now potentially increasing under post-Maduro PDVSA arrangements); changes in SPR drawdown pace (indicating whether disruption is creating domestic supply pressure); import composition shifts (if Venezuelan heavy crude replaces Iranian-origin barrels in the global supply chain). Signal threshold: Venezuelan crude imports to US above 100,000 bpd = successful PDVSA reorientation; SPR drawdowns exceeding 1M bbl/week = supply stress from dual disruption; significant shifts in import source composition = market restructuring in response to collapsed sanctions-evasion trade routes.

## Key Analytical Judgments

1. **The Iran-Venezuela axis is functionally destroyed.** Twenty years of bilateral infrastructure collapsed in 60 days (January-February 2026). The political protection that sustained the relationship -- Maduro's regime -- no longer exists.

2. **Iranian financial exposure is catastrophic.** The $4.7B-$7.8B in Venezuelan investments, combined with $2B+ in unrecoverable debts, represents a significant portion of Iran's overseas financial capital. This loss compounds the fall of Assad in Syria and the weakening of Hezbollah in Lebanon -- Iran's alliance network is in rapid contraction.

3. **The sanctions evasion playbook is exposed but not destroyed.** Ship-to-ship transfer techniques, AIS spoofing methods, cryptocurrency payment channels (particularly USDT), and front-company architectures are transferable capabilities. The personnel and expertise that built the Iran-Venezuela network can be redeployed to other routes, particularly Iran-China.

4. **The cryptocurrency dimension is the most resilient layer.** Physical infrastructure (tankers, refineries, air bases) is capturable. Corporate shells are designatable. But stablecoin flows are harder to interdict permanently. PDVSA's pivot to 80% USDT settlement and Iran's $507M+ CBI USDT holdings demonstrate that crypto has become a structural feature of sanctioned-state finance, not a marginal evasion tactic.

5. **The drone technology transfer leaves lasting capability.** Even with Iranian advisors displaced, Venezuela retains assembled Mohajer-6 systems, Zamora V-1 loitering munitions, and -- critically -- the institutional knowledge from years of Iranian training. Whether a post-Maduro government retains, repurposes, or surrenders this capability is an open question with significant regional security implications.

6. **The dual-pressure strategy is the analytical key for China-Taiwan implications.** The US demonstrated willingness and capability to simultaneously dismantle both the Venezuelan regime and strike Iran. This is a single strategic design, not two separate policies. The implications for China's calculus regarding Taiwan are the subject of Track A.
