The DHS Machine: Trade Coercion, Industrial Detention, and the Procurement Network That Brought Down Kristi Noem

March 2026

Classification: Open Source Intelligence Topic: DHS under Noem: Trade coercion infrastructure, detention expansion, procurement corruption Analyst confidence: Medium (data quality high; integration thesis contested)

Executive Summary

In February 2026, the Department of Homeland Security fired its own Secretary. The official reason was procurement irregularities – $220 million in advertising contracts routed through firms with no federal contracting history, at least one incorporated eight days before receiving a $143 million award. But the procurement scandal was the surface. Underneath it, three partially integrated tracks of DHS activity had been running simultaneously, and the data tells a larger story than any single track suggests.

The first track is trade coercion infrastructure. Comtrade data shows the United States systematically leveraged extreme export dependencies – Guatemala sends 91.8% of its apparel exports to the US market, Colombia routes 38% of its petroleum through American buyers – to compel receiving-country agreements for deportation flights. A 2-month action-reward cycle is visible in the timeline: tariff threats in January 2025, capitulation by February, punitive tariffs in April, negotiated agreements by June, operational deportation flights by October, tariff removal in November. This is not ad-hoc diplomacy. It is an operationalized policy cadence.

The second track is industrial-scale detention infrastructure. Beyond the $3.78 billion in FY2025 operational spending documented through USAspending, a $38.3 billion multi-year authorization for detention expansion was embedded in the “One Big Beautiful Bill Act” – funding eight mega-centers with 10,000 beds each, sixteen regional processing sites, and ten turnkey centers for a total of 92,600 beds. The combined figure – $42.1 billion in enforcement and infrastructure – reframes the scale from aggressive operations to industrial detention architecture with multi-year commitment.

The third track is procurement corruption. Corey Lewandowski, operating as a “special government employee” with contract approval authority, served as the hub connecting political operatives to sole-source contracts awarded under emergency authority. The $99,999 contract structuring – one dollar below the threshold requiring additional oversight – and the personal relationship between Noem and Lewandowski created a bypass channel for spending approvals that circumvented standard procedural safeguards.

I assess these tracks as partially integrated rather than a single coordinated operation. Trade coercion was Executive and State Department machinery, not DHS-specific. The procurement corruption was enabled by personal access networks that died with Noem’s firing. The detention infrastructure has bureaucratic inertia that will outlast any individual secretary. The connections between tracks are real – trade coercion enables enforcement scale, enforcement justifies infrastructure, infrastructure creates corruption opportunities – but they are looser than a unified conspiracy narrative would suggest. The machine was built by multiple hands for overlapping purposes, not designed from a single blueprint.

What matters now: the machine survives its operator. Mullin inherits the trade coercion toolkit, the detention infrastructure authorization, and the enforcement apparatus. What he does not inherit is the personal bypass channel for procurement corruption. I assess 55% probability that enforcement operations continue within 20% of Noem-era levels through Q3 2026, driven by infrastructure inertia offset by investigation-driven political constraints.

Structured Data

Five open-source intelligence feeds inform this assessment. Each is described with its measurement methodology, current reading, baseline context, and analytical significance.

Comtrade: Trade Dependency as Coercion Leverage

UN Comtrade trade statistics quantify the economic pressure available to the United States against deportation receiving countries. The numbers are stark.

Guatemala’s apparel sector is the clearest case. HS 6109 (T-shirts and singlets) alone shows $421.8 million in total Guatemalan exports, of which the United States purchases 91.8% – $387.2 million, 57.2 million units (Comtrade, 2024 annual data, queried 2026-03-06). This is not trade partnership. This is existential dependency. A tariff threat against Guatemala’s garment sector is not a negotiating position; it is an economic annihilation threat. Historical trade diversification data shows single-market dependency above 70% for manufactured goods is rare. Guatemala’s 91.8% is extreme concentration, almost certainly reflecting deliberate supply chain architecture under CAFTA-DR – the 91.8% concentration is anomalous absent structural explanation.1

Coffee exports tell a similar story at lower intensity. Guatemala ships $917.3 million in coffee globally, with the US taking 41.6% ($381.7 million). Honduras: $1.28 billion total, US share 31.2% ($399.7 million). El Salvador: $137.1 million total, US share 43.1% ($59.1 million). These dependencies are material – 31-43% of export revenue – but not existential – alternative buyers exist. The apparel dependency is the binding constraint.

Colombia’s petroleum trade validated the coercion mechanism in real time. At $12.1 billion in total petroleum exports with the US purchasing 38% ($4.6 billion), Colombia had enough dependency to feel tariff pressure but enough diversification to negotiate. When Colombia turned away two US military deportation flights in January 2025, Trump’s immediate tariff threat produced a reversal within hours – Colombia’s foreign minister sent a military plane to San Diego to collect the deportees. This episode is better understood as coercion-enabled diplomatic resolution than pure economic annihilation. Colombia had options; it chose capitulation because the cost-benefit calculation favored compliance, not because alternatives didn’t exist.2

Remittance flows add a second pressure vector. Guatemala received $21.5 billion in remittances in the most recent reporting period, representing 19% of GDP. Deportations threaten this flow – deportees cannot send money home. Combined with export dependency, this creates dual leverage: tariffs on goods plus deportation-driven remittance reduction. I assess this mechanism as theoretically sound but empirically unvalidated. Deportation volumes affect less than 1% of the population, deportees may re-migrate through circular migration patterns, and no data confirms that aggregate remittance flows actually dropped. The leverage is theoretical until World Bank quarterly remittance data for Q1 2026 becomes available in April or May.3

USAspending: The Scale Story

Federal procurement data documents three layers of DHS spending under Noem’s tenure.

Layer 1: Advertising contracts ($220 million). The procurement corruption that ended Noem’s tenure. Three firms with no federal contracting history received sole-source awards under emergency authority: Safe America Media, People Who Think LLC (incorporated eight days before receiving $143 million), and Strategy Group. Contract structuring at $99,999 is documented across multiple awards. This is the smallest layer by dollar value and the one that generated the scandal.

Layer 2: Operational enforcement ($3.78 billion FY2025). Deportation flights, detention facility contracts, ICE operational spending. This is documented, largely uncontested, and represents the running cost of the enforcement apparatus. It includes the flights, the facilities, the personnel, and the logistics of processing 600,000 claimed removals in 2025.

Layer 3: Infrastructure authorization ($38.3 billion multi-year). Authorized through the “One Big Beautiful Bill Act” for detention infrastructure expansion: eight mega-centers with 10,000 beds each, sixteen regional processing sites, ten turnkey centers, 92,600 total beds. This is the number that changes the narrative from aggressive operations to industrial-scale detention architecture.

A critical distinction: multi-year authorization is not current-year appropriation. If FY2026 appropriation is $4-5 billion against FY2025’s $3.78 billion, the actual year-over-year spending increase is 15-30%, not the 10x figure that the $38.3 billion headline implies. The authorization ceiling matters for long-term trajectory planning – it signals political commitment to sustained detention capacity – but it does not mean $38.3 billion is being spent this year. The combined $42.1 billion figure ($3.78B + $38.3B) represents total enforcement plus infrastructure commitment across the authorization window, not annual spending.4

Data gap: USAspending API returned 400 errors on Q2 2026 queries. This data is not yet available and is expected in July 2026. Contract term structure – quarterly versus annual – remains unknown, and this gap significantly affects the advertising spending continuation forecast.

dolarVzla: Venezuelan Economic Context

Venezuela’s parallel exchange rate provides context for one deportation destination but does not drive the coercion analysis.

Official rate: 433.17 VES/USD. Parallel rate: 613.95 VES/USD. Premium spread: 42%. (dolarVzla, 2026-03-06) A parallel premium above 30% signals severe capital flight pressure and low confidence in the official exchange rate.

Currency controls continue generating 42% arbitrage spreads. Economic pressure driving emigration has not eased despite the existing sanctions regime. Venezuela received over 13,000 deportees in 2025. However, Venezuela does not fit the trade coercion model – it is already heavily sanctioned, and US leverage appears diplomatic and sanctions-based rather than trade-dependent. I include this for completeness, not because it changes the assessment.

Qualitative Intelligence

The Trade Coercion Timeline

The chronological sequence reveals an operationalized policy cadence, not ad-hoc diplomatic responses. I am walking through this in detail because the pattern is the finding.

January 2025: Trump announces tariff threats, visa restrictions, and sanctions against countries refusing deportation flights. This is the opening position – maximum pressure, broad scope.

Late January 2025: Colombia turns away two US military deportation flights. Trump threatens tariffs immediately. Within hours, Colombian Foreign Minister Murillo reverses the decision and sends a military plane to San Diego to collect the deportees. The speed of reversal – hours, not days – indicates pre-negotiated fallback positions rather than pure coercion. Colombia had diplomatic channels; it chose compliance over confrontation. The 38% petroleum dependency made the tariff threat credible, but Colombia’s export diversification meant this was pressure, not annihilation.

February 2025: Guatemala capitulates. Agrees to a 40% increase in deportation flight acceptance and begins receiving third-country nationals – non-Guatemalans deported through Guatemala. Secretary Rubio negotiates parallel deals with El Salvador and Guatemala. At 91.8% apparel export dependency, Guatemala’s negotiating position is qualitatively different from Colombia’s. There is no fallback market for $387 million in T-shirt exports.

March 2025: Honduras signs an Asylum Cooperative Agreement. Venezuelan deportation flights resume after a suspension period.

April 2025: Punitive tariffs imposed – 10% on Central American goods broadly, 18% on Nicaragua specifically. This is the punishment phase: agreements have been signed, but tariffs are applied to reinforce the power dynamic.

June 2025: Noem personally negotiates safe third country agreements in Central America. The Honduras agreement is amended retroactively to apply to non-Honduran migrants – an expansion to multilateral scope from the March baseline. Honduras’s immigration director initially denied signing the amended agreement, suggesting coercion rather than voluntary partnership. This denial was later walked back.

October 2025: First third-country removal flight arrives in Guatemala carrying 56 Guatemalans and 3 Hondurans. The system is now operational end-to-end.

November 2025: US removes tariffs on some products from Guatemala and El Salvador. The reward phase: compliance is compensated. The 10-month arc from threat to reward is complete.

December 2025: ICE reports 600,000 deportations through December, later revised to 675,000 for the full calendar year. The Northern Triangle received over 50% of May 2025 deportations.

The pattern – threat, capitulation, punishment, agreement, implementation, reward – repeats on approximately a 2-month action-reward cycle. This is not improvisation. It is operationalized policy with a visible cadence. I assess that this toolkit is Executive and State Department machinery, not DHS-specific. Mullin inherits the enforcement end but does not control the trade coercion lever. That distinction matters for forecasting enforcement continuity.

The Procurement Corruption Network

The procurement corruption that ended Noem’s tenure operates through a documented chain: emergency authority declarations enable sole-source contracting, bypassing competitive bidding and routing awards to political operatives with no federal contracting history.

Three firms received the bulk of advertising contracts:

Safe America Media received contracts for DHS advertising campaigns. The firm’s principals include individuals connected to Republican political operations with no prior federal contracting record.

People Who Think LLC was incorporated eight days before receiving a $143 million contract. The timeline alone – incorporation to nine-figure federal award in eight days – indicates the entity was created specifically to receive the contract. Standard federal contracting requires registration, capability demonstration, and competitive evaluation. Emergency authority bypassed all of these requirements.

Strategy Group received additional advertising contracts. Senate investigators Welch and Blumenthal sent letters to all three firms demanding documentation.

Corey Lewandowski operated as the hub. Officially a “special government employee,” he held contract approval authority that contradicted Noem’s sworn congressional testimony. ProPublica documented this contradiction: Noem testified that Lewandowski did not approve contracts; operational documents show he did. This discrepancy is the basis for the perjury referral Senator Blumenthal demanded on March 6, 2026.

The $99,999 structuring pattern – awards set one dollar below enhanced oversight thresholds – appears across multiple contracts. Federal procurement analysts recognize this as deliberate threshold avoidance.

Investigation and Fall

The investigative exposure followed a compression pattern: years of operation, weeks of exposure, days to termination.

ProPublica published the foundational reporting documenting contradictions between Noem’s testimony and Lewandowski’s operational role. The Wall Street Journal reported on the personal relationship between Noem and Lewandowski in February 2026, describing that “they do little to hide their relationship inside the department.” Both deny the characterization. Noem called it a “disgusting lie” during congressional testimony. Representative Kamlager-Dove confronted Noem directly in a House Judiciary Committee hearing.

The personal relationship is relevant as scandal context, not as a legal element. The corruption case rests on emergency authority abuse, sole-source contracting to unqualified firms, and threshold structuring. These are documentable, prosecutable facts. Whether Noem and Lewandowski were personally involved changes public perception but not the legal exposure. Perjury, if provable, would require demonstrating willful falsehood rather than definitional dispute – a high bar given that both parties deny the characterization.5

The Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations is actively investigating. Blumenthal’s perjury referral was sent March 6, 2026. No DOJ or Inspector General reports have been published as of this writing. No grand jury empanelment has been reported.

DHS fired Noem in February 2026 and simultaneously announced Mullin as replacement. The simultaneity suggests leadership planned the transition, not reacted to events – they arranged succession before executing termination.

Threat/Opportunity Assessment

The question is not what Noem did. That story is largely documented. The question is what survives her departure.

What survives:

Detention infrastructure. The $38.3 billion authorization has bureaucratic inertia. Contracts have been signed, construction has been planned, bed capacity expansion is underway. New DHS leadership does not typically cancel predecessor infrastructure programs – the political cost of appearing “soft on enforcement” outweighs the governance benefit of reassessing procurement. I assess 70-80% probability that infrastructure expansion continues at or near planned levels, based on bureaucratic inertia and political incentives against cancellation.

Trade coercion toolkit. Tariff threats, visa restrictions, and safe third country agreements are Executive and State Department machinery. They operated through Trump, Rubio, and interagency coordination, not through DHS alone. Noem’s personal role in June 2025 Central American negotiations was execution, not design. The toolkit is inherited infrastructure.

Enforcement operations. ICE operational tempo has institutional momentum. Field offices, regional priorities, and deportation flight logistics do not change with a secretary’s departure. However, removal volume is politically contested in a way that infrastructure is not. Congressional pressure, litigation, and public attention create friction that infrastructure spending does not face.

What dies with Noem:

The personal bypass channel. Lewandowski’s contract approval authority, the personal relationship enabling informal approvals, the specific network of political operatives receiving sole-source awards – this is the mechanism that required Noem specifically. Mullin has no documented connection to McLaughlin, Yoho, or the Safe America Media network. The procurement corruption was enabled by personal capture of emergency contracting authority. Without that specific capture, the bypass channel closes.

Political cover for aggressive spending. The investigation creates a chilling effect on future emergency authority use. Even if Mullin wanted to route contracts through political networks (no evidence suggests this), the precedent of Noem’s firing makes such behavior higher-risk for any successor.

The net assessment: Mullin inherits the machine but not the scandal. Enforcement operations continue with moderate probability. Infrastructure expansion continues with high probability. Procurement corruption through the specific Noem-Lewandowski channel stops. The policy apparatus is larger than any individual secretary – which is precisely what makes the $38.3 billion authorization significant. It is designed to outlast political cycles.
