## Executive Summary

The SAVE Act will not become law. I assess **3%** probability of passage — nine votes short of cloture, no reconciliation pathway, no prediction market lists a contract.

The system the SAVE Act describes is already operational.

The Department of Justice has demanded voter rolls from 44+ states, suing 24 that refused. Twelve states handed over complete, unredacted data. DHS confirmed it cross-references this data for immigration enforcement. In October 2025, DHS retroactively formalized changes to the SAVE database — adding U.S. citizens to its scope, integrating Social Security records, expanding data sharing — five months after implementation, in violation of Privacy Act notice requirements.

Section 8(j)(5)(D) of the SAVE Act mandates that DHS "shall investigate for removal proceedings" when voter verification flags a noncitizen. No data firewall. No restrictions on secondary use. Voter registration becomes an input to the deportation pipeline.

The behavioral signal is already measurable: 31% of naturalized citizens now fear detention or deportation, up from 12% in 2023. Academic evidence shows immigration enforcement reduces voter registration propensity by 5% among citizens in mixed-status households. The documentation barrier affects 21.3 million citizens — but it cuts across both parties. The fear channel does not.

This piece examines the pipeline, the data, and what it means for 2026.
