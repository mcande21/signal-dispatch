## Signal Convergence

Four independent tracks are pointing the same direction — this is not a case of one bad indicator:

**1. Soft → Hard data transition is underway.** Consumer confidence collapsed to 65.1 on the Conference Board Expectations Index (every sustained sub-80 read has historically preceded recession within 12 months). January retail sales confirmed the sentiment signal in hard spending data. The SF Fed documents savings depletion. Upper-income consumers, the last backstop, began pulling back in January per Bain. The cycle from confidence → spending contraction has completed its first full turn.

**2. Labor market is cracking at the headline level.** February payrolls came in at -92,000 — the worst monthly print since the pandemic. January layoffs were the highest to start a year since 2009 per Challenger. Initial jobless claims hit 231,000. The Sahm Rule, designed to detect recessions in real time, has triggered at 4.4% unemployment. Goldman Sachs revised its recession probability upward immediately after the February employment release, citing it explicitly as the catalyst.

**3. The Fed is caught in a stagflation trap.** Q1 GDP tracking at 0.7% while core PCE sits at 3.1% — simultaneously below trend on growth and above target on inflation. The Fed's own 2.3% GDP forecast for 2026 is already contradicted by Q1 actuals. The December 2024 QT reversal was a distress signal, not a policy choice. The Sahm Rule trigger at 4.4% unemployment is what the Fed projected as its *optimistic* scenario. Chair transition in May 2026 arrives at the worst possible moment.

**4. Tariff shock is amplifying every existing vulnerability.** Goldman models +1.5-2ppt core inflation from tariffs. Tax Foundation calculates $1,500/household burden. China's 84% retaliatory tariffs mirror the 2018-2019 structure that cost US agriculture $27B. Corporate bankruptcies are at their highest rate since 2010 — margin compression from import costs meeting demand softening. RSM, EY, Stanford SIEPR, and JPMorgan have independently converged on "stagflation lite" framing. Multi-institutional consensus from different analytical traditions.

**Cross-cutting transmission chain:** Tariff shock → import cost inflation → Fed constrained on cuts → monetary tightening lag still active (2022-2023 hikes, fastest 525bp cycle in 40 years) → investment freeze → labor deterioration (February payrolls) → consumer confidence collapse → hard spending contraction → earnings pressure → credit stress. The chain is sequentially engaged.

## Signal Divergence

The bull case is not dead — three meaningful divergences require honest accounting:

**1. ISM manufacturing broke a 26-month contraction.** This is the single most important bullish data point. The LEI normally leads PMI recovery; here PMI is recovering while LEI keeps falling. This is the central unresolved tension. Either the LEI is capturing a policy-shock response the PMI hasn't yet felt, or the PMI recovery signals underlying demand resilience that the LEI's soft components are overstating.

**2. Industrial production is positive on both MoM and YoY.** Hard output data still growing, even as leading indicators deteriorate. Capacity utilization is 2.6pp below long-run average — slack in the system, but not contraction.

**3. OECD CLI above 100, yield curve normalized.** Both mechanistic indicators point toward moderate growth, not contraction. The OECD CLI has a meaningful track record. The yield curve's normalization (from deeply inverted) would historically signal the lagged recession risk is dissipating.

**4. Prediction markets (29-36%) sit well below institutional estimates (40-45%).** The NY Fed's yield curve model is at 18.8%. Either: (a) markets and model are correctly pricing a soft landing that judgment-based forecasters are missing due to narrative momentum, or (b) markets are systematically underpricing a structural break that doesn't fit historical model parameters. The 2022-2025 tariff/pandemic policy shock era arguably falls into category (b).

**5. Wall Street consensus remains uniformly bullish on equities, with S&P 500 EPS consensus intact.** This is either: rational forward discounting that macro fears will resolve, or the last stage of denial before earnings revision cascades. Historical pattern strongly favors the latter when Sahm Rule is triggered alongside 0.7% GDP.

## Preliminary Assessment

### Bear Case — Recession by Q3/Q4 2026
**Probability: 40%**

The Sahm Rule trigger is not noise. It was designed by a former Fed economist specifically to identify recessions in real time with minimal false positives. Combined with -92,000 February payrolls, 0.7% Q1 GDP, and consumer confidence at 65.1, the historical pattern is clear: every time this cluster has appeared, recession followed within 1-2 quarters.

The transmission mechanism is already engaged. The tariff shock amplifies it in a way classical models underweight — the $1,500/household burden hits simultaneously with credit stress (auto repossessions at Great Recession levels) and savings depletion. The Fed cannot respond at speed: PCE at 3.1% with tariff amplification incoming constrains aggressive cuts. The May chair transition creates a policy vacuum at the worst possible moment.

Historical parallel from Worker 2 is apt: the 2004-2006 rate cycle (525bp, 24 months) preceded the December 2007 recession by approximately the same elapsed time we are now post-peak.

**What confirms it:** March/April payrolls sustain negative trend; Conference Board LEI continues declining despite ISM recovery; tariff escalation beyond current levels; PCE acceleration above 3.5%.

### Base Case — Stagflation Lite / Growth Recession
**Probability: 45%**

The US avoids a technical recession (two consecutive quarters of negative GDP) but experiences a growth recession: unemployment rises to 5.0-5.5%, GDP stays 0.5-1.5%, consumer spending flatlines, earnings revisions cascade. This is the "controlled descent" framing from Worker 3 — not freefall, but structural deterioration.

The ISM recovery is the linchpin here. If manufacturing demand genuinely stabilized and the PMI reading is leading rather than lagging, the hard output data may prevent full contraction even as consumers retrench. The OECD CLI above 100 and normalized yield curve provide corroborating support.

The stagflation trap, in this scenario, produces Fed paralysis rather than recession: they hold rates, inflation stays elevated, growth stays weak, but no collapse. This is politically damaging and economically painful — and it's the conditions under which a second shock (geopolitical, financial) would tip to the bear case.

**What confirms it:** ISM sustains recovery through Q2; unemployment stabilizes 4.5-5.0%; PCE stays 2.5-3.5%; tariff negotiations produce partial de-escalation.

### Bull Case — Soft Landing Preserved
**Probability: 15%**

The traditional recession signal constellation is "not fully illuminated" per Worker 0's formulation. ISM recovery, positive IP, OECD CLI above 100, and normalized yield curve are not the data profile of a pre-recession economy. Prediction markets at 29-36% and the NY Fed model at 18.8% represent a real analytical tradition, not just denial.

The bull case requires: the February payroll print was weather/seasonal noise (subsequent months recover to +100K+); tariff policy produces a negotiated settlement or exemptions that reduce the inflation impact; the Fed's December QT reversal was precautionary, not distress; and the ISM recovery represents genuine demand stabilization.

This scenario is not implausible — it's just that the weight of evidence doesn't favor it, and the asymmetry of risks (tariff amplification, Fed constraints, Sahm trigger) creates a downside that is much larger than the upside of the bull case.

**What confirms it:** March/April payrolls strongly positive; LEI turns; tariff pause or rollback; PCE decelerates below 3%.
