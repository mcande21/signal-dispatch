# Public Reader Review -- SD #12 "The Gap"
**Reviewer profile:** 34-year-old, no energy regulation background, found via social share

---

## Comprehension

**First 30 seconds: keep reading or close tab?**

Keep reading. The opening works. "Nobody noticed" is a good hook. The image of a routine filing in a combined notice the week before Thanksgiving, while 49,000 people had no idea -- that lands. The first two paragraphs earn another scroll.

**Where does the jargon wall hit?**

Late in the Executive Summary. The piece successfully defines PPA and FERC in the lede, but then immediately piles on: CPUC, Liberty Utilities, Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE), TRIC, IRP, PUCN, Sierra Pacific, Greenlink West. By the fourth paragraph you are managing six entities and tracking which one is a subsidiary of which.

The piece defines the big terms (PPA, FERC, obligation to serve) well. The subsidiary relationships -- NV Energy / Sierra Pacific / Berkshire Hathaway Energy -- are the hardest to track. I had to re-read to understand that NV Energy and Sierra Pacific Power Company are the same company (or at least parent/subsidiary). That is never explained clearly.

**Undefined or confusing terms even after definition:**

- "IRP" (Integrated Resource Plan) -- defined once in section header context, but not in plain English before it starts appearing. What is an IRP and why should I care that NV Energy filed one?
- "Section 206 / Section 207 / Section 202(b)" -- legal sections cited extensively in the final section without explanation of what they do in practice. A reader can infer "legal tools FERC could use" but not why one matters more than another.
- "El Paso Electric Co. v. FERC (2000)" -- cited as if the case name means something. The structural parallel is briefly stated but the significance is not explained.
- "PJM Interconnection" -- defined as an RTO covering 67 million customers, but RTO is not defined.
- "NERC Level 3 alert" -- defined comparatively ("like the 2021 Texas winter storm") which works, but what does NERC actually do? Its authority is never explained.
- "Clean Transition Tariff" -- mentioned once, not explained.
- "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" -- mentioned once in the final section with no sourcing or context beyond the sentence it appears in.

**Can you follow the argument start to finish?**

Yes, with effort. The core argument is clear: wholesale obligation to serve is a contract, not a law; data centers broke the capacity assumptions that made that seam tolerable; the regulatory tools exist but nobody has used them; the people paying the price are low-income service workers. That logic holds.

The difficulty is that the piece makes the argument through six named sections that do not always carry the thread explicitly. "The Incentives" section is the hardest -- it spends several paragraphs on lobbying dollar amounts, then makes the key point (asymmetry of presence in regulatory process, not dollar amounts) at the very end. The reader has to hold a lot of numbers to get to the insight.

## Engagement

**How much did you actually read?**

Roughly 80%. I skimmed the Sources section entirely, and I skimmed the Escalation/Resolution Indicators. I read the body carefully through "The Pattern."

**Which sections drag, which fly?**

- Flies: Executive Summary (strong), The Architecture (clearest explanation of the legal seam), The Community (the demographics humanize it fast).
- Drags: The Contract (a chronology of PPA extensions is hard to care about), The Incentives (lobbying figures accumulate without much payoff until the very end), the legal section of The Question (citing case names without plain-English stakes).

**Is the piece too long?**

Not by much, but it is at the outer edge. The Contract section could be cut to three sentences without losing the argument. The lobbying dollar breakdown in The Incentives is more granular than it needs to be -- K&L Gates at $80K, Steptoe at $80K, etc. -- the total and the point (they lobbied the exact body that has jurisdiction) is what matters. A social-media reader will lose the thread there.

**Does the ending land?**

"The mechanism is legal. The choice is economic. The absence is regulatory." -- yes, this lands cleanly. It is the best line in the piece. The three-sentence summary in the final paragraph before that ("a system where a utility can legally choose its customers...") is the clearest summary of the core thesis and it appears at the very end, when many readers have already stopped.

## Trust and Tone

**Does the author seem credible?**

Yes. The sourcing is visibly extensive. FERC docket numbers, 990 filings, Federal Register citations -- a reader does not need to verify these to feel that someone did. The piece also actively names where the argument is complicated (Liberty's financial incentives are not obviously aligned with fighting the termination). That kind of counter-argument builds trust.

**Does it feel balanced?**

Mostly, but with a visible editorial lean. The piece frames NV Energy's "planned transition" characterization as "factually correct and misleading about proximate cause" -- that is editorially loaded language presented as neutral analysis. The bottom line of The Incentives section ("the other side of the table is empty") is stated as fact but is actually a political framing. The piece earns most of its conclusions but occasionally slides from analysis into advocacy without signaling the shift.

**Do the probability numbers help or confuse?**

Mixed. The 48% probability for Liberty securing replacement power is useful because the "roughly a coin flip" translation makes it concrete. The 15% FERC intervention probability is useful for the same reason. The 40% replication probability in The Pattern section and the 18% federal legislation probability in The Question section feel like they are there to demonstrate rigor rather than add insight. Four probability estimates in one piece starts to feel like a tic rather than a methodology.

## The Share Test

**Would you share it?**

Yes, to a specific list. Not broadly. I would send this to people I know who are interested in tech policy, energy policy, or inequality. I would not post it to my main social accounts because the length and the technicality create friction.

**Where?**

Group chat with people who send each other longform links. Not Twitter/X. Not Instagram. Maybe LinkedIn if I work in policy or energy.

**With whom?**

People who already know what FERC is, or people who are angry about tech companies and would read past the jargon to get to the community section. The piece has two potential audiences -- policy nerds who want the docket numbers and people who want the class-conflict story -- and it serves both but not simultaneously. A policy person will find the community demographics section a bit simple. A general reader will lose patience during the lobbying breakdown.

**One-sentence summary:**

A Nevada utility is legally allowed to cut power to 49,000 California residents to prioritize data center demand, the regulatory tools to stop it exist but nobody has filed the right paperwork, and the people who will absorb the cost shock are hotel workers earning $49K a year.

**Would you pay for more?**

Possibly, but not yet. This piece earns credibility. It does not yet sell a subscription. The closing does not tell me what to do with this information, what to watch for next, or why I should want the next issue specifically.

## Impact

**Did it change your mind about anything?**

Yes -- I had a vague sense that AI/data centers were straining the power grid, but I assumed that was a future problem or a technical problem. The specific mechanism (wholesale obligation to serve is contractual, not statutory) was genuinely new and the Lake Tahoe case makes it concrete. The detail about residential ratepayers funding the Greenlink West transmission line that serves the data centers that displaced them is the clearest crystallization of the structural irony.

**What question wasn't answered?**

What can residents actually do? The piece ends on "the gap is in attention" -- which is a diagnosis but not an action. If someone in South Lake Tahoe reads this and wants to help, the piece gives them nothing. Who do they call? What petition do they sign? The tools exist at FERC -- but a reader cannot file a Section 206 complaint. Is there an organization working on this? Is there a legislator to call?

Also: what happens if Liberty fails to secure replacement power? The piece says 48% probability of adequate procurement, but what does inadequate look like on the ground -- brownouts, emergency purchases at 5x cost, CPUC emergency orders? The actual worst-case scenario is implied but not stated.

## Additional Checks

**Readable without prior SD issues?**

Yes. No prior context is required. The piece is fully self-contained.

**Is the "so what" clear?**

Partially. The structural problem is clear. The immediate stakes (49,000 people, May 2027 deadline) are clear. What is not clear is what a reader is supposed to do with this information, or what "winning" would look like for the affected community.

**Undefined acronyms on first encounter:**

- BHE appears in paragraph 2 without expansion -- it is expanded later but the reader sees "BHE" cold.
- TRIC appears in paragraph 2, defined inline ("Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center") but then abbreviates immediately -- fine on first use, but it appears again several sections later and the reader has to remember what it means.
- RTO is never defined (used in "The Pattern").
- NRS appears without definition (used in "The Incentives"). A reader can infer "Nevada law" but it is not stated.
- IRP is expanded ("Integrated Resource Plan") but never explained in plain language.
