## Executive Assessment

Linerixibat is a first-in-class IBAT inhibitor for adult cholestatic pruritus in primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) — the only drug seeking FDA approval for this specific indication. The pivotal Phase 3 GLISTEN trial hit its primary endpoint with clean statistics (p=0.0013). There is no Advisory Committee. The drug has Breakthrough Therapy and Orphan Drug designations.

None of that is the story. The story is: **this is a resubmission after a hepatic safety CRL**.

FDA issued a Complete Response Letter on December 23, 2025. The concern was not the trial. The concern was a hepatic safety signal and a mandatory REMS requirement. GSK responded with a Class 1 resubmission — labeling changes and a revised REMS, no new clinical data. FDA accepted the resubmission under a 2-month review goal. PDUFA closes March 24.

Fifteen days before that PDUFA, GSK sold the drug to Italian pharma Alfasigma for $300M upfront, $100M on approval, and up to $690M total.

The March 24 decision is a single binary question: **is the revised REMS adequate to address what FDA flagged in December?** The exact nature of that hepatic signal is not public. That is the central unknown.

**Our estimate — 68% approval** — is lower than the 77% prior this brief was initialized with. The research moved the number down, not up. Here's why.

## Evidence Base

### Drug Mechanism and Indication

**Drug:** Linerixibat (formerly SHP625/LUM001)
**Mechanism:** Ileal bile acid transporter (IBAT) inhibitor. IBAT is the sodium-dependent bile acid transporter in the terminal ileum responsible for reabsorbing conjugated bile acids from the gut into enterohepatic circulation. Linerixibat blocks this transporter, preventing reabsorption. Bile acids that would normally recirculate instead pass into the colon and are excreted. Systemic bile acid load drops. The pruritogen hypothesis: elevated circulating bile acids (or related compounds) activate dermal itch receptors, likely via TGR5 and MRGPRX4 agonism. Reducing systemic bile acid levels reduces pruritic signaling.

**Indication:** Cholestatic pruritus in adult patients with primary biliary cholangitis (PBC). PBC is an autoimmune liver disease that causes progressive bile duct destruction. Cholestatic pruritus — itch driven by bile stasis — affects 50–70% of PBC patients and is a primary quality-of-life concern. No drug is currently FDA-approved specifically for this symptom in adults.

**Dose:** 40mg twice daily. Selected in GLIMMER dose-finding trial to optimize tolerability over efficacy maximization.

**Biomarker mechanism (C4):** IBAT inhibition increases 7α-hydroxy-4-cholesten-3-one (C4), a bile acid synthesis biomarker. Elevated C4 confirms target engagement. The same mechanism that drives efficacy (bile acid depletion from enterohepatic circulation) drives the primary adverse effect: bile acid spillover into the colon causes osmotic/secretory diarrhea. This is pharmacodynamic, not idiosyncratic. It cannot be engineered away.

### GLISTEN Phase 3 Trial

**Full name:** A Phase 3, Randomized, Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Study of Linerixibat in Adults with Cholestatic Pruritus and PBC (GLISTEN)
**Publication:** PubMed 41173016; Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology (PIIS2468-1253(25)00192-X)
**Duration:** 24 weeks

**Primary endpoint:** Worst Scratch Intensity (WSI), 0–10 Numerical Rating Scale. Weekly average over the last 4 weeks of treatment.

| Metric | Linerixibat | Placebo | Difference | p-value |
|--------|-------------|---------|------------|---------|
| WSI change from baseline | -0.72 points | Reference | -0.72 | p=0.0013 |
| Responder rate (≥2-point WSI reduction) | 56% | 43% | +13 pp | p=0.043 |

**What the numbers mean:** The 0.72-point treatment difference is statistically significant. Whether it crosses the minimal clinically important difference (MCID) for NRS itch scales — typically cited as 1–2 points in the literature — is a live question. FDA evaluates clinical meaningfulness, not just p-values. The responder rate secondary endpoint is marginal: p=0.043, confidence interval touching 0% on the lower bound. That is not a strong secondary endpoint.

**Safety:**

| Event | Linerixibat arm | Notes |
|-------|----------------|-------|
| Diarrhea | 61% | Pharmacodynamic, class effect, expected |
| Serious adverse events (SAEs) | 12% | 4× higher than IBAT class meta-analysis (2.2%) |
| Hepatic adverse events | Not disclosed (CRL basis) | Central unknown |

The 12% SAE rate versus the 2.2% IBAT class meta-analysis rate is the number FDA is looking at. The worker synthesis explanation — adult PBC patients with established disease are sicker than pediatric cholestatic patients — may be correct. But it explains *why* the number is high, not *whether the number is acceptable*. Those are different questions. FDA is asking the second one.

**Trial duration concern:** 24 weeks is short for a drug treating a lifelong chronic condition. The hepatic safety signal that triggered the CRL may be time-dependent and not fully characterized at 24 weeks. FDA often requires long-term data in REMS situations.

### Advisory Committee

**None convened.** Confirmed across multiple sources. This is consistent with IBAT class precedent and standard FDA practice for rare disease drugs with well-characterized endpoints. No briefing documents are public as of T-7.

The no-adcom signal is genuinely ambiguous:
- **Bullish read:** FDA has sufficient confidence in the data that external input isn't required
- **Bearish read:** FDA already issued a CRL *without* an adcom. The same conditions can produce a second CRL without an adcom. Absence of adcom is uninformative about direction

The adcom absence removes the strongest public probability signal available. The lack of briefing documents means we have no FDA staff analysis to read.

## Probability Assessment

### Final Estimate: 68% (range: 65–72%)

**Why this is lower than the 77% going-in prior:**

The 77% prior was the priority review first-cycle approval base rate — appropriate before looking at the specifics. The research found two factors that discount from that base rate:

1. **This is a resubmission, not an original approval.** A second FDA review of the same drug after a safety-based CRL. No base rate for "Class 1 resubmission after hepatic safety CRL" exists in public literature, but this class of decision is not the same as a first-cycle priority review. The denominator for the 77% base rate is first-cycle decisions.

2. **The Kalshi market at 62% is an informed prior.** Sophisticated pharma traders priced this contract. The synthesis dismissed the market as "mispriced" because our estimate was higher. After adversarial review, the correct posture is that the 10-point gap between our estimate (68%) and the market (62%) is a claim that we have information the market is missing — and we need to be specific about what that is. We don't have the exact CRL language. We don't have the REMS proposal. The 6-point edge is real but not decisive.

**Why it's not lower than 68%:**

The pivotal trial is statistically published and FDA-reviewed. The REMS pathway exists. FDA mandated a REMS (not a CRL citing insurmountable safety issues) — which means FDA was contemplating approval, just conditional approval. Class 1 resubmission acceptance means FDA agreed the resubmission addressed the outstanding issues in format, even if not necessarily in substance. The unmet need is genuine.

### Base Case: Approval — 68%

**Resolution criteria:** FDA issues approval letter for NDA219624 on or before March 24, 2026. Kalshi contract resolves YES.

**Logic:** The REMS pathway was FDA's stated mechanism for addressing the hepatic signal. REMS for hepatic monitoring (LFT testing intervals, prescriber certification) is a standard FDA tool for this type of concern. If GSK proposed an adequate monitoring protocol, FDA has a clear path to approval. No new trial data needed. The benefit-risk calculus is: real unmet need, published pivotal trial, REMS-managed safety concern.

**Key assumption:** The revised REMS proposal in the Class 1 resubmission adequately addresses what FDA flagged in December. This assumption cannot be verified from public sources.

### Bear Case: Second CRL — 26%

**Resolution criteria:** FDA issues a Complete Response Letter for NDA219624. Kalshi contract resolves NO.

**Logic (three independent pathways to CRL):**

*Pathway 1 — REMS inadequate:* The revised REMS doesn't satisfy FDA's hepatic safety concern. Could be insufficient monitoring frequency, inadequate patient enrollment criteria, or a concern the REMS architecture can't address (e.g., if the hepatic signal is related to drug exposure levels rather than something manageable by monitoring).

*Pathway 2 — Clinical significance:* The -0.72 NRS treatment difference may not clear FDA's clinical meaningfulness threshold. For a drug with 61% diarrhea and a hepatic safety signal, a less-than-MCID effect size tips the benefit-risk calculation. FDA reviewers will compare the modest itch improvement against a drug that gives 6-in-10 patients diarrhea for life and requires liver monitoring. The responder rate secondary endpoint (p=0.043, CI touching 0%) doesn't strengthen this case.

*Pathway 3 — Hepatic signal is structural:* The December CRL hepatic concern is not known. If ALT elevations were significant, frequent, or potentially irreversible, a REMS cannot address that — the drug has a safety ceiling that labeling changes don't fix.

## Adversarial Notes

The following are the adversarial review's highest-merit challenges, integrated into this assessment:

**1. Class 1 misread:** The synthesis initially characterized Class 1 resubmission as "FDA views the concern as circumscribed." This is wrong. Class 1 describes what *type of data* was submitted (labeling/REMS changes, no new clinical studies), not how FDA views the *severity* of its concern. A second CRL after a Class 1 resubmission is entirely possible. The Class 1 signal is neutral-to-positive, not directionally bullish. The initial +2pp upward revision was unsupported. Reverted.

**2. Clinical significance gap:** A 0.72-point NRS improvement is statistically significant at p=0.0013 but the MCID for itch NRS scales is typically cited as 1–2 points. The synthesis called GLISTEN "clean pivotal data." It is *statistically* clean. Whether it's *clinically convincing* to an FDA reviewer who is also looking at 61% diarrhea and a hepatic signal is a different question. The synthesis didn't answer it.

**3. GSK deal timing is ambiguous:** The synthesis treated the March 9 deal signing as evidence of GSK's non-approval assessment. The adversarial review identifies at least four other equally plausible interpretations (see Evidence Base section). The deal timing is informative but not directionally bearish on its own.

**4. 12% SAE explanation is circular:** Explaining that adult PBC patients have more SAEs because they're sicker tells you *why* the number is high but not *whether it's acceptable*. FDA's question is the second one. The synthesis used Worker 2's population-difference argument to close an open question that remains open.

**5. The Kalshi market is not obviously wrong:** The synthesis flagged 62% as "mispriced" because our estimate was higher. The adversarial review correctly notes that Kalshi participants include sophisticated pharma event traders. If the gap between our estimate and the market is real, we should be able to name what information we have that they don't. We have: the IBAT mechanism is well-characterized, the pediatric precedent is favorable, Class 1 resubmission is confirmed. The market may have: better read on the CRL hepatic signal, knowledge of the REMS proposal content, or conversations from healthcare channels. The gap is real (6–10 points) but modest. This brief is not "the market is mispriced by 10 points" — it is "our estimate is slightly higher, but with appropriate uncertainty."

## What the Market Is Missing

If the market is underpriced at 62%, here's what it hasn't fully weighted:

**1. First-in-indication advantage.** No drug is approved for adult cholestatic pruritus in PBC. FDA's benefit-risk calculus explicitly accounts for unmet need. When there is no alternative, FDA accepts a different safety tolerance. The clinical significance challenge on -0.72 NRS is real, but the context is: patients currently have nothing FDA-approved. That context shifts the MCID question.

**2. REMS pathway = FDA was already contemplating approval.** A CRL that mandates a REMS is different from a CRL that rejects a drug's safety profile. The December CRL said: this drug needs a safety management plan, not this drug's safety is unacceptable. That is a meaningful distinction. The Kalshi price may not fully weight the difference between a "fix the REMS and we'll approve it" CRL and a "this drug's risk-benefit is too unfavorable" CRL.

**3. No AdCom = no organized opposition.** An adcom is where patient advocates, physicians, and external experts can voice skepticism. Without an adcom, there is no formal public forum for benefit-risk debate. FDA reviewers can — and do — reach approval decisions without external input validation.

**4. Pediatric IBAT class precedent.** The same mechanism is approved in two pediatric populations. The hepatic safety signal in GLISTEN is concerning, but the class is approved and the precedent exists that IBAT inhibition can be managed to an approvable safety profile.

## What Could Go Wrong

*Ranked by probability of contributing to a CRL:*

**1. REMS inadequacy (highest risk):** The hepatic signal may require monitoring requirements that are either commercially unworkable (REMS with Elements to Assure Safe Use) or that FDA finds structurally insufficient. The exact nature of the December CRL hepatic signal is unknown. This is the number-one unknown that cannot be resolved from public data.

**2. Clinical significance failure:** FDA reviewer determines 0.72 NRS points does not constitute a meaningful clinical benefit, particularly given 61% diarrhea. The benefit-risk calculation fails. The drug is "statistically effective but not clinically meaningful."

**3. Hepatic signal is structural, not manageable:** If the hepatic signal reflects a dose-related or mechanistic toxicity that monitoring can't prevent — not just detect — a REMS doesn't solve it. Only dose reduction or treatment discontinuation does. If that's FDA's view, approval requires labeling restrictions that may make the drug commercially unviable, or a second CRL.

**4. Manufacturing issue (lower probability):** Class 1 resubmissions can include manufacturing data. If GSK/Alfasigma had a CMC (chemistry, manufacturing, controls) deficiency unresolved from the original NDA, it could resurface. No public signal of this, but it's a possible CRL vector.

**5. 24-week durability insufficient:** FDA requires longer-term data before approval. Possible but low-probability given no AdCom discussion of this issue and the REMS pathway doesn't typically require this for the initial approval.

**6. Alfasigma transition issues:** The FDA approval is technically being transferred to a new sponsor. If the CMC responsibility transition between GSK and Alfasigma introduces procedural complications, it could delay the decision. Low probability, noted for completeness.
