## QUESTION 1: Affected Communities

### Service Territory Overview

Liberty Utilities (CalPeco Electric) LLC serves approximately 49,000-51,551 electric customers (figures vary by filing year) across portions of seven California counties. The service territory extends from Portola in the north to Walker/Coleville/Topaz Lake in the south. Approximately 80% of customers are in the Lake Tahoe Basin. The territory is winter-peaking (Christmas week peak), mountainous (5,000-9,050 ft elevation), and heavily forested. About 60% of residential accounts are vacation homes or second-family rentals.

**Critical infrastructure note:** The territory is located entirely within the NV Energy Balancing Authority Area and is neither geographically nor operationally part of CAISO. Liberty connects to NV Energy at 38 points and relies entirely on Nevada transmission lines.

### Adjacent Utility Context

- **Truckee Donner PUD (TDPUD):** Serves ~14,648 customers in the core Truckee area. Also 100% transmission-dependent on NV Energy. Surrounded by Liberty territory on south, east, and north. TDPUD faces the same transmission vulnerability but has its own separate supply arrangements.
- **PG&E:** Serves west-slope El Dorado County (not the Tahoe Basin). No physical interconnection to Liberty's territory -- PG&E infrastructure stops on the west side of the Sierra crest.
- **Pioneer Community Energy:** CCA serving Placer and El Dorado Counties via PG&E infrastructure. Explicitly excludes Liberty Utilities customers in eastern Tahoe. El Dorado Board of Supervisors has created an ad hoc committee to explore Pioneer as a potential option, but Pioneer operates over PG&E transmission -- it cannot serve territory that is not on PG&E's grid.

## QUESTION 2: What Happens If Supply Fails?

### Liberty's Legal Obligation

**Liberty cannot stop serving its customers. The obligation to serve is absolute under California law.**

- **Cal. Pub. Util. Code Section 451:** "Every public utility shall furnish and maintain such adequate, efficient, just, and reasonable service, instrumentalities, equipment, and facilities... as are necessary to promote the safety, health, comfort, and convenience of its patrons, employees, and the public."

- **Section 761:** Gives the CPUC authority to determine and fix "the rules, practices, equipment, appliances, facilities, service, or methods to be observed, furnished, constructed, enforced, or employed" by any public utility.

- **Section 762:** The CPUC can order "additions, extensions, repairs, or improvements... to secure adequate service or facilities."

- **Section 768:** The CPUC can require utilities to "construct, maintain, and operate its line, plant, system, equipment, apparatus, tracks, and premises in a manner so as to promote and safeguard the health and safety of its employees, passengers, customers, and the public."

**Precedent from the 2000-2001 California Energy Crisis:** In CPUC Decision D.01-01-046, the Commission stated: "State law clearly requires utilities to serve their customers, and a threatened bankruptcy filing or threat of insolvency does not change that obligation." The Commission issued a temporary restraining order compelling PG&E and Edison to continue procuring and delivering power even during the energy crisis. The Commission invoked its "plenary powers" under Sections 451, 761-788.

**Bottom line:** Liberty Utilities is legally required to keep serving all 49,000 customers regardless of what happens with NV Energy. The question is not whether Liberty serves them, but how, at what cost, and with what reliability.

### Emergency Mechanisms

#### CPUC Emergency Powers

The CPUC has broad statutory authority:

- Can compel Liberty to take specific procurement actions
- Can order emergency rate increases to fund emergency procurement
- Can approve expedited contracts without full evidentiary proceedings
- Can issue temporary restraining orders to maintain service
- In 2001, the CPUC ordered emergency capacity procurement for PG&E/SCE/SDG&E under D.21-02-028 (for summer 2021 reliability) -- this was expedited procurement without full proceeding

#### Provider of Last Resort (POLR) Framework

California's POLR framework (SB 520, 2019; Rulemaking R.21-03-011) was designed for CCA/ESP failures, where the IOU steps in as backup. This framework does **not apply** to Liberty's situation because:

- Liberty IS the IOU in its territory -- there is no "bigger" utility behind it
- PG&E is not physically interconnected to Liberty's territory
- POLR assumes the backup provider shares the same transmission infrastructure

**This is a gap.** The POLR framework was designed for retail provider failures within an existing grid, not for wholesale supply loss to a physically isolated utility.

#### CAISO Emergency Procedures

Liberty is NOT in the CAISO Balancing Authority. CAISO's Energy Emergency Alert (EEA) system -- EEA Watch through EEA 3 (which triggers rolling blackouts) -- applies within the CAISO footprint. Liberty sits within the NV Energy BA.

NV Energy has its own emergency procedures, but NV Energy's obligation is to its own Nevada retail customers, not to Liberty's California customers.

### Physical Grid Reality

**Liberty's territory is functionally a Nevada grid island that happens to serve California customers.**

Key facts from CPUC filings and reporting:

1. **No CAISO connection:** Liberty's territory is "neither geographically nor operationally part of the California Independent System Operator" (D.10-10-017).

2. **38 interconnection points with NV Energy:** All electricity flows from Nevada. This was the case when Sierra Pacific Power owned the territory, and it remains the case today.

3. **Transmission entirely over NV Energy lines:** Power generated anywhere must reach Liberty customers via NV Energy's transmission system. There are no California-side transmission paths into the territory.

4. **Mountain barrier:** The Sierra Nevada creates a physical barrier. Building a new transmission line from the California grid (e.g., from El Dorado Hills) would require crossing the Sierra crest. Liberty President Eric Schwarzrock described this as costing "hundreds of millions of dollars" with "significant land impacts." This is not a viable emergency option.

5. **On-territory generation:** Liberty owns only the 12 MW Kings Beach Diesel Generation Station. Liberty's peak winter demand (around Christmas) is estimated at 200-250 MW. The diesel station covers roughly 5% of peak demand -- it was designed for local backup, not system-wide supply.

6. **Off-territory generation:** Liberty owns the 50 MW Luning Solar Facility and 10 MW Turquoise Solar Facility, both located in Nevada. Combined with Kings Beach, Liberty generates approximately 25% of its supply. The remaining 75% comes from NV Energy under the wholesale PPA.

7. **TDPUD shares the same vulnerability:** Truckee Donner PUD, serving ~14,648 customers in adjacent territory, is also 100% transmission-dependent on NV Energy and "not directly interconnected with the California transmission system nor to any California utility in a meaningful way."

### Liberty's Backup Options

#### Option 1: New Wholesale Supply via Greenlink West (Primary Plan)

- **What:** Once Greenlink West is operational, Liberty gains transmission access to a broader Western market. Liberty can issue an RFP and contract with generators in Nevada, Utah, Idaho, Oregon, or elsewhere.
- **Status:** Liberty filed an advice letter with CPUC in March 2026 seeking authorization for an expedited RFP. Liberty plans to issue the formal RFP in summer 2026.
- **Timeline risk:** Greenlink West expected online May 2027 (NV Energy), but NNBW reported in April 2026 it may slip to "after summer 2027." Only 150 of ~2,500+ transmission structures erected as of April 2026. Stringing of actual power lines not yet begun (scheduled summer 2026). BLM permit suspension in January 2025 caused delays for the Bureau of Indian Affairs right-of-way through Snow Mountain Reservation.
- **WECC filing note:** NV Energy's March 2026 WECC progress report states the company is "considering methods to mitigate the impact of the Suspension and maintain the proposed in-service date of May 2027."
- **Even if on time:** Liberty still needs to negotiate, execute, and get CPUC approval for a replacement contract. The RFP has not yet been issued. The timeline from RFP issuance (summer 2026) to executed contract to power delivery by May 2027 is extremely compressed.
- **Market leverage concern:** 49,000 customers competing against major utilities and data center operators in the Western electricity market. As one expert told Fortune: "zero leverage." Liberty's winter-peaking profile (Christmas week) may help -- it is counter-cyclical to most Western demand.

#### Option 2: NV Energy Extension (Emergency Stopgap)

- **What:** NV Energy agrees to yet another extension beyond May 2027.
- **Precedent:** NV Energy has extended repeatedly -- the original 5-year PPA expired in 2016, was extended in 2015, 2020, and late 2025.
- **Current signals:** NV Energy's public statements emphasize this was "a planned transition for many years" and that Greenlink West "will make this transition possible." The company has positioned May 2027 as a firm deadline tied to Greenlink West completion.
- **Leverage:** If Greenlink West is delayed, NV Energy may be forced to extend because Liberty physically cannot receive power from anyone else until the new transmission capacity exists. NV Energy stated it will "continue providing service until Liberty's transmission service is available."
- **Assessment:** This is the most likely fallback scenario. NV Energy cannot physically cut off Liberty without also disconnecting TDPUD's 14,648 customers (shared transmission infrastructure). The reputational and regulatory cost of leaving 63,000+ California customers without power would be enormous.

#### Option 3: Kings Beach Diesel Generation (Emergency Only)

- **What:** The 12 MW Kings Beach diesel station can provide localized emergency power.
- **Capacity:** 12 MW against ~200-250 MW peak demand = ~5% of peak load.
- **Use case:** Could keep critical facilities running during a brief emergency. Cannot sustain the service territory for any meaningful duration. Fuel supply logistics in a remote mountain location add further constraints.
- **CPUC transfer decision noted:** The 625/650 line upgrade project was partly designed to "decrease reliance on the Kings Beach Diesel Generation Station."

#### Option 4: CAISO Spot Market (Not Viable)

- **What:** Purchase emergency power from the CAISO spot market.
- **Problem:** Liberty is not in the CAISO Balancing Authority. There is no physical transmission path from the CAISO-controlled grid to Liberty's territory. Power would still need to flow over NV Energy's transmission system. Even if commercially possible, physically impossible without NV Energy cooperation.

#### Option 5: Bonneville Power Administration (Not Viable Short-Term)

- **What:** Import power from Pacific Northwest via BPA.
- **Problem:** Same physical constraint. Any power reaching Liberty must flow over NV Energy's transmission system. BPA delivery would require wheeling arrangements through NV Energy. Not impossible in principle once Greenlink West provides access to wider markets, but not a near-term emergency option.

#### Option 6: Direct California Grid Connection (Not Viable Short-Term)

- **What:** Build a new transmission line over the Sierra Nevada connecting to PG&E/CAISO infrastructure.
- **Cost:** "Hundreds of millions of dollars" per Liberty President Schwarzrock.
- **Timeline:** 5-10+ years minimum (environmental review, permitting, construction at high elevation).
- **This is a long-term structural solution, not an emergency option.**

#### Option 7: Pioneer Community Energy/PG&E Absorption (Not Viable)

- **What:** Local officials have explored whether Pioneer Community Energy could serve the Tahoe Basin.
- **Problem:** Pioneer is a CCA that operates over PG&E transmission infrastructure. PG&E has no physical connection to the Lake Tahoe Basin east of the Sierra crest. Pioneer cannot serve customers it cannot physically deliver power to.
- **Status:** El Dorado Board of Supervisors created an ad hoc committee to explore. The Tahoe Daily Tribune reported this as a topic of discussion. Fundamental physics problem remains.

### Contingency Planning -- What Liberty and CPUC Have Said

**Liberty:**
- March 2026: Filed advice letter with CPUC seeking authorization for expedited RFP process
- Plans to issue formal RFP in summer 2026
- "This does not mean the power is shutting off" -- President Eric Schwarzrock
- "Liberty has committed to no blackouts" -- per Energy News Beat
- Priorities: meet California RPS requirements, keep costs affordable
- Plans to bid to "anybody and everybody," leveraging winter-peaking profile

**CPUC:**
- Has not opened a standalone proceeding on the supply transition
- Continuing Liberty's existing GRC proceeding (A.24-09-010)
- CPUC spokesperson Terrie Prosper: once advice letter is approved, Liberty would choose its new supplier and seek CPUC approval on the final contract
- Cal Advocates and TURN are active intervenors in the GRC

**Local advocates:**
- Danielle Hughes (CEO Tahoe Spark, CEC supervisor): "We don't exist" in state energy planning
- Sierra Club Tahoe Area Group: calling for full CPUC proceeding, not expedited RFP
- South Lake Tahoe Mayor Cody Bass: April 2026 letter to CPUC expressing "a great deal of concern"
- State Senator Marie Alvarado-Gil: seeking input on power in Tahoe

**What is notably absent:**
- No CPUC order requiring Liberty to demonstrate supply adequacy before May 2027
- No emergency procurement directive from CPUC
- No formal CPUC investigation into the supply gap
- No FERC intervention (despite the FERC-jurisdictional wholesale contract)
- No NV Energy contingency commitment beyond "continue providing service until Liberty's transmission service is available"

### The Worst Case

If Liberty fails to secure replacement power AND NV Energy refuses to extend AND Greenlink West is delayed:

1. **Liberty cannot simply stop serving.** Cal. Pub. Util. Code Section 451 mandates continued service. The CPUC can and would compel emergency action.

2. **The 12 MW Kings Beach diesel station** could provide limited emergency power to critical facilities. At 5% of peak capacity, this means most of the territory goes dark.

3. **Rolling blackouts** for 49,000 customers plus TDPUD's 14,648 (total ~63,000) in a high-altitude mountain environment. In winter, this means:
   - Temperatures regularly below 0 degrees F at night
   - Electric heating is primary heat source for many homes
   - Water systems depend on electric pumps
   - Wildfire risk from alternative heating sources
   - Resort/tourism economy collapses (ski areas, hotels, restaurants)
   - Road safety systems (signals, snowplows) compromised
   - Medical equipment for elderly/disabled residents fails

4. **Economic devastation:** The Lake Tahoe tourism economy (25-28 million visitors/year) would be crippled. Businesses cannot operate without reliable power. Property values collapse. Permanent residents forced to relocate.

5. **This scenario is extremely unlikely.** The political, legal, and reputational costs to all parties of allowing it would be catastrophic. Most probable outcome: NV Energy extends (again) if Greenlink West is delayed, while Liberty works through the RFP process. The real risk is not blackouts -- it is higher costs and reduced leverage being passed to ratepayers who have already seen 77% rate increases since late 2022.
