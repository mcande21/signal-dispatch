# The Gap
## Signal Dispatch Deep Dive | May 16, 2026

**Classification:** Open Source Intelligence
**Topic:** How electricity regulation lets utilities choose data centers over communities
**Analyst confidence:** High

---

## Executive Summary

On November 17, 2025, Sierra Pacific Power Company filed a document with the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) titled "Amended Liberty PPA" -- PPA as in Power Purchase Agreement, the contract under which NV Energy supplied Liberty Utilities' electricity. It appeared in the Federal Register three days later -- one of twenty-five filings listed in a routine Combined Notice, published the week before Thanksgiving. The twenty-one-day comment period closed on December 8. Nobody protested. Nobody noticed.

Four months later, 49,000 residents of Lake Tahoe learned they were losing their electricity supply. NV Energy -- the parent brand for Sierra Pacific Power Company in northern Nevada, itself a subsidiary of Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE) -- was terminating the wholesale power agreement that had kept their lights on since 2011. The reason, per Liberty Utilities' own filing with the California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC): "data centers in the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center (TRIC) area and northern Nevada transmission constraints."

The story that followed -- covered by *Fortune*, *Ars Technica*, and a dozen other outlets -- framed this as a utility choosing data centers over people. That framing is incomplete. NV Energy is not breaking the law. It is not even bending it. It is operating within a structural gap in American electricity regulation where the obligation to serve -- bedrock of utility law since English common law -- dissolves at the wholesale level. The contract was always temporary. Liberty Utilities had sixteen years to find alternative supply. The question is not whether NV Energy can do this. The question is why the legal architecture makes it possible, who benefits from the gap, and why the regulatory body with clear jurisdiction has not been asked to act.

I assess a **50% probability** (range 40-55%) that Liberty secures adequate replacement power by May 2027 -- a coin flip, driven by the uncertain completion of a $4.2 billion transmission line and a procurement timeline measured in months for a problem sixteen years in the making. I assess a **15% probability** that FERC intervenes to extend service -- about one in seven, conditional on someone filing a complaint, which nobody has. (1)(2)

## The Architecture

Every U.S. state has an obligation-to-serve statute. The formulations vary -- Nevada's runs ten words, California's fills multiple code sections -- but the principle is universal: a regulated utility must provide adequate, efficient, and reasonable service to all qualified customers within its territory. This is the regulatory compact. The utility receives a monopoly franchise, protection from competition, and a guaranteed rate of return. In exchange, it serves everyone, including the unprofitable customers. The principle traces to English common law and the common carrier doctrine. It is as close to bedrock as American infrastructure law gets.

But it has a seam.

The obligation to serve applies at the retail level -- where a utility sells directly to homes and businesses. At the wholesale level -- where one utility sells to another for resale -- the obligation is contractual, not statutory. A wholesale supply agreement is a commercial arrangement between two companies. It has terms, expiration dates, and renewal clauses. When those terms expire, the seller can walk away. The Federal Power Act gives FERC jurisdiction over interstate wholesale sales and the tools to intervene, but intervention requires someone to ask. Without a complaint, FERC watches.

This distinction between retail and wholesale was tolerable for decades. Wholesale relationships were stable. Capacity was abundant. The seam was theoretical.

It stopped being theoretical when data centers arrived.

In 2009, NV Energy sold its California distribution assets -- the grid infrastructure serving approximately 47,000 customers across seven counties around Lake Tahoe -- to what would become Liberty Utilities for $132 million. To keep the lights on, the sale included a PPA: NV Energy would supply Liberty's full electricity requirements at system-average cost for an initial five-year term. The CPUC approved the arrangement in October 2010, noting an "additional, five-year right to obtain power" -- a maximum supply horizon through 2020. The arrangement was always described as temporary. Liberty was expected to develop independent supply sources.

The seam in the architecture was now load-bearing. NV Energy no longer owed these customers anything under Nevada law. Liberty owed them everything under California law. But Liberty could only deliver on that obligation as long as NV Energy kept selling.

**Bottom line: the 2009 asset sale converted 47,000 retail customers into wholesale dependents -- and wholesale obligation to serve is a contract, not a law.**

## The Contract

The five-year PPA expired in 2015. Liberty had not secured independent supply. NV Energy extended.

The extension expired in 2020. Liberty had built two solar facilities generating roughly 25% of its needs. The other 75% still came from NV Energy. NV Energy extended again.

That extension approached its end in late 2025. Liberty's dependency had barely changed. But NV Energy's capacity situation had changed completely.

On November 17, 2025, Sierra Pacific Power Company filed docket ER26-534-000 with FERC -- the "Amended Liberty PPA." The prior contract had been set to expire on December 29, 2025. The amendment bridged the gap to May 2027, aligned with the expected completion of NV Energy's Greenlink West transmission line. But unlike the previous extensions, this one set a firm, non-renewable termination date.

NV Energy's spokesperson, Katie Jo Collier, called it "a planned transition for many years, not a reaction to recent developments." This is technically accurate. The original PPA contemplated a maximum ten-year supply period. NV Energy extended three times over sixteen years, each time accommodating Liberty's failure to achieve independence. The fourth non-extension is the end of extraordinary patience.

That framing is factually correct about the contract structure and misleading about the proximate cause. Liberty's own advice letter to the CPUC, filed in March 2026, states that NV Energy cited "data centers in the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center area and northern Nevada transmission constraints, among other reasons" for ending service.

I assess the contract was always going to end. Data centers determined when.

**Bottom line: NV Energy's "planned transition" framing is factually correct about contract structure and misleading about proximate cause.**

## The Capacity

NV Energy's 2024 Joint Integrated Resource Plan (IRP), filed with the Public Utilities Commission of Nevada (PUCN), contains the numbers that make this structural rather than anecdotal.

Thirty-nine projects have requested 7,600 MW of new capacity connections in NV Energy's territory through 2033. Twelve of those projects are data centers. They account for 5,900 MW -- 78% of all capacity requests. NV Energy applies 49-85% planning discounts to account for projects that will not materialize (per the 2024 Joint IRP, Section 4.2, load forecast methodology); after discounting, data centers still represent an estimated 40-60% of incorporated load growth. Even the low-end read is dominant. Nearly all concentrate in Sierra Pacific's northern Nevada territory, where Liberty's wholesale supply originates. Sierra Pacific's available import capacity during the 2024-2027 action plan period: 0 MW.

Zero is not a policy position that can be negotiated. It is a physical transmission constraint.

The counterfactual sharpens the causal claim: remove the 5,900 MW of data center capacity requests and the remaining 1,700 MW of general load growth is manageable within Sierra Pacific's existing system. Liberty's 300+ MW of full-requirements load would fit. The capacity crisis that is terminating Liberty's contract does not exist without data centers.

The 2025 IRP Amendment makes the tradeoff explicit. Filed because developers cancelled or delayed 144 MW of previously approved resources, it requests a 150 MW battery storage system by July 2027 and lists the Liberty contract termination as a load forecast adjustment. Liberty's end date is a planning input, managed in the same document as data center interconnection queues.

Then there is Greenlink West.

NV Energy's $4.2 billion, 472-mile, 525-kV transmission line is the largest infrastructure project in the company's history. It was pitched to regulators and ratepayers as clean energy infrastructure -- NV Energy's 2021 Plan of Development called it "necessary to accommodate decommissioning of conventional fossil fuel generation." By September 2025, NV Energy's own business development director was more candid: "I don't think we intended to build that for data center load, but it's certainly beneficial." Its northern terminus sits behind Google's campus at the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center. NV Energy has committed 4,000 MW of the line's 5,000 MW total capacity to data center customers -- Switch, Novva, and others -- per reporting by *Fortune* (2026-05-12) and Sierra Club expert testimony filed in NV Energy's 2024 IRP proceeding. The remaining 1,000 MW serves general load growth. Construction costs are borne by residential ratepayers -- approximately $4 or more per month for typical households, over $25 per month during peak usage, per the PUCN's September 2025 rate approval (*The Nevada Independent*, 2026-04-29).

The completion date for Greenlink West: May 2027. The same month Liberty's power supply ends.

NV Energy told South Tahoe Now that Liberty would be "first in the waiting line" for Greenlink capacity. But 80% of that capacity is already spoken for. The line was designed to serve the data centers that created the capacity constraint that is terminating Liberty's contract. Residential ratepayers are funding the infrastructure that serves the customers that displaced them. Even with Greenlink complete, Northern Nevada's total import capacity reaches 2,800 MW against 21,000 MW of data center demand requests -- the system will be capacity-constrained almost immediately upon completion.

I assess a **50% probability** (range 40-55%) that Liberty secures adequate replacement power by the May 2027 deadline. Major transmission projects of this scale complete on schedule 60-70% of the time. If Greenlink West slips six months, Liberty has no replacement and no alternative pathway. The Department of Energy's (DOE) 2023 transmission study found average delays of 12-18 months for projects of comparable scale. The estimate depends on what "adequate" means: in the lights-stay-on scenario (emergency short-term purchases keep service running), I assess 70-75%. In the clean-transition scenario (cost-neutral procurement without rate shock), I assess 25-30%. The headline 50% reflects the range between those outcomes -- Liberty likely avoids a blackout but almost certainly does not avoid a rate shock.

**Bottom line: Sierra Pacific has 0 MW of available import capacity, and the $4.2 billion transmission line being built to solve that is 80% committed to the customers who caused the shortage.**

## The Incentives

Nevada's data center tax abatement program, administered under Nevada Revised Statutes (NRS) 360.754, offers 75% property tax abatement and a reduced 2% sales tax rate for qualifying facilities. The ten-year package requires a minimum of 10 full-time Nevada employees and $25 million in capital investment. The twenty-year package requires 50 employees and $100 million. Data center facilities at TRIC have received approximately $457 million in cumulative abatements for an estimated 300 permanent jobs -- roughly $1.5 million in public subsidy per permanent position, per the Nevada Governor's Office of Economic Development (GOED) abatement records filed under NRS 360.754. (3)

NV Energy itself markets these incentives. The company publishes a data center brochure advertising Nevada's tax package and has created a "Clean Transition Tariff" specifically designed for large customers -- minimum 5 MW load, long-term contract periods, pricing structured to fully cover the costs of new renewable generation. The tariff is a product designed to attract the demand that NV Energy simultaneously claims is straining its system beyond capacity.

This is where the steel-man defense of NV Energy -- that it faces genuine physical capacity constraints and is simply managing scarce resources -- breaks down. You cannot claim 0 MW of available capacity while spending $600,000 per quarter lobbying for policies that accelerate data center demand.

In Q1 2026, BHE spent $600,000 on lobbying -- $240,000 across four external firms plus $360,000 in-house. K&L Gates, retained at $80,000, lobbied FERC directly on energy permitting -- the exact regulatory body with jurisdiction over the Liberty termination, on behalf of the company executing it.

On the industry side, the Data Center Coalition -- the sector's primary trade association -- deployed $620,000 in Q1 2026 lobbying. Akin Gump ($140,000) worked energy tax credits and transmission policy. Crossroads Strategies ($60,000) covered data center industry issues. The Coalition's own in-house lobbying ($420,000) targeted energy permitting reform, nuclear incentives, interconnection policy, and the GRID Power Act. Vantage Data Centers separately retained Cornerstone Government Affairs ($50,000) to lobby the National Energy Dominance Council -- a Trump administration body -- on "energy and technology policy related to data center operations and development."

The Data Center Coalition's 990 filings trace the buildup: revenue jumped from $706,000 in 2022 to $2.5 million by 2023 -- nearly five-fold in one year. The industry recognized that its growth trajectory would collide with grid constraints and invested in the political infrastructure to manage that collision on favorable terms.

NV Energy's PAC is modest by comparison: $49,000 in receipts for the 2026 cycle, distributed across Nevada's Congressional delegation in bipartisan increments. This is maintenance-level political engagement, not the story.

The story is who is present in the regulatory process and who is not. BHE lobbies FERC. The Data Center Coalition lobbies Congress. Vantage lobbies the White House. On the other side -- representing 49,000 Lake Tahoe residents, the Sierra Club Tahoe Area Group, the South Lake Tahoe mayor -- nobody has filed at FERC. Nobody has petitioned for Section 206 review. Nobody has invoked the tools that exist. The asymmetry is not in the dollar amounts. It is in attention.

**Bottom line: the industry invested in the political infrastructure to manage the collision between data center demand and grid constraints on favorable terms. The other side of the table is empty.**

## The Community

The default mental image of Lake Tahoe -- ski chalets and vacation homes -- obscures who actually lives there.

South Lake Tahoe, the population center of Liberty Utilities' service territory, is 26.1% Hispanic and Latino. The median household income is below the California average. 50.7% of the workforce earns less than $49,000 per year. These are the housekeepers, lift operators, restaurant servers, and maintenance workers who keep the tourism economy running. Liberty's territory has zero physical connection to the California grid -- all 38 transmission interconnection points run through Nevada, making these customers physically captive to a single wholesale supplier. Ninety percent of households earning less than 30% of area median income have unmet housing needs.

Liberty's electricity rates have already increased approximately 77% since late 2022. Most of that increase predates the data center conflict -- it reflects wildfire mitigation costs, insurance premiums exceeding $30 million annually, and infrastructure upgrades approved in Liberty's 2024 general rate case. The data-center-driven rate shock has not hit yet. It arrives after May 2027, when Liberty must procure replacement power at market rates rather than NV Energy's cost-based formula -- assuming replacement power is available at all.

Liberty Utilities' president has said the company is pursuing an expedited Request for Proposals (RFP) for replacement supply. The Sierra Club Tahoe Area Group and Tahoe Spark have filed protests with the CPUC calling for a full regulatory proceeding rather than the expedited process Liberty proposed.

There is a harder question embedded in the community dynamics, one that complicates the clean victim narrative. Liberty Utilities is a subsidiary of Algonquin Power & Utilities Corp (now Liberty Energy), a publicly traded Canadian company. If Liberty procures replacement power at higher market rates after May 2027, the CPUC rate case process allows the company to pass those increased costs through to ratepayers -- with an approved rate of return on new capital investment. Higher procurement costs can mean higher allowed revenue and higher returns for the parent company. Liberty's financial incentives are not obviously aligned with fighting the termination. This does not change the impact on residents. It does change the calculation of why Liberty has not filed a complaint at FERC.

**Bottom line: the people who will absorb the rate shock are hotel staff and lift operators earning less than $49,000 a year, and the company nominally representing their interests has a financial structure that profits from higher procurement costs.**

## The Pattern

Lake Tahoe is the sharpest case -- a direct wholesale termination stranding a residential population -- but it is not the only front in this conflict. Data center electricity demand is colliding with grid capacity across at least ten states, through different mechanisms that produce the same outcome: residential customers absorbing costs and risks created by industrial load growth they did not choose.

On May 4, 2026, the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) issued a Level 3 alert -- the highest severity classification, rarely deployed -- specifically addressing data center loads. The alert cited "immediate risks" to bulk power system reliability from data centers unexpectedly dropping or oscillating load, and imposed mandatory compliance actions due August 3, 2026. NERC does not issue Level 3 alerts casually. The last one of comparable scope addressed the 2021 Texas winter storm.

In PJM Interconnection, the regional transmission organization (RTO) covering 67 million customers across 13 states from New Jersey to Illinois, capacity auction prices rose from $28.92 to $329.17 per MW-day over two delivery years -- an approximately 11-fold increase. PJM's independent market monitor attributed the increase "almost entirely" to data center load growth in Virginia and surrounding states. This is a different mechanism than Nevada's bilateral termination -- PJM operates a competitive wholesale market, not a vertically integrated utility system, and the 11x figure does not directly predict cost impacts for Lake Tahoe residents -- but the downstream effect is the same: residential ratepayers absorb cost increases driven by data center demand they have no voice in approving.

The Maryland Office of People's Counsel filed a Section 206 complaint at FERC on May 7, 2026, arguing that PJM's cost allocation rules unfairly assign approximately $1.6 billion in data-center-driven transmission costs to Maryland ratepayers over the next decade. In Virginia, residents near the Loudoun County data center corridor have seen wholesale electricity prices increase 267% and residential bills rise 80%, per Dominion Energy rate filings and reporting by *Utility Dive* (2026-05-13).

Community resistance is accelerating. The Reno City Council voted 6-1 for a data center moratorium. More data center projects faced rejection or cancellation in Q1 2026 than in all of 2025 (*Data Center Frontier*, 2026-03-12). The bipartisan GRID Act (Grid Reliability and Infrastructure Development), introduced by Senators Hawley and Blumenthal, represents the first Congressional proposal to explicitly prioritize residential ratepayers over data center customers in grid planning.

The Electric Power Research Institute's (EPRI) 2026 *Powering Intelligence* report projects data center electricity demand 60% higher than EPRI's own 2024 estimates. The range: 9-17% of total U.S. electricity consumption by 2030, up from approximately 4.4% today. Even the low-end scenario doubles the current share. The structural mismatch is temporal: data centers can be built in two years; grid connections and transmission upgrades take five to seven. That gap guarantees more Lake Tahoes -- unless demand proves more elastic than projected. Cryptocurrency mining generated similar grid-capacity alarm in 2017-2021 before demand collapsed with prices. Whether AI compute demand follows the same elasticity curve or is structurally stickier (the compute must happen somewhere, and the capital investments are larger and longer-lived) is the key uncertainty in the long-range forecasts.

I assess a **40% probability** (range 30-50%) that another community faces a wholesale supply termination specifically driven by data center demand within the next twelve months -- roughly two in five, more likely not to happen than to happen, but the conditions are replicating. Resolves YES if a utility files to terminate or non-renew a wholesale supply agreement citing data center load, affecting 1,000+ residential customers, by May 2027. The broader pattern of rate increases, grid reliability issues, and community conflict is already reality -- the question is whether the most extreme mechanism repeats. The conditions that produced the Lake Tahoe situation -- interstate wholesale relationship, isolated grid, capacity constraint, concentrated data center demand -- are unusual but not unique.

**Bottom line: Lake Tahoe is the leading edge. The structural conflict between data center demand and residential service exists wherever the conditions align, and the conditions are aligning faster than regulatory architecture can adapt.**

## The Question

The Federal Power Act gives FERC clear authority over the NV Energy-Liberty wholesale relationship. Section 206 allows FERC to investigate whether a termination of jurisdictional service is "unjust and unreasonable" and to order relief. Section 202(b) allows FERC to order continued interconnection and wholesale sales when the public interest warrants it. Section 207 allows FERC to compel a utility to extend or improve its facilities if necessary to provide adequate service. In *El Paso Electric Co. v. FERC* (2000), the Fifth Circuit affirmed FERC's authority to order temporary wholesale sales to the City of Las Cruces, New Mexico, under circumstances structurally similar to the Lake Tahoe situation.

None of these tools has been invoked.

Liberty Utilities has not filed a Section 206 complaint. The California Attorney General has not petitioned FERC on behalf of 49,000 California residents. The Sierra Club, which has filed protests at the CPUC, has not filed at FERC. The story broke nationally on May 12, 2026. It is May 16 as I write this. The comment period on the November 2025 FERC amendment closed six months ago. The tools exist. The jurisdiction is clear. The precedent supports intervention.

FERC Chair Swett said at a May 2026 public commission meeting that PJM is, in her words, "too big to function" and faces a "serious legitimacy crisis" partly driven by data center demand. The Commission is aware of the structural conflict. But FERC is a reactive body -- it acts on complaints, petitions, and filings. Without a filing, it watches.

I assess a **20% probability** that federal legislation addressing data center grid impact passes within twenty-four months -- one in five, low but not negligible given bipartisan interest. Resolves YES if Congress passes and the President signs legislation explicitly addressing data center electricity demand or residential ratepayer protection from data center load growth by May 2028. The GRID Act has bipartisan sponsorship, which is necessary but not sufficient. The Trump administration has framed data centers as national security infrastructure -- essential to AI competitiveness with China -- and signed what the administration characterized as a "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" with major tech companies in March 2026 (White House fact sheet, 2026-03-14). That pledge is both a policy response and an implicit admission that the cost-shifting problem is real. But the administration's energy posture favors permitting acceleration, not ratepayer protection.

The gap in this story is not in the law. FERC has the tools. States have moratorium authority. Courts have the precedent. The gap is in attention -- who is present in the regulatory process, who understands the filing deadlines, who has the resources to navigate a FERC docket. NV Energy filed an amendment in a routine combined notice the week before Thanksgiving, and the twenty-one-day comment period passed in silence. That is not concealment. It is how infrastructure decisions that affect tens of thousands of people are made: in plain sight, in technical proceedings, before audiences that do not include the people affected.

The legal architecture of American electricity regulation was not designed for a world where a single industrial use case -- training AI models -- can consume more power than mid-sized cities. The wholesale/retail distinction in obligation to serve was tolerable when wholesale relationships were stable and capacity was abundant. Data centers broke that assumption. The result is a system where a utility can legally choose its customers based on profitability, where residential ratepayers fund the transmission lines that serve the industrial loads that displaced them, and where the regulatory body with jurisdiction sits idle because nobody asked it to act.

The mechanism is legal. The choice is economic. The absence is regulatory.

**Bottom line: the gap is not in the law. It is in attention.**
