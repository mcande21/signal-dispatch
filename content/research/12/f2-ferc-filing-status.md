What *was* found:

1. **2019 Federal Register Combined Notice (84 FR 29511, June 24, 2019):** Sierra Pacific Power Company filed tariff cancellations at FERC:
   - **Docket ER19-2174-000:** "Rate Schedule No. 66 SPPC & Liberty EPC Cancellation to be effective 8/17/2019"

2. **2011 Federal Register Combined Notice (Feb 16, 2011):** CalPeco filed at FERC (Docket ER11-2833-000) a "Cancellation of CalPeco Filing" shortly after the January 2011 asset transfer closed. This was CalPeco establishing its own FERC tariff/rate schedule presence.

3. **2023 Federal Register -- Section 206 Proceeding (Docket EL23-27-000):** FERC instituted a Section 206 proceeding against Nevada Power Company and Sierra Pacific Power Company -- but this was related to the proposed merger of NPC and SPPC (which was subsequently abandoned in April 2023), NOT the Liberty wholesale agreement.

4. **FERC document E-32 (2020):** The MidAmerican/Berkshire Hathaway acquisition of NV Energy. Confirms both Nevada Power and Sierra Pacific are FERC-jurisdictional public utilities that provide wholesale power services.

**Bottom line:** The 2019 cancellations prove SPPC knows the FERC filing requirement and has complied with it before for Liberty-related rate schedules. No equivalent filing for the current full-requirements wholesale agreement termination was found in any search of FERC-adjacent public records.

## Legal Commentary

### Direct Commentary on NV Energy / Liberty / FERC

**No energy law firm, legal blog, or regulatory expert has publicly analyzed the FERC jurisdictional question specific to the NV Energy/Liberty termination.** This is itself significant -- the story broke nationally on May 12, 2026 (Fortune), and as of May 15, no law firm client alert or legal analysis has appeared.

### Relevant Adjacent Commentary

1. **Orrick (November 2025):** Detailed analysis of FERC's response to data center interconnection demand. Key finding: The DOE directed FERC to initiate rulemaking on large-load interconnections (>20 MW) by April 2026. The ANOPR "clarifies that the interconnection of large loads to the interstate transmission system falls squarely within FERC's jurisdiction." However, this addresses *new* interconnection, not *termination* of existing wholesale service.

2. **ENR (May 2026):** Reports FERC Chair Laura Swett called large-load integration "the most important and pressing problem in contemporary American public policy" and signaled willingness to push jurisdictional boundaries. Swett stated at the Energy Bar Association meeting (April 15, 2026): "I have an appetite to push right up to that edge [of precedent], if it may secure effective results."

3. **EBA Journal (1985):** The seminal article on wholesale obligation to serve, cited in F.1, remains the most relevant legal analysis. Its core conclusion -- that FERC approval is effectively required before wholesale service can be abandoned -- has not been publicly contradicted by subsequent FERC orders.

4. **Mondaq / FERC PJM Order (December 2025):** FERC ruled existing rules for co-located load service are "unjust and unreasonable," establishing that FERC is willing to find utility practices related to data center load unlawful under Section 206.

5. **FERC vs. DOE timeline conflict:** FERC stated it would not meet the DOE's April 30 deadline for the large-load interconnection rulemaking. The rulemaking is expected by June 2026. This is relevant because a new FERC rule on large-load interconnection could indirectly affect the NV Energy/Liberty dynamic by establishing federal standards for how utilities prioritize load.

## ASSESSMENT

### Is there a FERC filing or not?

**No FERC filing for the termination of the NV Energy/Liberty full-requirements wholesale supply agreement was found.** Confidence: **High** that no such filing is publicly visible as of May 15, 2026.

This assessment is based on:
- Extensive searches of FERC-adjacent databases (Federal Register, SEC EDGAR, CPUC docket system)
- Confirmation that SPPC filed cancellation notices for *other* Liberty-related rate schedules at FERC in 2019 (Dockets ER19-2174-000 and ER19-2176-000), establishing that SPPC knows the process
- Absence of any reference to a FERC filing in any of the 15+ news articles, regulatory filings, or SEC disclosures reviewed
- Absence of any law firm commentary analyzing a FERC proceeding

**Caveat:** Exa-based web search cannot directly query FERC's eLibrary database (elibrary.ferc.gov). A direct eLibrary search for Sierra Pacific Power Company filings in 2025-2026 would be the definitive verification. FERC eLibrary filings sometimes appear before Federal Register publication.

### What does the absence mean?

Three possible interpretations, ranked by likelihood:

1. **Most likely -- Filing not yet made (timing):** NV Energy may intend to file the termination notice closer to the May 2027 end date. Under 18 C.F.R. Section 2.4(c)(4), the notice must be filed, but the regulation does not specify *how far in advance*. NV Energy may be waiting until replacement arrangements are clearer. However, FPA Section 205(d) requires 60 days' notice to the Commission before any change in service, so the filing would need to occur by approximately March 2027 at the latest.

2. **Possible -- Contract structured to avoid FERC filing:** If the wholesale agreement was structured as a bilateral contract with a fixed expiration (not a FERC-filed rate schedule), NV Energy might argue that mere expiration does not require a FERC filing -- the contract simply ends by its own terms. However, 18 C.F.R. Section 2.4(c)(4) explicitly covers service that "is to terminate by its own terms," suggesting this argument is weak.

3. **Less likely but significant -- Deliberate avoidance:** NV Energy may be deliberately avoiding a FERC filing to prevent FERC review of the termination under Sections 205/206. A FERC filing could trigger protests from Liberty, the Sierra Club, California officials, and potentially a FERC investigation into whether the termination is "just and reasonable." Given NV Energy's stated rationale (data center capacity) and the current political sensitivity of data center power allocation, FERC review carries meaningful risk for NV Energy.

### Strategic implications for SD #12

The absence of a FERC filing is arguably the most important legal fact in this case. It creates:

- **A potential enforcement hook:** If FERC determines that the wholesale agreement is a FERC-jurisdictional rate schedule (which the 2019 cancellation filings strongly suggest), then NV Energy *cannot* terminate service without filing at FERC. An unfiled termination would be a violation of FPA Section 205.
- **An intervention opportunity:** Liberty, California officials, the Sierra Club, or FERC itself (on its own motion under Section 206) could raise this issue. FERC Chair Swett's stated appetite to push jurisdictional boundaries makes this more plausible than it would have been under prior FERC leadership.
- **A ticking clock:** If the termination proceeds without FERC review, it sets a precedent that wholesale suppliers can redirect power from residential-serving utilities to industrial customers (data centers) without federal oversight -- precisely the scenario FERC's large-load rulemaking is trying to address.
