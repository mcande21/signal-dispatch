### Constitutional Framework Post-Jackson

**Jackson v. Metropolitan Edison (1974) remains controlling law.** The Supreme Court held 6-3 that a privately owned utility terminating service to one customer is NOT state action triggering 14th Amendment due process, even when the utility holds a state-granted monopoly and operates under extensive regulation. Justice Rehnquist's majority opinion established that the state must be "sufficiently connected" with the challenged action -- mere regulation, monopoly status, or tariff approval does not create that nexus.

**The doctrine has NOT been meaningfully relaxed since 1974.** Post-Jackson evolution has actually *tightened* the standard:

- **Blum v. Yaretsky (1982):** Even a nursing home with 90%+ government-funded patients was not a state actor. The Court required plaintiffs to show the state was responsible for "the specific conduct of which the plaintiff complains."
- **Rendell-Baker v. Kohn (1982):** A school receiving virtually all its funding from government was still a private actor.
- **Lindke v. Freed (2024):** The most recent state action case (social media context) reaffirmed the requirement of "actual authority rooted in written law" and conduct "fairly attributable to the State." The Court emphasized: "the presence of state authority must be real, not a mirage."
- **Murthy v. Missouri (2024):** The Court addressed government "coercion" and "significant encouragement" of private actors as potential state action, but ultimately found standing issues. The Fifth Circuit's framework required showing the government "compelled" or was "actively, meaningfully" involved in the specific decision.

**Four tests remain in play, none of which clearly apply here:**
1. **Public function test:** Utilities provide essential services but are not performing a "traditionally exclusive" state function (Jackson itself rejected this).
2. **Joint action test:** Requires the state to be a "willful participant" in the challenged activity itself.
3. **Nexus test:** Requires a "sufficiently close nexus" between the state and the specific challenged action.
4. **State compulsion test:** Requires the state to have *compelled* the private conduct.

**However -- the NV Energy/Lake Tahoe situation is factually distinguishable from Jackson in critical ways:**

| Factor | Jackson (1974) | NV Energy/Tahoe (2026) |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Scale | 1 retail customer | 49,000 customers |
| Cause | Customer nonpayment | Utility's commercial preference |
| State role | Commission merely permitted termination | State actively incentivized data centers via NRS 360.754 (75% property tax abatement, sales tax reduced to 2% for 10-20 years) |
| IRP involvement | None | NV Energy's 2024 Integrated Resource Plan explicitly allocates capacity to data centers; 75% of projected load growth is data centers |
| Resource planning | N/A | PUCN approved resource plan prioritizing data center load |

**The strongest constitutional argument** is that Nevada's conduct goes beyond "mere regulation" into active resource allocation: the state granted tax incentives specifically to attract data centers (NRS 360.754), the PUCN approved an IRP that prioritizes data center load, and the combined effect is the displacement of 49,000 residential customers. This is closer to the "state compulsion" or "joint action" tests than Jackson's passive regulatory approval. But no court has yet applied this reasoning to an energy reallocation case.

**Megatel Homes v. City of Mansfield (5th Cir. 2026)** -- a recent antitrust state action case -- is relevant by analogy. The Fifth Circuit held that even where state law endorses monopoly regulation, immunity does not attach unless the state delegated anticompetitive authority to the specific actor. Applied here: Nevada delegated monopoly authority to NV Energy but arguably did not delegate authority to terminate wholesale supply to prioritize a different customer class.

### Environmental Justice Analysis

**Executive Order 12898 (1994)** requires federal agencies to identify and address "disproportionately high and adverse human health or environmental effects" on minority and low-income populations. However:

- EO 12898 applies to *federal agency actions*, not state utility decisions.
- The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) has jurisdiction over interstate wholesale electricity sales -- the NV Energy/Liberty contract is exactly this type of transaction. If FERC were to act on the wholesale termination, EO 12898 would apply to FERC's analysis.
- DOE is currently developing an Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on large-load interconnections (data centers >20MW). If finalized, this federal rulemaking would trigger NEPA and EO 12898 analysis.

**The data center environmental justice movement is accelerating in 2026:**

- **NAACP sued xAI** in April 2026 for illegally operating 27 gas turbines without air permits near homes, schools, and churches in Southaven, Mississippi. Abre Conner, NAACP Director of Environmental and Climate Justice: "Our homes, churches, and playgrounds will not be sacrifice zones for Big Tech's convenience."
- **120+ organizations** sent a letter to Congress in April 2026 urging rejection of fast-tracking data centers, stating: "Nearly half of U.S. data centers are already located in [low-income and communities of color], deepening cumulative pollution burdens."
- **Georgetown professor** (TheGrio, April 2026): "Big Tech is siting [data centers] in Black and poor communities, raising utility bills, polluting the air, and draining watersheds."
- **Michigan PSC** (November 2025) approved first data center tariff provisions, stating projected rate increases for residential customers would be "unacceptable."
- **Wisconsin PSC** (April 2026) required data center operators to cover full cost of generators built to serve them.
- **Pennsylvania regulators** (May 2026, same day as this research) moved to protect utility customers from data center costs.

**Biden-era EJ framework** (now partially dismantled): The Trump administration's EO 14318 fast-tracked federal permits for data center infrastructure. The CEQ rescinded NEPA regulations. DOJ pulled environmental justice guidelines. This means the federal EJ framework is weaker than it was 12 months ago, but state-level EJ frameworks remain intact.

**California-specific EJ framework:** California has its own EJ requirements through SB 1000 (2016) and CalEnviroScreen. The CPUC has an Environmental and Social Justice Action Plan. However, Liberty's situation falls into a regulatory gap -- CPUC regulates Liberty's rates and procurement but cannot order NV Energy to continue selling wholesale power. That falls to FERC.

### Community Demographics: Who Loses vs. Who Benefits

**Lake Tahoe (the losers):**

South Lake Tahoe (the incorporated city, ~21,300 residents) has a more complex demographic profile than the "wealthy ski town" narrative suggests:

- **26.1% Hispanic/Latino** (Census 2024) -- the majority are service-industry workers in tourism/hospitality
- **Median household income:** $82,002 (near national median) but **50.7% of the workforce earns less than $49,000/year** (City Housing Element, 2019 ACS data)
- **9.3% poverty rate** (lower than national 12.5% but significant)
- **19.2% foreign-born residents**
- **27.9% speak a language other than English at home**
- **Largest employment sectors:** Accommodation & Food Services (2,276 people), Health Care (1,440), Retail (1,374) -- heavily service-oriented
- **Homeownership rate:** 51.3% (well below national 65.2%)
- **Median home value:** $682,400 (2x national median) -- creating severe housing cost burden
- **Housing crisis:** 62% of workforce housing need is for households <120% AMI (TRPA 2025 Assessment). 90% of households earning <30% AMI have unmet housing needs. Approximately half of Tahoe workers now commute from outside the region.
- **Electricity rates already up ~77% since late 2022** (Bloomberg reporting)

**Critical quote from community advocate (Fortune/eritvnews reporting):** "Even though we have low-income communities in both South Lake Tahoe and North Lake Tahoe, Kings Beach, both the Energy Commission and the California Public Utility Commission do not include us in any of their socioeconomic plans." -- Tahoe Spark's Sue Hughes

The affected population is NOT uniformly wealthy. The service workers who run the tourism economy -- hotel staff, restaurant workers, ski resort employees, grocery workers -- are disproportionately Latino, lower-income, and already housing-cost-burdened. They face rate increases they cannot absorb.

**TRIC / Data Centers (the beneficiaries):**

- **107,000-acre industrial park** in Storey County (population ~4,100), the smallest county in Nevada
- **18,000+ employees by 2018** (mostly Tesla Gigafactory, logistics); data center permanent staff is minimal
- **NRS 360.754 tax abatements:** 75% property tax reduction + sales tax reduced to 2% for 10-20 years. Requirements: only 10 or 50 full-time employees depending on abatement tier.
- **$457 million in approved tax incentives** for data centers -- alongside only ~300 promised permanent jobs. That works out to **~$1.5 million in incentives per permanent job** (vs. ~$190,000/job for the Tesla deal).
- **$537 million in foregone sales/use tax revenue** over 8 years across local governments (Nevada Independent analysis, March 2026).
- **Data center electricity consumption:** 22% of Nevada's total in 2024, projected to reach 35% by 2030.
- **12 major projects** could add 5,900 MW of demand by 2033 -- 75% of NV Energy's projected load growth.
- Companies: Google ($4.75B invested for AI power in 2025), Apple, Microsoft, Switch, Tract, EdgeCore, Novva

**The disparity:** State-subsidized data centers requiring minimal employment are displacing power from 49,000 residential customers, many of whom are low-income service workers in a tourism economy already under severe housing and cost-of-living pressure.

### Right to Electricity: Emerging Legal Theories

**No U.S. court has recognized a constitutional right to electricity.** The landscape:

- **International law:** CEDAW mentions electricity access for women specifically. UN SDG 7 calls for "affordable and clean energy for all." Academic literature (Lofquist 2019, Tully 2006) treats electricity access as a "derived right" -- necessary to fulfill other recognized rights (housing, health, education) but not a standalone right.
- **Congressional resolution (2022):** Reps. Tlaib, Bush, and Bowman introduced a non-binding resolution recognizing utilities including electricity as "basic human rights." It did not advance. The resolution would have recognized electricity access as needing to be "accessible, safe, acceptable, sufficient, affordable, justly sourced and sustainable."
- **State-level protections are extensive but framed as consumer protection, not rights:**
  - **40+ states** have cold-weather disconnection moratoria
  - **29 states** have seasonal protections; 23 have temperature-based protections
  - California (CPUC D.20-06-003) has among the strongest protections: disconnection caps by ZIP code (30% maximum), 12-month payment plans required before disconnection, no deposits for service establishment, no reconnection fees, special protections for medical baseline/life support customers
- **COVID precedent (2020-2021):** 34 states + DC implemented emergency utility disconnection moratoria. Research (PMC, 2023) found these protections reduced credit delinquencies and benefited residents of majority-Black communities most.
- **Scale of the problem (2024):** First-ever EIA national disconnection report found **13.5 million residential disconnection events** in 2024; 94.9 million final notices sent. "The high volume of final notices and disconnections serves as an indicator of household financial stress."

**Key gap:** All existing protections address *retail* disconnection for nonpayment by individual customers. No framework addresses *wholesale supply termination* that effectively strands an entire service territory. This is the novel legal territory the Lake Tahoe situation occupies.

### California-Specific Protections

**California Public Utilities Code Section 451 -- Obligation to Serve:**

> Utilities must "furnish and maintain adequate, efficient, just, and reasonable service" for customers in their service territories.

- Utilities ordinarily **cannot terminate service without providing "adequate" substitute service** (Stanford Law analysis, 2020).
- The CPUC has interpreted Section 451 broadly: in Corona City Council (1992), SoCalGas violated 451 by closing rural branch offices even though it had not cut off gas service -- the lack of adequate substitute access was enough.
- **Section 451 grants customers due process rights**, including adequate notice and opportunity to be heard prior to service termination.
- The obligation to serve has two prongs: (1) extension of service to new customers, and (2) **continuation of service once begun**.

**Section 387 -- Provider of Last Resort:**

- Designates a load-serving entity to provide electrical service to any retail customer whose provider "failed to provide, or denied, service."
- Requires "universal access, equitable treatment of all classes of customers, and other customer protections."
- The CPUC "shall supervise and regulate each provider of last resort, as necessary... to ensure the provision of electrical service to customers without disruption."

**CPUC Disconnection Framework (D.20-06-003, Phase I Decision):**

- Residential disconnection caps by ZIP code (30% maximum rate)
- 12-month payment plans mandatory before disconnection
- Special protections for vulnerable populations (life support, medical baseline, low-income housing)
- Disconnection Citation Program with penalties of $500-$100,000 per violation
- These apply to CPUC-regulated utilities including Liberty Utilities.

**Critical limitation:** Section 451 and CPUC protections bind *Liberty Utilities* (as a California-regulated IOU) but do NOT bind *NV Energy* (a Nevada utility). The CPUC "cannot order NV Energy to keep selling wholesale power or dictate how Nevada plans for data centers" (Fortune/eritvnews reporting). The wholesale relationship falls under FERC jurisdiction. This creates a regulatory gap:

- **California sets the rules** (retail rates, consumer protections, renewable requirements)
- **Nevada runs the wires** (transmission infrastructure, generation planning)
- **FERC regulates the wholesale market** (but has not intervened)
- **No single entity is accountable for the outcome**

Liberty's March 2026 advice letter to CPUC sought authorization for an expedited RFP process. The Sierra Club and Tahoe Spark have argued this should be a full CPUC proceeding rather than an expedited advice letter process, given the scale (49,000 ratepayers on an isolated grid in a high wildfire risk area).

**The obligation-to-serve doctrine is being actively reexamined** in the context of building decarbonization (gas-to-electric transition), where the question is whether "adequate substitute service" can mean a different fuel type. The Building Decarbonization Coalition's 2024 report found the obligation to serve exists in every state but varies enormously in specificity. The parallel to Tahoe: can adequate substitute service come from a different wholesale supplier, even at substantially higher cost?

### ASSESSMENT

**Is there a viable constitutional or EJ challenge?**

**Constitutional (14th Amendment/Due Process): WEAK under current doctrine.**
Jackson remains controlling, and the state action doctrine has only tightened since 1974. However, the NV Energy/Tahoe situation presents *the strongest factual basis for distinguishing Jackson* since that case was decided: the state's active role through tax incentives (NRS 360.754), PUCN-approved resource planning explicitly prioritizing data centers, and the scale of displacement (49,000 customers vs. 1 in Jackson). A creative plaintiff would argue this crosses from "mere regulation" to "joint action" or "state compulsion" -- but this is untested and would likely fail under existing precedent.

**Environmental Justice (Federal): MODERATE but narrowing.**
EO 12898 would apply if FERC takes action on the wholesale termination or if DOE finalizes its large-load interconnection rulemaking. The demographic data supports an EJ claim: Lake Tahoe's service workforce is disproportionately Latino and low-income, already housing-cost-burdened, and will bear the brunt of replacement power costs. However, the Trump administration has dismantled key federal EJ enforcement mechanisms (CEQ NEPA regulations rescinded, DOJ EJ guidelines pulled). The NAACP/xAI and congressional mobilization signals growing political salience but weakening federal enforcement.

**California Regulatory (Section 451 / CPUC): STRONG -- but limited in scope.**
Liberty Utilities has a clear obligation to serve under Cal PUC Code 451. If Liberty cannot secure affordable replacement power and rates spike dramatically, affected customers have a cognizable claim that Liberty is failing to provide "adequate, efficient, just, and reasonable service." The CPUC has tools to intervene -- rate caps, provider of last resort designation, formal proceedings. But the CPUC cannot solve the upstream problem: it cannot compel NV Energy to sell power or control wholesale market prices. The most likely regulatory outcome is a CPUC-supervised transition that keeps the lights on but at significantly higher cost, with ratepayer advocates fighting over cost allocation.

**Novel Theory (Utility Service Prioritization / Energy Democracy): NOVEL-UNTESTED.**
The Lake Tahoe situation is a first-of-its-kind case: a utility explicitly redirecting capacity from residential customers to data centers receiving state tax subsidies. No existing legal framework directly addresses this. The emerging data center EJ movement (NAACP, Climate Justice Alliance, Earthjustice, state PSCs in Michigan/Wisconsin/Pennsylvania) is building the political and regulatory infrastructure to address it, but through cost-allocation mechanisms and tariff reform rather than constitutional claims. The strongest path is regulatory, not constitutional.

**Overall assessment: The constitutional challenge is weak; the regulatory challenge is strong; the political momentum is real.** The most productive legal avenue runs through CPUC proceedings, FERC wholesale market oversight, and the growing state-level movement to insulate residential ratepayers from data center costs. The EJ framing is powerful for narrative and political purposes even where it lacks direct legal enforceability.
