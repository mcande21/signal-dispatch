## Probability Assessment (Integrated)

### Base Case: NO mention — 66% *(up from 62%)*
Powell holds the line. Verbatim rule confirmed — "recessionary pressures" is his comfortable ceiling. Two recent NO resolutions provide behavioral base rate. Iran war provides external attribution. Penultimate meeting incentivizes stability. He frames -92K as "significant softening" or "considerable headwinds" without crossing into recession framing. The March 2020 playbook repeats: the language defers 4-8 weeks. Reporters press; he deploys the epistemic disclaimer ("no one can predict") and geopolitical attribution ("extraordinary external circumstances"). Resolution: NO.

### Bull Case (for YES): 24% *(down from 28%)*
A reporter asks the direct question under impossible-to-deflect conditions. The -92K NFP is the hook — "Chair Powell, with the worst jobs report in years, are we heading into a recession?" Under direct questioning, Powell's reflex is to deny ("I do not think the US is currently in a recession") — and **that denial itself uses the word**. The 2022 pattern: Powell said "recession" in congressional testimony by defending against the premise. The same mechanism could trigger at a presser.

Key constraint: the verbatim rule now confirmed means the denial mechanism *does* count if the word appears anywhere in Powell's spoken statement. The resolution criteria don't discriminate between affirmative and defensive usage. This is the residual YES edge.

Probability hinges on: (a) whether a reporter frames the question in a way that forces the word into the denial, and (b) whether Powell chooses "deny with the word" vs. "pivot without the word" when cornered.

### Bear Case (deep NO): 10% *(unchanged)*
Conditions are bad but not 2020-bad. The Iran external shock framing holds completely. Powell pivots to dot plot discussion and inflation dynamics. Reporters don't land the direct recession question cleanly. Market is right at 28¢. No edge.
