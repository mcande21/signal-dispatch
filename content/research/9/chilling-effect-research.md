## Executive Summary

The SAVE Act's voter suppression potential operates through two distinct channels: (1) the **mechanical documentation barrier** (citizens who lack proof-of-citizenship documents), and (2) the **behavioral fear channel** (eligible citizens who voluntarily disengage from civic participation out of fear of immigration enforcement systems). This report examines the second channel -- which may ultimately suppress more votes than the first.

The evidence establishes that:

- **31% of naturalized citizens** now fear detention or deportation of themselves or family members, up from 12% in 2023 (KFF/NYT 2025 Survey)
- **21% of naturalized citizens** have limited outdoor activities or government interactions since January 2025
- Immigration enforcement reduces voter **registration** propensity by 5% among citizens in mixed-status households (Amuedo-Dorantes & Lopez 2017)
- **10.6 million U.S. citizens** live with an undocumented immigrant; **22 million people** total in mixed-status households
- The SAVE database structurally links voter registration to DHS immigration enforcement infrastructure, creating a novel fear vector with no historical parallel
- Law enforcement presence at polling places produces a **32% reduction** in African American voter participation (Niven 2021)
- State-targeted ethnicity produces **multigenerational political disengagement** -- documented across 80+ years in Japanese American internment research (Komisarchik, Sen & Velez 2022)

The compound effect -- aggressive ICE enforcement + SAVE database voter-DHS linkage + visible community operations + rescission of sensitive locations protections -- creates a suppression environment qualitatively different from any prior election cycle.

## A. Academic Evidence: Immigration Enforcement Effects on Civic Participation

### The Mobilization-Suppression Paradox

The academic literature reveals a critical paradox: immigration enforcement can simultaneously **mobilize** some Latino voters while **suppressing** others, and the balance depends on which population you measure.

**Mobilization findings:**

- White & Ariel (2016), "When Threat Mobilizes" -- Secure Communities implementation led to a **2-3 percentage point increase** in county-level Latino voter turnout. The effect was driven by organizational activism, not individual responses to enforcement encounters. Published in *Political Behavior*.
- Arizona SB 1070 (2010) -- Latino voter registration tripled in the permanent early voter list (90,000 to 300,000+). Organizational infrastructure (One Arizona coalition) translated threat into registration drives.
- The Obama campaign's 2012 Latino outreach drew directly on lessons from Harry Reid's 2010 response to SB 1070. National Latino turnout rose from 9.7M (2008) to 11.2M (2012).

**Suppression findings:**

- Amuedo-Dorantes & Lopez (2017), "Interior Immigration Enforcement and Political Participation of U.S. Citizens in Mixed-Status Households" -- Using 2002-2014 CPS Voting and Registration Supplements, enforcement **reduced voter registration propensity by 5%** among citizens in mixed-status households. However, it did **not** reduce voting propensity among those already registered. Published in *Demography* 54(6): 2223-2247.
- The same study found that lower registration was accompanied by **greater civic volunteering** -- suggesting disengagement from government-facing systems specifically, not political apathy.
- Romo Alba, Schachter & Moinester (2025), "Resisting Enforcement" -- Found mixed effects depending on the **type** of enforcement contact. Personal and proximal contact with enforcement increased some forms of civic engagement among Latino adolescents, but the mechanisms differed from formal electoral participation.

### The Critical Distinction

The mobilization literature measures **aggregate turnout at the county or community level**. The suppression literature measures **individual registration behavior of citizens in mixed-status households**. Both can be true simultaneously:

- Politically engaged citizens with strong ethnic identity mobilize in response to threat
- Vulnerable citizens in mixed-status households withdraw from government-facing registration systems
- Net county-level turnout can rise even as specific subpopulations are suppressed

This distinction is analytically essential for the SAVE Act analysis. The SAVE Act's fear channel specifically targets the suppression-vulnerable population (citizens connected to immigration enforcement databases through household composition), not the mobilization-responsive population (citizens with organizational infrastructure and strong group identity).

### Historical Parallel: Japanese American Internment

Komisarchik, Sen & Velez (2022), "The Political Consequences of Ethnically Targeted Incarceration" -- Published in *The Journal of Politics* 84(3): 1497-1514.

This Harvard study provides the strongest historical analog for state-targeted ethnic suppression of civic participation:

- Japanese Americans who were incarcerated or had relatives who were incarcerated were **significantly less likely** to participate politically, be political leaders, or engage in political discussion
- Effects **persisted across generations** -- detectable in subsequent generations decades later
- Disengagement **increased with internment length**
- Camps that witnessed intragroup violence or demonstrations produced **sharper declines**, suggesting group fragmentation as a mechanism
- The effects operated through **stigma** rather than just material barriers

This is directly relevant: the SAVE Act creates a system where naturalized citizens and citizens in mixed-status households are systematically flagged, investigated, and required to prove their right to participate. The stigma mechanism identified in the internment research operates through the same channel.

### Post-9/11 Muslim American Civic Engagement

The Muslim American experience after 9/11 shows both channels:

- **Suppression:** Post-9/11 surveillance programs (NSEERS requiring registration of males from 25 Muslim-majority countries) created pervasive community fear. A 2003 Brooklyn survey found families fleeing with children, 50 students disappearing from one school. Government surveillance "chills free speech" with individuals avoiding political engagement "out of fear that they're going to become targets of law enforcement."
- **Mobilization:** Muslim-American voter registration rose from 60% (2016) to 78% (2020). Emgage turned out over 1 million Muslim voters in 2020. Partisan realignment pushed Muslim Americans toward the Democratic Party.
- **Net effect:** Mobilization eventually dominated, but only after organizational infrastructure developed over 15+ years. In the immediate post-9/11 period, suppression effects were dominant.

## B. The SAVE Database Connection

### What SAVE Is and How It Changed

The Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements (SAVE) program was originally a federal lookup tool used to verify immigration status for public benefits applications. The Trump administration fundamentally transformed it into a voter verification system:

**Timeline of transformation:**

- **January 20, 2025:** Executive Order 14159 issued on inauguration day
- **March 25, 2025:** Executive Order 14248 directing voter citizenship verification access
- **Spring-Summer 2025:** USCIS refashioned SAVE into a platform capable of bulk voter roll scanning
- **May 2025:** Social Security Administration (Numident) database integrated into SAVE
- **September 2025:** DOJ confirmed sharing state voter roll lists with DHS
- **October 31, 2025:** DHS published Systems of Records Notice (SORN) retroactively formalizing changes -- months after implementation, bypassing Privacy Act advance notice requirements
- **February 2026:** ProPublica/Texas Tribune investigation documented widespread SAVE errors flagging citizens as noncitizens

### Known Error Rates

**National data:**
- Across 7 states with ~35 million registered voters, roughly 4,200 people were flagged as noncitizens
- Of 49.5 million voter registrations checked (per NYT, January 2026), DHS referred ~0.02% (~10,000 cases); county investigations found "only a fraction" were potentially noncitizens

**Texas:**
- 2,724 flagged from 18+ million registrations
- Survey of 97 counties: >5% of those flagged proved to be citizens
- Travis County: 11 of 97 flagged were confirmed citizens
- Denton County: 12 of 84 flagged proved citizenship -- **at minimum 14% error rate**
- Moore County: 6 of 11 flagged were eligible (55% error)
- Randall County: 1 in 5 had U.S. passports

**Missouri:**
- Boone County: >50% of initially flagged voters were actually citizens
- St. Louis County: initial list of 691 revised down to 133 after corrections (81% reduction)
- Officials acted on SAVE findings **before verifying**, temporarily blocking hundreds of eligible citizens from voting

**Idaho:**
- 760 flagged from 1 million+ registrations; further review narrowed to ~12 actual cases

### Structural Limitations

The SAVE system has inherent data gaps that disproportionately affect specific populations:

1. **Cannot identify derivative citizens** -- Americans who gained citizenship as children when their parents became citizens have no individual naturalization record
2. **Numident database gaps** -- SSA citizenship data is "often outdated or incomplete" for foreign-born populations. Does not have comprehensive citizenship information for Americans born before 1978
3. **Relies on unreliable source databases** -- Automated Targeting System (ATS) and TECS incorporate FBI terrorism watchlist data, which the Brennan Center documents as containing "unreliable information -- including mistaken identities" and "bias against certain religious, racial, and ethnic minorities"
4. **SEVIS accuracy depends on decentralized university data entry**

### The Structural Fear: Voter Registration as Immigration Enforcement Data Trail

This is the analytically novel element. The SAVE Act creates a system where:

1. A citizen registers to vote
2. Their registration data is submitted to DHS
3. DHS runs it through SAVE, which cross-references immigration enforcement databases
4. If flagged (even erroneously), the citizen must produce citizenship documents or lose registration
5. The same DHS infrastructure is simultaneously being used for aggressive immigration enforcement

**The DOJ-DHS data pipeline:** The DOJ's Civil Rights Division has sent requests for voter registration information to at least 24 states, with complete voter roll lists demanded from at least 22 states (in at least one case threatening legal action for refusal). This data is being shared with DHS Homeland Security Investigations for use in "criminal and immigration probes."

**Separately:** DHS launched a nationwide campaign to investigate naturalized citizens who may have voted before completing naturalization. HSI agents in all field offices are required to review cases of suspected illegal voting by immigrants.

**The fear logic for mixed-status households:** If a naturalized citizen registers to vote, their data enters a DHS system that is actively being used to identify, investigate, and deport undocumented immigrants. A citizen in a mixed-status household faces a rational fear: could their voter registration data expose an undocumented family member? The SAVE system's documented high error rates make this fear more, not less, rational -- if the system regularly misidentifies citizens as noncitizens, what happens when it encounters a household where some members genuinely are noncitizens?

### The Anthony Nel Case

NPR (December 2025) documented the case of Anthony Nel, born in South Africa, naturalized as a teenager over a decade ago. Texas ran all registered voters through SAVE. Nel was flagged as a potential noncitizen. He received a notice requiring him to prove citizenship by showing his passport. He was uncomfortable sharing sensitive documents online, missed the deadline, and was removed from the voter rolls. He is no longer registered to vote.

Nel is exactly the type of citizen the SAVE Act would affect: foreign-born, naturalized, with documentation that the system failed to match. His case illustrates how the system converts eligible citizens into non-voters not through legal prohibition but through bureaucratic friction and rational caution.

## C. Current ICE Enforcement Climate (2025-2026)

### Scale of Operations

The current enforcement environment is historically unprecedented in scale:

- **ICE workforce:** More than doubled from ~10,000 to over 22,000 officers and agents as of early 2026 (120% increase announced January 2026)
- **Detention:** ~73,000 immigrants in ICE custody -- the highest number ever recorded, an 84% increase from January 2025. Over 70% have no criminal record; 95% have never been convicted of a violent crime
- **Directive:** White House ordered arrest of up to 3,000 immigrants per day
- **Operation Metro Surge (Minneapolis):** DHS called it "the largest immigration enforcement operation ever carried out" -- 3,000+ arrests, thousands of agents deployed

### Sensitive Locations Policy Rescinded

On January 20, 2025, within hours of taking office, Trump revoked the policy protecting schools, churches, hospitals, health clinics, domestic violence shelters, food pantries, playgrounds, bus stops, and other locations from immigration enforcement. ICE agents now operate at their discretion in formerly protected spaces.

Documented enforcement near sensitive locations since rescission:
- ICE targeting immigrants near **schools in Denver**
- Enforcement **outside churches in Washington, D.C.**
- ICE presence at **Aquila Elementary School** in Minneapolis during Operation Metro Surge, forcing the school to change pick-up procedures
- An apartment building across the street from the school under ICE surveillance

### Citizens Wrongfully Detained

The system's impact extends to U.S. citizens:

- **674 potential U.S. citizens** arrested by ICE, **121 detained**, **70 deported** during a period analyzed by a government watchdog
- ProPublica tallied at least **170 U.S. citizens** arrested or temporarily detained (acknowledged as an undercount)
- **Leonardo Garcia Venegas:** U.S.-born citizen, detained twice in construction-site sweeps. Agents assumed undocumented status based on appearance, treated his U.S. Real ID as "fake"
- **Jason Brian Gavidia:** U.S. citizen, handcuffed by Border Patrol at a tow-truck yard

**KFF/NYT 2025 Survey of Immigrants (n=1,805, August-October 2025):**

| Limited outdoor activities | 30% | 21% | 35% | 74% |

**Operation Metro Surge behavioral observations:**
- Commuters waiting around corners until buses arrive, then running home after disembarking
- Car horns and whistles used as community alert systems when ICE is active
- Children afraid to go to school; parents staying home from work
- Churches, schools, coffee shops, and stores became pop-up food and supply distribution centers for immigrants sheltering in place

## D. The Racialized Chilling Effect

### "Looking Like an Immigrant"

Immigration enforcement targeting one demographic group creates fear among citizens who share visible characteristics. This operates through:

1. **Racial profiling in enforcement:** Leonardo Garcia Venegas -- a U.S.-born citizen -- was detained twice because agents "assumed undocumented status based on appearance." His Real ID was treated as fake.

2. **Community-wide behavioral shifts:** When ICE operates in Latino or Asian neighborhoods, **all** residents change behavior, not just those without status. The KFF survey shows 21% of naturalized citizens -- people with full legal rights -- limiting their movements.

3. **The AAPI enforcement escalation:** The number of Asian people arrested by ICE has **more than tripled** under the current enforcement regime. Only 4% of international students report feeling "very" or "extremely" safe in the U.S. 88% of international students have scaled back political engagement due to anti-immigrant policies.

### Historical Evidence: Ethnically Targeted Enforcement Suppresses Civic Participation

**Japanese American internment (Komisarchik, Sen & Velez 2022):**
- State-targeted ethnic incarceration produced political disengagement lasting **generations**
- Effects increased with duration and intensity of enforcement
- Mechanism: **stigma and distrust of government**, not material barriers
- Directly analogous: SAVE creates a system where ethnically identifiable citizens are systematically scrutinized when exercising civic rights

**Post-9/11 Muslim and South Asian Americans:**
- NSEERS required registration of males from 25 countries (24 Muslim-majority)
- In one Brooklyn neighborhood, 50 students disappeared with families
- "Legal citizenship is clearly no longer enough to guarantee protection under the law"
- Post-9/11 policies "fostered a sense of alienation that made it harder for these communities to participate in elections and civic life"

**Police presence at polling places (Niven 2021, *Democracy and Security*):**
- First study to quantify law enforcement presence effects on U.S. voting
- 2017 Alabama Senate special election
- **32% reduction** in African American participation at polling places with police presence
- If African Americans are 10% of a precinct's registered voter base, police presence reduces their share of participants to 7%

### The Compound Racialized Effect

For the 2026 midterms, the racialized chilling effect layers:
1. Aggressive ICE enforcement visibly targeting Latino and Asian communities
2. Sensitive locations policy rescinded (enforcement near schools, churches)
3. SAVE database linking voter registration to DHS
4. DOJ demanding voter rolls from 22+ states for immigration probes
5. DHS investigating naturalized citizens for pre-naturalization voting
6. Steve Bannon publicly suggesting ICE agents "surround the polls"

Even though DHS officials have stated ICE will not be at polling places, the **perception** of threat matters more than the operational reality. The fear is already priced into community behavior -- 31% of naturalized citizens carrying proof of status at all times.

## F. Quantification Attempts

### Direct Quantification

No study has directly estimated the number of eligible voters deterred by fear (as opposed to documentation barriers) under the SAVE Act specifically. However, we can construct reasonable bounds from available data:

**Documentation barrier channel (better quantified):**
- Arizona Prop 200: blocked 31,500+ registration applicants (~12% of new registrants)
- Kansas proof-of-citizenship: prevented ~31,000 citizens from registering over its lifespan, while identifying only 39 noncitizens
- Brennan Center: 21.3 million Americans (9% of voting-age population) lack easy access to citizenship documents
- FactCheck.org/Bipartisan Policy Center: 12% of registered voters lack either a passport or birth certificate + photo ID

**Fear channel (this report's contribution to estimating):**

*Population at risk:*
- 10.6 million U.S. citizens live with an undocumented immigrant
- Subset who are eligible voters: estimated 5-6 million adults (excluding children and non-voting-age)
- Naturalized citizens with enforcement fear: 31% of 23.5 million naturalized citizen eligible voters = ~7.3 million experiencing fear
- Citizens limiting government interactions: 21% of naturalized citizens = ~4.9 million

*Expected behavioral impact:*
- Academic evidence: 5% reduction in registration propensity (Amuedo-Dorantes & Lopez 2017)
- Applying 5% to 5-6 million mixed-status household eligible voters = **250,000-300,000 voters potentially deterred from registration nationally**
- If fear effects are 2-3x higher in the current enforcement environment vs. the 2002-2014 study period (plausible given the scale escalation), the range becomes **500,000-900,000**

*Competitive state implications:*

| State | Estimated Naturalized Citizen Voters at Risk of Fear Suppression | 2020 Margin | Risk Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arizona | ~50,000-80,000 (9% naturalized, high enforcement) | 10,457 | EXTREME -- fear suppression alone could exceed margin |
| Georgia | ~40,000-70,000 (574K naturalized total) | 11,779 | EXTREME -- same dynamic |
| Nevada | ~60,000-90,000 (14% naturalized, highest %) | 33,596 | HIGH -- fear suppression significant fraction of margin |
| North Carolina | ~20,000-40,000 (lower concentration) | 74,483 | MODERATE -- insufficient alone but compounds |
| Michigan | ~15,000-30,000 (lower concentration) | 154,188 | LOW-MODERATE -- larger margin provides buffer |

*IMPORTANT CAVEAT: These are rough estimates extrapolating from pre-2025 academic findings to a substantially different enforcement environment. They should be treated as order-of-magnitude indicators, not precise predictions.*

### The Compound Effect

The **compound effect** of documentation barriers + fear suppression + ICE enforcement climate has not been studied as an integrated phenomenon. However:

- Documentation barriers: affect ~9-12% of eligible voters who lack documents
- Fear suppression: affects a potentially overlapping but distinct population (those who HAVE documents but are afraid to engage)
- ICE enforcement climate: amplifies fear suppression, potentially by multiples
- SAVE database linkage: creates a novel mechanism with no historical precedent
- Polling place intimidation fear: 32% reduction in minority participation with law enforcement presence (Niven 2021)

These channels are **multiplicative, not merely additive**. A citizen in a mixed-status household who also lacks easy access to documents faces both barriers simultaneously. The fear channel may deter some who would have overcome the documentation barrier if they were motivated to try.

## G. Counter-Evidence and Nuance

### Mobilization Evidence

The strongest counter-argument is that immigration enforcement **mobilizes** rather than suppresses:

- **Secure Communities:** 2-3 percentage point **increase** in county-level Latino turnout (White & Ariel 2016)
- **SB 1070:** Triggered massive organizational response in Arizona; Latino voter list tripled
- **California Prop 187 (1994):** Latino mobilization helped turn California permanently blue
- **2020 Muslim American turnout:** Registration rose from 60% to 78%
- **Post-SB 1070 partisan shift:** Arizona Latinos went from more Republican than national average to more Democratic, with 77% voting Obama in 2012

### Why Mobilization Doesn't Cancel Suppression

1. **Different populations:** Mobilization occurs among politically engaged citizens with organizational infrastructure. Suppression occurs among isolated individuals in mixed-status households. These are largely separate populations.

2. **Organizational infrastructure required:** SB 1070 mobilization required One Arizona, a major coalition. Secure Communities mobilization was driven by activist networks. Where organizational infrastructure is absent, threat produces withdrawal, not engagement.

3. **Ethnic identity strength matters:** Research shows Latinos with strong ethnic identities mobilize in response to threat; those with weaker ethnic identities do not. The SAVE Act's fear effects disproportionately target those with weaker connections to mobilization networks.

4. **Registration vs. turnout:** The Amuedo-Dorantes study found enforcement reduced **registration** but not **turnout among the registered**. Mobilization studies measure turnout. These are compatible -- mobilized voters turn out more, while potential voters never register.

### Intra-Latino Diversity

The Latino community is not monolithic in its response:

- **Cuban Americans:** 58% Republican, historically insulated from immigration enforcement through Cuban Adjustment Act. However, even Cuban American Republicans are souring on Trump's immigration policy -- 47% now say deportations are "going too far," up from 28% in March 2025.
- **Mexican Americans:** Lean heavily Democratic, long history of immigration policy salience, stronger connection to enforcement fears
- **Recent naturalized citizens:** Most vulnerable to fear effects (shortest history of civic participation, closest connections to immigration system)
- **Established multi-generation Latino citizens:** More likely to be organizationally connected and responsive to mobilization

### Does the Fear Effect Dissipate?

Evidence is mixed:

- **Short-term:** Immigration enforcement shocks produce immediate behavioral changes (census withdrawal, medical care avoidance, school attendance drops)
- **Medium-term:** Organizational mobilization can overcome initial fear (SB 1070 trajectory: initial shock --> organizational response --> sustained higher engagement)
- **Long-term:** Japanese American internment research shows ethnically targeted enforcement can produce **multigenerational** disengagement through stigma mechanisms
- **Current environment differs:** The SAVE database linkage creates a permanent structural connection between voting and enforcement that did not exist in prior enforcement episodes. It is not a one-time shock that can be organizationally overcome -- it is an ongoing system

### The Mobilization vs. Suppression Balance

The overall literature suggests:
- **When organizational infrastructure exists and enforcement is visible:** Mobilization dominates
- **When individuals are isolated and enforcement is proximate:** Suppression dominates
- **For registration (government-facing):** Suppression effect documented
- **For turnout (community-facing):** Mobilization effect documented
- **For the SAVE Act specifically:** The structural DHS linkage creates a new suppression mechanism that has no prior analog in the mobilization literature

## Analytical Implications for SD #9

### What This Means for the SAVE Act Deep Dive

1. **The fear channel is real, documented, and distinct from the documentation barrier.** It operates through rational behavioral responses to enforcement proximity, not ignorance or apathy.

2. **The SAVE database creates a structurally novel suppression mechanism.** No prior voter suppression tool has directly linked voter registration to immigration enforcement databases. This is not analogous to voter ID laws or proof-of-citizenship requirements alone -- it is a new category.

3. **The compound effect in competitive states is potentially decisive.** In Arizona and Georgia, where naturalized citizens constitute 7-9% of the electorate and margins are under 12,000 votes, even modest fear-driven suppression exceeds the margin.

4. **The mobilization counter-argument has limits.** It requires organizational infrastructure, strong ethnic identity, and visible threat. The SAVE Act's fear effects are diffuse, individual, and operate through bureaucratic systems rather than visible enforcement actions.

5. **The racial dimension is intrinsic, not incidental.** The system disproportionately flags foreign-born citizens (who are disproportionately non-white), operates in the context of enforcement that targets Latino and Asian communities, and creates an environment where "looking like an immigrant" is a civic liability.

6. **Historical precedent suggests long-term damage.** The Japanese American internment research shows that ethnically targeted enforcement by the state can suppress civic participation for generations. The current enforcement-voting linkage may produce similar lasting effects.

### Recommended Probability Framing

For the SD #9 analysis, the fear-based suppression channel should be treated as a **separate variable** from the documentation barrier, with its own probability estimate for impact on 2026 midterm outcomes. The evidence supports:

- **High confidence:** Fear-based suppression will reduce registration and participation among citizens in mixed-status households in enforcement-heavy states
- **Moderate confidence:** The scale of suppression in AZ, GA, and NV could affect competitive races
- **Lower confidence:** Precise quantification (the exact number of voters deterred) remains uncertain due to the novelty of the SAVE-enforcement linkage
- **Key uncertainty:** Whether organizational mobilization will offset suppression in these specific states in 2026
