## Signal Convergence

All eight workers converge on the following as established fact:

**The legal chain is real and complete.** *Learning Resources Inc v. Trump* (Feb 20, 2026) is a genuine SCOTUS ruling on the merits — statutory, not merely procedural. IEEPA's "regulate importation" language does not authorize tariffs. The ruling covers all IEEPA-based tariffs universally; it is not limited to the specific orders challenged. Section 232 and Section 301 authority are explicitly preserved.

**A domestic court refund mechanism now exists.** The CIT issued a reliquidation order in *Atmus* (March 4, 2026) directing CBP to reliquidate entries without IEEPA duties. This is the enforcement mechanism — it is what separates this situation from the Bush steel tariff precedent (WTO win + no domestic order = no refunds). Workers 4 and 6 both independently identify this as the critical differentiating variable.

**CBP acknowledged it cannot immediately comply.** CBP filed an affidavit on March 6, 2026 stating non-compliance with the reliquidation order and obtained a stay while building the CAPE refund processing platform, targeting approximately April 20 readiness. The court accepted this. CBP must report progress by March 19, 2026. As of the March 11 Orrick progress report, CAPE is approximately 70% complete on the portal and 40% complete on the reliquidation system.

**The administration is building Section 301 as a structural replacement.** USTR launched new Section 301 investigations immediately after the IEEPA ruling. This does not moot the refund obligation for duties already collected but signals the administration's intent to maintain tariff-equivalent pressure through IEEPA-immune authority.

**Historical precedent favors refunds when domestic court orders exist.** Nixon's TWEA surcharge (Yoshida, 1974) produced refunds. The Harbor Maintenance Tax (Shoe Corp.) produced refunds with interest. The common variable: a domestic court reliquidation order. The IEEPA situation now has one.

## Signal Divergence

**Kalshi and Polymarket are measuring fundamentally different things.** This is the most important finding in the research corpus and the primary source of analytical confusion. Deep Pull 1 confirms: the mismatch dominates, and traders appear to be pricing the title ("court orders") not the resolution bar ("importers receive"):

| Platform | Resolution trigger | Current price | Implication |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|-------------|
| Kalshi (KXTARIFFREFUND) | Court *orders* a refund | 66c | May already be YES — CIT ordered March 4 |
| Polymarket | Importers *actually receive* money | ~28–36% | Genuinely uncertain; requires operational execution |

Workers 1 and 2 identified this independently. The 66c Kalshi price may be pricing a near-certain legal event (court order already issued), not a probability question about actual payment. **The CAPE portal electronic setup rate makes this distinction operationally critical:** only 21,423 of 330,566 importers (6.5%) have completed electronic setup required to receive payment. Refunds to the remaining 93.5% would currently be rejected by the system even if CAPE launches on schedule.

> **⚠ Contradiction resolved:** The synthesis previously cited ~327,000 importers with ACH enrollment (~99%) as evidence the technical barrier was lower than the political narrative suggested. Deep pull clarifies these are distinct metrics: ACH enrollment (bank routing information on file with CBP, ~99%) vs. CAPE portal setup (registration with the new refund-processing system, ~6.5%). High ACH enrollment does not translate to immediate payment eligibility — importers must separately complete CAPE portal registration. The bull case was overstated on this factor.

**The slow-walk hypothesis is structurally coherent and now has explicit political confirmation.** Trump publicly stated the refund situation would be "litigated for the next two years." Treasury Secretary Bessent stated no payments would be made until CIT formally orders them — CIT has ordered them, but CBP is citing the incomplete CAPE system as justification for delay. Whether this is deliberate obstruction or operational constraint is analytically indistinguishable, and the political statements now tilt toward intentional.

**The 180-day protest clock divergence.** The clock for importers to file protest claims runs concurrent with the CAPE system build. Rights are being extinguished in real time for importers who don't file. This creates a distinction between "importers entitled to refunds" (shrinking as days pass without filings) and "importers who will eventually receive refunds" (a subset requiring successful navigation of the individual filing system). Protest filing rate data remains unavailable — the eligible refund pool size is unknown.

**Barrett's "mess" comment was at oral argument (Nov 5, 2025), not in opinion.** She flagged remedy complexity before the ruling, voted to strike anyway, and the majority said nothing about remedy in the opinion. Kavanaugh quoted her in dissent as evidence of majority irresponsibility. The Court made a deliberate choice: rule on the legal question, leave the $160-175B cleanup to CBP and lower courts.

**Section 122 as a wild card.** The 24-state coalition led by Oregon (filed March 5, 2026) challenges the Section 122 replacement tariffs on separate statutory grounds. If successful, the replacement tariff regime could also fall, compounding the refund exposure substantially. This track is entirely separate from the IEEPA litigation and was underweighted by most workers.

## Updated Assessment

### Bear case — No meaningful refunds before June 30 (45%)

*Upgraded from 40%. Political confirmation now explicit.*

The structural slow-walk is the most operational-reality-consistent scenario. CIT suspended its own order. CBP has no hard compliance deadline. Only 21,423 importers (6.5%) have completed CAPE portal setup — the other 93.5% cannot receive payment even if the system launches April 20. The administration's stated position is explicit obstruction: Trump's "litigated for two years" framing and Bessent's statement that payments require CIT's formal order (which exists, yet CBP continues to cite CAPE incompleteness) signal coordinated delay. The 2-year statutory window is fully available without defying any court.

**Trigger for bear resolution:** CAPE launches late, or launches but processes trivially small volume. No hard court deadline imposed at March 19 hearing.

### Base case — Partial, token, or definitional refunds (35%)

*Unchanged from 35%. Structure remains consistent.*

CBP launches CAPE, processes the 21,423 already-enrolled importers plus a second wave of completions before June 30. Kalshi resolves YES on the "court order" criterion — may already be YES. Polymarket resolves NO because the "importers actually receive money" criterion is not met at sufficient scale. Litigation over the scope of the stay and what counts as "compliance" drags into Q3 2026.

**Trigger:** CAPE operational, small-to-medium processing volume, platform-dependent resolution.

### Bull case — Substantial refunds before June 30 (20%)

*Downgraded from 25%. CAPE portal setup rate undermines the technical optimism.*

CIT imposes a hard compliance deadline at the March 19 progress hearing or thereafter. CBP expedites CAPE and processes enrolled importers at scale under contempt threat. Political calculation shifts: refund payments become a policy constituency tool. Courts reject further stays. **Key constraint:** even in the bull case, the 93.5% without portal setup creates a hard processing ceiling — the bull case now requires both legal pressure AND rapid onboarding of the unenrolled, not just CAPE launch.

**Trigger:** March 19 CIT hearing produces hard deadline with contempt exposure; CAPE launches on schedule with mass portal enrollment drive.

### Overall assessment

**Revised base estimate for "some importers actually receive money before June 30": 35–38%**

*Revised down from 38–42%. Deep pulls confirm the prior was at the high end.*

Deep Pull 0 recommends revising toward 30–35% on operational bottlenecks. Deep Pull 1 finds 42% "defensible and may still be generous." The primary driver of the revision is the CAPE portal setup rate (6.5% enrolled vs. the prior synthesis's implicit assumption that 99% ACH enrollment translated to payment readiness). The Trump/Bessent political statements add to the bear case weight.

**Do not use 42% in print without flagging this context.** The revised range is 35–38%, with credible arguments for 30–35% depending on how March 19 resolves.

**The Kalshi/Polymarket trade is structurally interesting but requires contract language confirmation before acting.** If Kalshi resolves on "court orders refund" (already issued) rather than "importers receive money," the 66c vs. 35–38% divergence is primarily a measurement mismatch, not a mispricing.
