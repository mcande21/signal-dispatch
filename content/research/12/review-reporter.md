## Sourcing Rate

**Headline figure: approximately 80-85% of material claims are sourced. That is strong for opinion-adjacent long-form. It is not strong enough for a piece making this many specific numerical assertions.**

The sourcing architecture is genuinely good. Primary documents -- FERC dockets, CPUC decisions, PUCN IRP filings, Senate LDA disclosures, FEC data, ProPublica 990s, case citations -- are named with specificity. An editor can verify the docket numbers. A reader can pull the filings. That is the right approach.

What is unsourced or undersourced:

**1. The 78% / 40-60% capacity request figures.**
The piece states data centers account for 5,900 MW of 7,600 MW in NV Energy's capacity queue -- "78% of all capacity requests" -- and then applies "49-85% planning discounts" to arrive at "40-60% of incorporated load growth." The 2024 Joint IRP is cited as the source. But the planning discount percentages (49-85%) appear without a citation to a specific section of the IRP. A reader cannot verify whether those are NV Energy's own discount factors or the author's interpretation. This needs a direct citation to the IRP table or section.

**2. The $457 million / 300 jobs data center abatement figure.**
"Approximately $457 million in cumulative abatements for an estimated 300 permanent jobs -- roughly $1.5 million in public subsidy per permanent position." The footnote references the Tesla comparison but does not cite the source for the $457M and 300-job figures. NRS 360.754 is cited as the enabling statute but not as a data source. The Governor's Office of Economic Development (GOED) publishes abatement data. If that is the source, it should be named. If the figure is estimated, the methodology should be disclosed.

**3. The "approximately 70% / 30%" Greenlink West cost allocation.**
Residential ratepayers bear "approximately 70% by Southern Nevada customers and 30% by Northern Nevada customers, translating to $4 or more per month for typical households and $25 or more during peak usage periods." No source is cited for these specific figures. This is a significant consumer-impact claim. It needs a PUCN order, an IRP filing section, or a news account with attribution.

**4. The 4,000 MW / 5,000 MW Greenlink West capacity allocation.**
"NV Energy has committed 4,000 MW of the line's 5,000 MW total capacity to data center customers -- Switch, Novva, and others." The source of the 4,000 MW commitment figure is not cited. NV Energy press releases? An IRP? This is load-bearing.

**5. The 267% / 80% Virginia wholesale and retail price increase figures.**
"In Virginia, residents near the Loudoun County data center corridor have seen wholesale electricity prices increase 267% and residential bills rise 80%." No source. These are high-visibility numbers that will be challenged. They need a primary source -- EIA, PJM market monitor report, or a named published study.

**6. FERC Chair Swett's "too big to function" quote.**
The piece attributes a public statement to "FERC Chair Swett" but does not say where it was made -- testimony, press conference, interview. "Said publicly this month" is not enough for a direct quote from a sitting federal commissioner.

**7. The Trump administration "Ratepayer Protection Pledge."**
"The Trump administration...signed a Ratepayer Protection Pledge with all major tech companies in March 2026." This is a specific named document. If it exists, it needs a citation -- White House, Federal Register, press release. If this is a characterization of a broader energy agreement, say so. As written, it reads as a formal named document, and the reader should be able to find it.

**8. Danielle Hughes' role.**
Hughes is identified as CEO of "Tahoe Spark" and "a supervisor with the California Energy Commission's Efficiency Division." These are two separate positions. If she holds both simultaneously, that is unusual and should be noted. If one is current and one is former, the construction is misleading.

**9. The EPRI 60% upward revision.**
"EPRI's 2026 demand projections for data centers are 60% higher than their own 2024 estimates." EPRI is cited as an industry data source in the sources block, but no specific report title or publication date is given. The piece distinguishes "2024 estimates" from "2026 projections" -- the reader needs enough information to find both documents.

**10. "More data center projects were rejected or cancelled in Q1 2026 than in all of 2025."**
No source. This is a trend claim that would require either an industry tracker, trade press database, or a named report. As written it is asserted.

## Weasel Sourcing and Attribution Problems

**Two legitimate weasel issues and one structural problem:**

**Issue 1: NV Energy's "first in the waiting line" claim.**
"NV Energy told South Tahoe Now that Liberty would be 'first in the waiting line' for Greenlink capacity." South Tahoe Now is cited in the sources block. That is acceptable. However, the piece immediately follows this with the author's counter-analysis -- 80% committed, designed to serve the displacing customers -- without telling the reader whether NV Energy disputed the characterization. The reader gets one side's quote and the other side's framing. Fair procedure requires noting whether NV Energy was asked to respond to the capacity commitment figures.

**Issue 2: Liberty's financial incentives paragraph.**
"Liberty's financial incentives are not obviously aligned with fighting the termination." This is an inference -- a reasonable one -- but it is stated as analysis without the word "I assess" or an equivalent attribution. The motive attribution is implicit. The draft's own stated standard is "no unsourced motive attributions." This passage does not technically attribute motive, but it stops just short of doing so in a way that an editor at a major outlet would flag. Adding "I assess" or "the financial structure suggests" would fix it.

**Issue 3: The footnote numbering is broken.**
The draft uses `[^2]` in the body (Incentives section) but the footnote is labeled `[^1]` at the bottom. There is no `[^1]` reference in the body. This means either a citation was dropped from the body or the footnote was mislabeled. Either way, an editor catches it.

## Framing Assessment

**This piece is predominantly analysis. It is not advocacy. But it tilts -- and the tilt is identifiable.**

The framing is structurally fair in the following ways: NV Energy's position is represented through named spokespersons. The contract structure analysis is accurate and acknowledges that NV Energy is acting legally. Liberty Utilities' financial misalignment is aired openly, complicating the simple victim narrative. The "steel-man defense of NV Energy" is explicitly labeled and engaged.

The tilt shows up in three places:

**1. The proximate cause framing.**
"The contract was always going to end. Data centers determined when." This is the piece's thesis, and it is stated as fact rather than as the author's assessment. The steel-man defense paragraph acknowledges the tension but does not fully resolve it. An editor would ask: is there contemporaneous evidence from before 2024 that NV Energy's termination decision was accelerated by data center demand, beyond Liberty's own advice letter? If the answer is yes, it should be in the piece. If the answer is no, the framing needs hedging.

**2. The asymmetry of the Incentives section.**
The piece documents BHE lobbying, Data Center Coalition lobbying, and Vantage lobbying in detail, then notes the other side of the table is empty. This is accurate and newsworthy. But the section does not ask why it is empty. Liberty Utilities -- the party with direct financial standing -- has not filed at FERC. The Sierra Club has not filed at FERC. The California AG has not petitioned. The piece implies this absence is a resource and attention problem. That may be true. It may also be that attorneys for these parties have reviewed the filing options and made a strategic decision not to file. A journalist asks whether anyone declined to comment on why no FERC complaint has been filed.

**3. The community demographics section.**
The demographics of South Lake Tahoe are accurately cited (Census Bureau or American Community Survey -- source not named in the sources block, but this is standard enough that most editors would accept it). The framing is not wrong. But the piece transitions from demographics to "the housekeepers, lift operators, restaurant servers, and maintenance workers who keep the tourism economy running" without a source for the occupational claim. That is color writing, not analysis. It is fine for an opinion column. A reported piece needs the data behind it.

**Political spectrum test:** A reader skeptical of utility regulation would recognize the steel-man is genuine. A reader sympathetic to data center development would find the Incentives section imbalanced -- the PAC and lobbying data are presented as evidence of policy capture, but no one from NV Energy, BHE, or the Data Center Coalition is quoted responding to that characterization. The piece would be stronger with a "NV Energy declined to comment" or a specific NV Energy counter-argument on the capacity/marketing contradiction.

## Editorial Flags

**What an editor sends back:**

1. **The Greenlink West capacity commitment needs a source.** 4,000 of 5,000 MW is the structural fact that makes the ratepayer cross-subsidy argument work. Without a citation, the piece's strongest original finding is unverified.

2. **The cost allocation figures need a PUCN docket or order.** "$4 or more per month for typical households and $25 or more during peak usage periods" are vivid consumer-impact numbers. They need a source that can be verified independently.

3. **The Ratepayer Protection Pledge needs a citation or reframing.** If the document exists, cite it. If it is a characterization of a broader agreement, say so explicitly.

4. **The Virginia price figures need a source.** 267% wholesale / 80% retail are high-visibility numbers in a national context claim. They will be challenged.

5. **FERC Chair Swett's quote needs a venue.** Direct quotes from federal commissioners require attribution to a specific proceeding, speech, or interview.

6. **The footnote numbering error must be fixed before publication.**

7. **"Data centers determined when" -- hedge or support.** Either find the internal NV Energy document or contemporaneous statement that confirms demand acceleration, or frame this as the author's assessment.

8. **Was NV Energy or BHE given an opportunity to respond to the capacity/marketing contradiction?** The piece presents their own marketing materials against their capacity claims without noting whether they were asked to reconcile the two.

9. **Danielle Hughes' dual-role description needs clarification.**

**What would not come back:**
- The legal architecture analysis is clean and well-grounded. The case citations are real and the El Paso Electric precedent is directly on point.
- The contract history is documented and fair.
- The probability estimates are appropriately hedged with resolution criteria. They will raise eyebrows at a mainstream outlet but are defensible as disclosed methodology.
- The Liberty financial incentive analysis is the sharpest original observation in the piece and it is handled with appropriate restraint.

**Conflict of interest:** The disclosure statement ("I do not currently trade this analysis") is present. That is good practice. No other conflicts are apparent from the text.

## The Story

**Is this news?** Yes, on three counts: (1) the FERC filing mechanism -- how the November 2025 amendment passed in a routine combined notice with a 21-day comment window around Thanksgiving -- is an original observation that has not appeared in the *Fortune* or *Ars Technica* coverage; (2) Liberty's financial misalignment as a structural barrier to FERC intervention is original analysis not present in prior coverage; (3) the Greenlink West capacity commitment figure (if sourced) is an original finding that makes the ratepayer cross-subsidy concrete.

**What does this add?** The prior coverage (Fortune, Ars Technica, Futurism) treated this as a "utility chose data centers over people" story. This piece correctly identifies that framing as incomplete and locates the structural cause in the wholesale/retail seam. That is a genuine contribution. The FERC intervention analysis and the Liberty incentive structure are not present in prior coverage.

**Single most publishable finding:** The Thanksgiving filing mechanism. A utility terminated service to 49,000 people by amending a contract in a routine combined notice the week before Thanksgiving, during a 21-day comment window, with zero protests. That is the story that a national editor picks up and a congressional staffer reads twice.

**What is buried:** The Liberty financial misalignment analysis is in Section 5 (The Community), which is not where a reader expects structural analysis. This is the sharpest original claim in the piece and it is surrounded by demographic context. It belongs earlier -- it is a key reason the FERC tools have not been used, and that connects directly to the central thesis.

**What should be higher:** The El Paso Electric precedent and the FERC tools available. The piece buries the legal analysis in the final section. A reader who stops at Section 4 (The Capacity) understands the problem but not the solution. The legal architecture section -- which establishes that FERC has authority and has not been asked to use it -- is the piece's central insight and it arrives too late.

## Summary

This is well-reported by newsletter standards. The primary document sourcing is better than most commentary that touches regulatory law. The structural analysis -- the wholesale/retail seam, the asset sale conversion, the FERC tools available -- is sound.

The piece has approximately ten specific numerical claims that need source verification before a major outlet would accept them. Some are verifiable from the cited documents (the IRP is cited; the planning discount factors need a section reference). Others -- Greenlink West capacity commitment, Ratepayer Protection Pledge, Virginia price figures -- need new citations or disclosure that they are the author's estimates.

The Liberty financial misalignment finding is the most original and most underplaced claim in the draft. It changes what the piece is about: not just a utility choosing profits over people, but a regulatory gap that persists in part because the nominal advocate for the affected community has its own reasons not to file.

The Thanksgiving filing is the news hook. Neither the Liberty misalignment nor the Thanksgiving mechanism appears prominently in prior coverage. Both are real contributions.

**Sourcing rate:** ~80-85% of material claims sourced; ~10 claims need verification or disclosure
**Framing:** Analysis, not advocacy -- but with identifiable tilt in proximate-cause framing and asymmetric lobbying presentation
**Biggest editorial flag:** Greenlink West capacity commitment (4,000/5,000 MW) -- load-bearing claim, no source
**Buried lede:** Liberty's financial misalignment as structural barrier to FERC action
**My headline:** "Termination Notice: How a Routine FERC Filing Left 49,000 Lake Tahoe Residents Without a Power Company"
**Wire cut:** Thanksgiving mechanism + wholesale/retail seam + 0 MW constraint + Liberty misalignment + FERC tools unused = 800 words
