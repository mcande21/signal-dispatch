# F.3 Contract Terms Investigation: NV Energy / Liberty Utilities Wholesale Power Supply Agreement

**Issue:** SD #12
**Status:** Complete -- synthesized from CPUC decisions, regulatory filings, corporate disclosures, and press reporting

## The 2009 Asset Sale

### Transaction Structure

On April 23, 2009, NV Energy announced that its subsidiary Sierra Pacific Power Company had entered an agreement to sell its California electric distribution and generation assets to California Pacific Electric Company (CalPeco), a newly formed entity. CalPeco was jointly owned by Algonquin Power & Utilities Corp. (50.001%) and Emera Inc. (49.999%), held through their respective subsidiaries Liberty Electric Co. and Emera US Holdings, Inc.

**Key terms:**
- Purchase price: $116 million (announced); closed January 1, 2011 at approximately $132 million (included premium on net rate base plus net working capital adjustment)
- Assets transferred: California electric distribution and generation assets serving approximately 47,000 customers in seven counties (Nevada, Placer, Sierra, Plumas, Mono, Alpine, El Dorado), with ~80% in the Lake Tahoe Basin
- Generation assets included: Kings Beach diesel generating station (12 MW, emergency backup only)
- Assets NOT transferred: NV Energy's transmission system, generation portfolio, or Nevada operations

### The Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) -- Original 2011-2015 Term

The asset sale included a **Power Purchase Agreement** (Exhibit 10 to the transfer application, A.09-10-028) as one of seven Operating Agreements integral to the transaction. CPUC Decision D.10-10-017 (October 14, 2010) describes the PPA in detail:

> "The Power Purchase Agreement ensures delivery of CalPeco's full requirements, including 20% from renewable sources eligible for California's Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS), at rates reflecting Sierra's actual costs and based on Sierra's system-average cost, for an initial term of **five years**."

Additional PPA features from the CPUC decision:
- CalPeco received "certain rights to develop and/or procure other renewable sources during the five-year term"
- The PPA provided "an additional, five-year right to obtain power from Sierra in an amount up to CalPeco's full requirements for nonrenewable sources" -- this is a second five-year option, suggesting the original agreement contemplated a maximum 10-year supply period (2011-2020)
- Power from Sierra's Valmy coal plant was permitted in the supply mix for the initial five-year term
- "Ongoing transmission will be negotiated in accordance with federal law on non-discriminatory, open access transmission and Sierra's Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) tariffs"

**Critical finding:** The CPUC decision language -- "initial term of five years" with "an additional, five-year right" -- establishes that the power supply obligation was contractual and time-limited from inception, NOT an open-ended condition of the asset sale. The original framework envisioned supply through 2015, with a CalPeco option extending to 2020 at most.

### Was Power Supply a Condition of the Asset Sale?

Yes, effectively. The PPA was one of seven Operating Agreements "integral to the proposed transfer" that the CPUC reviewed and approved as part of the transaction. Without the PPA, CalPeco could not have served the 47,000 California customers, and the CPUC would not have approved the transfer. The CPUC decision notes that "electric power throughout the entire service territory will continue to move into California from Nevada or elsewhere outside California over the same facilities as it does now."

However, the CPUC did not impose the PPA as a permanent obligation. The agreement was structured with defined terms (5 years + 5-year option), giving CalPeco time to develop independent supply sources. NV Energy's spokesperson Katie Jo Collier's characterization that the transition was "a planned transition for many years" based on "the original 2009 understanding" is consistent with this structure.

## SEC/Corporate Filings

### Algonquin / Liberty Energy Disclosures

Algonquin Power & Utilities Corp. (TSX: AQN) was the ultimate parent of Liberty Utilities through the full history of the NV Energy supply arrangement. (Algonquin has since renamed to Liberty Energy.) Key corporate disclosures:

**Liberty CalPeco's 2018 Affiliate Transaction Compliance Plan** (Advice Letter 99-E-A, filed with CPUC July 2018) states:
> "The service territory of Liberty CalPeco is located entirely within the NV Energy Balancing Authority."

This confirms the complete dependence on NV Energy's grid infrastructure. The filing also confirms that by 2018, Algonquin was Liberty CalPeco's ultimate parent, with Liberty Utilities Co. as the immediate parent company.

**Liberty CalPeco's 2024 IRP Supplement** (Advice Letter 239-E, filed May 1, 2024) explicitly describes the supply arrangement:
> "Under the Energy Supply Agreement ('ESA') with NV Energy, Liberty receives unspecified power to the extent Liberty-owned renewables do not meet the immediate customer demands."

This filing also describes Liberty's only owned generation asset:
> "Kings Beach diesel-fueled generating plant that provides emergency reliability services only and thus on a forecast basis provides zero net generation."

**What's NOT in public filings:** The actual dollar amounts of the wholesale supply contract, the specific expiration dates of the extensions, or detailed termination notice provisions have not been located in publicly available SEC or CPUC filings. The contracts themselves appear to be filed confidentially with regulators.

## CPUC Proceedings

### CPUC Decision D.10-10-017 (October 14, 2010) -- The Foundational Approval

The CPUC conditionally approved the transfer of Sierra Pacific's California assets to CalPeco. Key regulatory conditions:

1. **The PPA was reviewed and approved** as part of the transaction (not imposed separately)
2. Valmy coal plant power was permitted in the PPA's supply mix for the initial 5-year term
3. Internal transfers of CalPeco's ownership required CPUC approval (to prevent flipping)
4. CalPeco and its upstream owners had to recognize CPUC jurisdiction
5. "Regulatory Commitments" (Appendix 3) bound CalPeco and its parents to service quality standards

The CPUC explicitly found: "Joint Applicants have established that the transfer will not harm ratepayers; in fact, certain service improvements are likely in the near term, at no cost to ratepayers."

### Liberty's 2015 Rate Case / 2016 NV Energy Services Agreement Application

Liberty's 2015 GRC filing (CPUC proceedings) provides the most detailed publicly available description of the contract structure. The filing states:

> "Liberty Utilities procures almost all of the remainder of its power through a 'full requirements' power purchase agreement with Sierra Pacific Power Company dba NV Energy ('Existing NV Energy Services Agreement')."

> "The Existing NV Energy Services terminates as of December 31, 2015."

On April 24, 2015, Liberty filed Application 15-04-019 requesting CPUC approval of a new agreement -- the **2016 NV Energy Services Agreement** -- executed on April 21, 2014.

### Liberty's Solar Projects Application (2015) -- Most Detailed Contract Description

Liberty's CPUC testimony for its Luning/Minden solar projects (Application 15-04-016) provides the most granular detail on the NV Energy supply arrangement:

> "Liberty Utilities has procured essentially all of its electrical energy, including its renewable energy, from Sierra Pacific Power Company dba NV Energy through a five-year service agreement ('Existing NV Energy Services Agreement'). The Existing NV Energy Services Agreement shall terminate on December 31, 2015."

On the 2016 replacement agreement:

> "Liberty Utilities and NV Energy have agreed to a new agreement to become effective on January 1, 2016 ('2016 NV Energy Services Agreement')."

> "If approved by the Commission, the 2016 NV Energy Services Agreement will obligate NV Energy to continue to serve the full requirements of Liberty Utilities' electric loads, but will also allow Liberty Utilities to replace some of the NV Energy supply with RPS-eligible energy generated by Liberty Utilities-owned generating facilities (i.e., the Solar Projects)."

> "In addition to delivering almost 100 percent of Liberty Utilities' physical energy, the Existing NV Energy Services Agreement also provides Liberty Utilities with 20 percent of its total energy from RPS sources."

> "NV Energy will sell Liberty Utilities RPS energy from its purchases from a pool of five RPS-certified geothermal resources."

The testimony reveals that Liberty negotiated the 2016 agreement with two key objectives:
1. "Obtaining the flexibility to procure renewable energy from sources other than NV Energy (i.e., the Solar Projects and third party renewable generation within its service territory)"
2. "Reducing the 'Demand Charge' portion of its payment obligation to NV Energy to account for Liberty Utilities' procurement of capacity from sources other than NV Energy"

The result: "Liberty Utilities is able to save its customers approximately $1.2 million each year through reductions in the Demand Charge."

The 2016 agreement also obligated NV Energy to "deliver any energy Liberty Utilities obtains from the Solar Projects over the NV Energy transmission facilities into Liberty Utilities' California service territory at FERC regulated cost-based rates."

### March 2026 Advice Letter

In March 2026, Liberty filed an advice letter with the CPUC seeking authorization for an expedited Request for Proposals (RFP) process to replace the NV Energy supply. Key points from the filing (as reported):

- NV Energy cited "data centers in the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center area and northern Nevada transmission constraints, among other reasons, for ending full-requirements service"
- Liberty's grid "sits inside NV Energy's balancing authority, connects to NV Energy at 38 points, and relies entirely on Nevada transmission lines"
- Liberty seeks to issue a formal RFP in summer 2026 for replacement power beginning June 1, 2027
- Liberty prioritizes lowest-cost power meeting California's renewable energy requirements

### Sierra Club / Tahoe Spark Protests

In an April 1, 2026 letter to CPUC commissioners, Sierra Club Vice Chair Tobi Tyler argued that "the scale of the procurement -- affecting 49,000 ratepayers dependent on an isolated, rapidly transforming grid -- demands the transparency and public participation that only a formal proceeding provides."

Tahoe Spark's protest states: "California does not produce a Liberty-specific forecast of demand, peak conditions, or procurement needed for numerous California communities in a high wildfire risk area."

South Lake Tahoe Mayor Cody Bass wrote to the CPUC in April 2026, noting locals are "tracking closely" the situation.

Danielle Hughes (CEO Tahoe Spark, California Energy Commission supervisor) has called for a full CPUC proceeding rather than an expedited RFP process.

## Contract Extension History

### Timeline Reconstruction

| Period | Agreement | Term | Notes |
|--------|-----------|------|-------|
| Jan 1, 2011 - Dec 31, 2015 | Original PPA (Exhibit 10 to Asset Sale) | 5 years | Full requirements at Sierra's system-average cost; 20% RPS from NV Energy geothermal pool |
| Jan 1, 2016 - ~Dec 2020 | 2016 NV Energy Services Agreement (App. 15-04-019) | ~5 years | Executed April 21, 2014; included flexibility for Liberty solar self-supply; reduced Demand Charge |
| ~2021 - ~Late 2025 | Extension (details unclear) | ~5 years | Extended because Liberty had not secured independent supply |
| Late 2025 - May 2027 | Final extension | ~18 months | NV Energy set firm end date of May 2027; no further extensions |

**Pattern:** Each agreement ran approximately five years. The first two transitions (2015 and 2020) were consensual -- Liberty had not yet secured alternatives and NV Energy agreed to continue. Fortune's reporting, corroborated by regulatory documents, confirms the extensions occurred "each time because Liberty had not yet secured an independent supply."

**The late 2025 extension was different:** NV Energy set a firm end date and stated it would not extend again. This coincides with: (a) the Greenlink West transmission line expected completion in May 2027, which would give Liberty access to alternative suppliers; and (b) accelerating data center load growth in northern Nevada.

## Replacement Power Costs

### Infrastructure Challenge

Liberty's service territory is physically isolated from the California grid. Key facts:

- Liberty's grid sits entirely within the **NV Energy Balancing Authority**, NOT the CAISO system
- The grid connects to NV Energy at **38 points**
- There is **no direct connection to California's Independent System Operator** grid
- All power must flow over NV Energy's transmission system

### Cost Estimates

**Direct California grid connection:** Liberty President Eric Schwarzrock stated a new transmission line from California's grid (e.g., from the El Dorado Hills area) "would cost hundreds of millions of dollars" with "significant land impacts." This option was considered and rejected.

**Greenlink West as alternative pathway:** NV Energy's $4.2 billion Greenlink West transmission project (525-kV line from Las Vegas to Yerington) is expected online in May 2027. Liberty says it will be "first in the waiting line" for access to a broader supplier pool. Approximately 70% of Greenlink West costs will be borne by Southern Nevada customers. This is the primary pathway Liberty is banking on for replacement supply.

**Rate impact already experienced:** Liberty customers have seen approximately 100% rate increases over the last four years. The 2025 general rate case sought a 19.1% revenue increase ($37.51/month for average residential customer); the CPUC approved 11.4%. Contributing factors include wildfire mitigation, insurance premiums exceeding $30 million annually, and infrastructure upgrades.

**Competitive disadvantage:** As Danielle Hughes (Tahoe Spark) noted: "Short term, you can commonly get good deals, but it's unstable. The short-term deal gets you through. But then you're in the Western market, competing against PG&E, Southern California Edison, data centers, and mining companies. We're 49,000 customers. We have no leverage."

## KEY CONTRACT QUESTIONS -- ANSWERED OR STILL OPEN

### Fixed expiration date?
**YES.** The original PPA had a 5-year fixed term (Jan 1, 2011 - Dec 31, 2015). The 2016 replacement agreement also had a defined term (commencing Jan 1, 2016). The 2020 extension and late 2025 extension likewise had defined endpoints. The current final deadline is May 2027. Each agreement appears to have been a fixed-term arrangement, not an evergreen contract.

### Notice period required?
**UNKNOWN.** The specific notice provisions of the current agreement have not been located in public filings. The late 2025 extension set May 2027 as the hard stop, giving approximately 18 months of advance notice. The March 2026 CPUC advice letter provides the public notification to regulators. Whether NV Energy was required to give formal notice under FERC regulations (18 C.F.R. 2.4(c)(4)) remains an open question flagged in F.1.

### Capacity reservation clauses?
**PARTIAL.** The 2016 agreement clearly included a "Demand Charge" component, and Liberty successfully negotiated reductions to that charge to account for its solar self-supply. This implies the contract had capacity/demand components, not just energy charges. Whether NV Energy reserved specific generation capacity for Liberty or simply served from its system portfolio is unclear.

### FERC-filed rate schedule or bilateral?
**LIKELY BILATERAL WITH FERC COST-BASED RATES.** The CPUC decision describes the PPA rates as reflecting "Sierra's actual costs" at "Sierra's system-average cost." The 2016 agreement required NV Energy to wheel Liberty's solar energy "at FERC regulated cost-based rates." Transmission services are governed by Sierra Pacific's FERC Open Access Transmission Tariff (OATT). The wholesale supply agreement itself appears to be a bilateral contract between the parties, but subject to FERC jurisdiction as an interstate wholesale sale. Whether NV Energy filed the agreement with FERC as a rate schedule (Section 205) or operates under market-based rate authority is not confirmed in public sources.

### Was power supply a condition of the asset sale?
**YES, FUNCTIONALLY -- BUT NOT PERMANENTLY.** The PPA was integral to the CPUC-approved asset transfer. Without it, the sale would not have been approved -- CalPeco had no other way to serve the 47,000 customers. But the CPUC decision contemplated a defined transition period (5 years + 5-year option = maximum 10 years through 2020). The agreement was always described as temporary, with Liberty expected to develop independent supply. Three extensions beyond the original framework suggest Liberty was slower to achieve independence than the parties anticipated.

### Was there a renewable energy obligation?
**YES.** Both the original PPA and the 2016 agreement required NV Energy to supply RPS-eligible renewable energy (initially 20% from a pool of five geothermal resources). The 2016 agreement also allowed Liberty to self-supply renewables and reduce its NV Energy dependence accordingly.

## ANALYTICAL NOTES

### The "Planned Transition" vs. "Data Center Redirect" Narratives

Both narratives are partially true, and the evidence supports neither as the complete picture:

**Evidence supporting "planned transition":**
- The original 2009 PPA was explicitly temporary (5-year term)
- The CPUC decision contemplated CalPeco developing independent supply
- Three extensions over 16 years suggest the transition was slower than planned but always anticipated
- NV Energy spokesperson: "well before data center load growth was a consideration"

**Evidence supporting "data center redirect":**
- The timing: NV Energy agreed to another extension in late 2025 but then set a firm end date coinciding with massive data center demand projections
- Liberty's own CPUC filing states NV Energy cited "data centers in the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center area and northern Nevada transmission constraints"
- NV Energy's 2024 IRP shows 5,900 MW of potential data center demand in northern Nevada by 2033
- Data centers consumed 22% of Nevada's electricity in 2024, projected to reach 35% by 2030
- NV Energy needs 47% more energy than forecast two years ago -- this spike is driven by data centers

**The synthesis:** The contract was always going to end. The question is whether data center demand accelerated the timeline and prevented further extensions that NV Energy might otherwise have granted. The evidence suggests the answer is yes. NV Energy extended three times over 16 years -- each time because Liberty had not yet found alternatives. The refusal to extend a fourth time coincides precisely with the data center demand explosion. The framing of "planned transition" is factually correct about the contract structure but misleading about the proximate cause of the hard stop.

### The Greenlink West Coupling

A critical and underexamined detail: Liberty's contract deadline (May 2027) aligns exactly with the expected completion of Greenlink West (May 2027). This suggests the final extension was calibrated to give Liberty access to the new transmission line as a replacement supply pathway. This is either: (a) a thoughtful transition plan coordinated between NV Energy and Liberty, or (b) a coincidence that gives NV Energy political cover. The charitable reading is that NV Energy set the deadline to match when Liberty would have infrastructure access to alternatives. The less charitable reading is that Greenlink West is primarily being built for data center load, and Liberty's access is incidental.

### The FERC Gap

F.1 identified that under 18 C.F.R. 2.4(c)(4), NV Energy should file notice of termination with FERC for any wholesale service cancellation. No such filing has been identified in public reporting. This remains the most significant unexplored regulatory angle. If FERC has not been engaged, it may be because: (a) the contract expires by its own terms and NV Energy considers no filing necessary, (b) a filing was made but has not received media attention, or (c) there is a regulatory gap. A FERC complaint by Liberty, the Sierra Club, or the CPUC could change the dynamics entirely.
