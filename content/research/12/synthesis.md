## 1. Signal-by-Signal Interpretation

### F.1: Legal Landscape -- Obligation to Serve and the Wholesale Gap

The foundational research establishes the central legal distinction that makes this entire story possible. Every U.S. state has an obligation-to-serve statute binding utilities at the retail level. But at the wholesale level -- where NV Energy sells power to Liberty Utilities for resale to 49,000 Lake Tahoe customers -- the obligation is contractual, not statutory. The Energy Bar Association's seminal framework (1985, updated by Jermyn 2025) confirms this: wholesale service can be terminated upon contract expiration, subject to FERC review. The Supreme Court's Jackson v. Metropolitan Edison (1974) further weakened the constitutional safety net by ruling that private utility service termination is not state action, even for monopoly providers of essential services.

**What this means for the thesis:** The gap between retail obligation and wholesale discretion is not an accident or an oversight. It is a structural feature of American electricity law that was tolerable when wholesale relationships were stable and capacity was abundant. Data center demand has turned this gap into a chasm. NV Energy's legal position -- that it is simply declining to renew a voluntary contract -- is technically correct. That is the point.

**Confidence:** HIGH. The legal framework is well-documented across multiple authoritative sources. The wholesale/retail distinction is not disputed by any source.

### F.2/F.2b/F.2c: FERC Filing Status and the November Amendment

This is the investigation's most significant original finding. Three rounds of progressively deeper FERC research produced a layered picture:

- **F.2** found no FERC filing for the termination of the Liberty PPA. This was initially interpreted as a potential regulatory violation.
- **F.2b** corrected the framework. It discovered ER26-534-000 -- a November 17, 2025 FERC filing titled "Amended Liberty PPA," effective December 29, 2025. This is an amendment, not a cancellation. It also identified the correct regulatory framework: 18 CFR 35.15, which exempts post-1996 power sales contracts from the 60-day advance termination filing requirement if they expire by their own terms.
- **F.2c** reconstructed the timeline. The December 29, 2025 effective date matches what South Tahoe Now reported as the original cessation deadline. The amendment most likely extended service from December 29, 2025 to May 2027 while setting a firm, non-renewable termination date -- an 18-month bridge rather than a fifth open-ended extension.

**What this means for the thesis:** The PPA is confirmed as FERC-jurisdictional (the Section 205(d) filing proves this). No termination/cancellation filing exists because the amendment likely built the end date into the contract itself, and 35.15(b) exempts natural expirations from advance filing. FERC retains Section 206 authority to intervene regardless. The November 2025 amendment was filed four months before any public disclosure -- filed alongside 24 other routine filings in a Federal Register combined notice, with a 21-day comment period over the Thanksgiving holiday. No party protested. Nobody noticed.

**Confidence:** HIGH on the filing's existence and timing. MODERATE (75%) on the interpretation that the amendment set the May 2027 end date, because we cannot access the actual filing document (FERC eLibrary interactive access required).

### F.3: Contract Terms -- The 16-Year Temporary Arrangement

The contract history reveals a pattern of rolling dependency. The original 2009 PPA was explicitly temporary: a five-year full-requirements supply at NV Energy's system average cost, with one five-year renewal option. The CPUC approved it as integral to the asset sale. The original framework contemplated supply through 2020 at most.

Instead: extended in 2015 (Liberty had not found alternatives), extended again in 2020 (same reason), extended a final time in late 2025 -- but this time with a hard stop. Liberty's own 2015 CPUC testimony reveals the structural trap: "Liberty Utilities has procured essentially all of its electrical energy from Sierra Pacific Power Company." Sixteen years later, that dependency is barely reduced -- Liberty generates approximately 25% from its own solar, and 75% still comes from NV Energy.

**What this means for the thesis:** Both the "planned transition" and "data center redirect" narratives are partially true, and the synthesis is what matters. The contract was always going to end. The data center boom determined when. NV Energy extended three times over 16 years, each time because Liberty had not yet secured alternatives. The refusal to extend a fourth time coincides precisely with 7,600 MW of data center capacity requests in NV Energy's territory. The framing of "planned transition" is factually correct about contract structure but misleading about proximate cause.

**Confidence:** HIGH. Contract history is well-documented across CPUC decisions, SEC filings, and FERC rate schedule records.

### F.4: IRP Capacity -- The Resource Allocation in Black and White

NV Energy's 2024 Integrated Resource Plan is the closest thing to a documentary smoking gun. The numbers: 39 bundled-service projects requesting 7,600 MW of capacity additions by 2033. Of those, 12 data center projects account for 5,900 MW -- 78% of all requested capacity. Sierra Pacific's 704B annual limit was set to 0 MW during the 2024-2027 action plan period because of severe import capacity constraints. Zero megawatts of available capacity. Every MW allocated to data centers is a MW unavailable for Liberty's wholesale service.

The 2025 IRP Amendment (PUCN Docket 25-10028) is even more explicit: it lists "the Liberty Utilities LLC load servicing agreement, previously scheduled to end in December 2025, has been extended until May 2027" as a load forecast adjustment. The amendment exists because 144 MW of approved resources were cancelled or delayed, and the utility needed a 150 MW battery storage system by July 2027 to fill the gap. Liberty's contract end date is being managed as a capacity planning variable alongside data center growth -- not as a community service obligation.

**What this means for the thesis:** The IRP does not contain a sentence reading "we are terminating Liberty to serve data centers." It does not need to. The resource allocation math speaks: 0 MW of available import capacity, 5,900 MW of data center demand, Liberty's termination treated as a planning input. This is not a conspiracy. It is a structural outcome of utility obligation hierarchy: when capacity is constrained, the voluntary wholesale customer loses to mandatory bundled-service industrial customers.

**Confidence:** HIGH. IRP data comes from NV Energy's own filings, verified against Sierra Club expert testimony and PUCN docket records.

### F.5: Constitutional and Environmental Justice -- Who Loses

The constitutional analysis confirms what the legal framework predicted: Jackson v. Metropolitan Edison blocks the strongest constitutional claim. The state action doctrine has only tightened since 1974. However, the NV Energy/Tahoe situation presents the strongest factual basis for distinguishing Jackson since that case was decided: 49,000 customers (not one), state-directed tax incentives actively attracting data centers, and a PUCN-approved resource plan explicitly prioritizing data center load.

The demographic data complicates the "wealthy ski town" narrative. South Lake Tahoe is 26.1% Hispanic/Latino -- predominantly service-industry workers. 50.7% of the workforce earns less than $49,000/year. The homeownership rate is 51.3%, well below the national 65.2%. Electricity rates have already increased approximately 77% since late 2022. These are hotel staff, restaurant workers, and ski resort employees facing rate shocks they cannot absorb, living in a community where 90% of households earning less than 30% of area median income have unmet housing needs.

On the other side: $457 million in state tax incentives for data centers that create approximately 300 permanent jobs. That is $1.5 million in public subsidy per permanent data center job, versus approximately $190,000 per job for the Tesla Gigafactory deal at the same industrial park.

**What this means for the thesis:** The constitutional path is weak under current doctrine but the environmental justice framing is powerful. The disparity is real: state-subsidized facilities requiring minimal employment are displacing power from a working-class service community. The legal pathway runs through CPUC proceedings and FERC wholesale oversight, not constitutional litigation. But the EJ frame is what gives this story political salience beyond a regulatory dispute.

**Confidence:** HIGH on demographics and incentive math. MODERATE on constitutional analysis (the NV Energy distinction from Jackson is novel and untested).

## 3. The Legal Architecture (Core Argument)

The American electricity system operates through three regulatory layers that were never designed to interact coherently under conditions of scarcity:

**Layer 1: The Retail Obligation.** Every state requires utilities to serve all qualified customers within their territory. NV Energy must serve data centers at TRIC because they are bundled-service customers in Sierra Pacific's territory. This is not optional. Liberty must serve its 49,000 California customers because it is a CPUC-regulated utility. This is also not optional.

**Layer 2: The Wholesale Market.** Power flowing between states -- like NV Energy selling to Liberty for resale to California customers -- falls under FERC jurisdiction. But wholesale service is contractual. The obligation lasts only as long as the contract. When NV Energy's contract with Liberty expires, the wholesale obligation dissolves. FERC has authority to review terminations (Section 206), but this authority is not self-executing. Someone must invoke it.

**Layer 3: The Resource Planning Framework.** PUCN approves NV Energy's Integrated Resource Plan, which determines what generation and transmission gets built. The 2024 IRP shows 78% of load growth driven by data centers. The PUCN approved this plan. But the PUCN does not appear to have ruled on whether existing wholesale customers should be prioritized over new industrial ones. The IRP treats Liberty's contract as a planning input, not a community obligation.

**The gap:** When these three layers collide -- when a utility's retail obligation to serve industrial customers in its territory conflicts with a wholesale obligation to an out-of-state residential community -- the system has no mechanism for resolving the conflict in favor of residents. The retail obligation wins because it is statutory. The wholesale obligation loses because it is contractual. And the resource plan treats both as inputs to an optimization problem that maximizes utility return on invested capital.

**The FERC absence** is the critical feature, not a bug. The November 2025 PPA amendment (ER26-534-000) was filed under Section 205(d) and took effect December 29, 2025. The contract was executed after July 9, 1996, so under 18 CFR 35.15(b), no advance termination filing is required if the contract expires by its own terms. FERC will receive notice within 30 days after the May 2027 termination -- after the fact. FERC could intervene under Section 206 at any time, but nobody has asked it to. Not Liberty. Not California. Not the Sierra Club. Not Congress. The agency with jurisdiction has not been invoked. The mechanism that could potentially prevent this outcome requires affirmative action, and no party has taken it.

**The contract structure** is the final piece. The 2009 PPA was always temporary -- five years with one five-year option, contemplating Liberty's eventual energy independence. Three extensions over 16 years extended the timeline but not the fundamental logic. The late 2025 amendment was different: it set a firm, non-renewable end date of May 2027, calibrated to Greenlink West's expected operational date. NV Energy's position is that it has been patient for 16 years and Liberty has not achieved independence. That is factually true. It is also true that the capacity NV Energy needs for data centers makes a fourth extension impossible in a way that was not true for the first three.

**The synthesis:** The regulatory architecture of American electricity has a gap wide enough to drive a data center through. Obligation to serve dissolves at wholesale. Contract terms expire. FERC has jurisdiction but must be asked to act. The IRP optimizes for capital return, not community continuity. Nobody designed this outcome. The system produced it because the system was built for an era when wholesale relationships were stable, capacity was surplus, and nobody imagined that computational demand would compete with residential communities for the same electrons.

## 5. The National Pattern

Lake Tahoe is the sharpest case because it involves direct power diversion -- a utility explicitly terminating wholesale supply to a residential community while onboarding industrial data center customers in the same service territory. But the broader pattern is documented in at least 10 states and involves multiple mechanisms:

**Direct Diversion (Nevada):** NV Energy terminates wholesale supply to 49,000 Lake Tahoe customers. 78% of projected load growth is data centers. 0 MW of available import capacity in northern Nevada.

**Cost Shifting via Wholesale Markets (PJM -- 13 states + DC):** PJM capacity auction prices rose from $28.92/MW-day (2024/2025 delivery year) to $329.17/MW-day (2026/2027) and $333.44/MW-day (2027/2028) -- an approximately 11x increase over two delivery years. PJM's Independent Market Monitor attributed the increases "almost entirely" to existing and projected large data center load additions. Total 2025/2026 Base Residual Auction cost: $14.7 billion, borne by 67 million ratepayers. Maryland's Office of People's Counsel filed a Section 206 complaint at FERC alleging $1.6 billion in data-center-driven transmission costs unfairly allocated to residential ratepayers over the next decade.

**Retail Rate Shock (Virginia):** Bloomberg documented wholesale electricity costs up 267% in areas near data centers compared to five years ago. Consumer Reports profiled residents whose bills doubled or tripled. Loudoun County -- 200+ data centers in 30 square miles -- is fighting Dominion Energy transmission lines routed through residential neighborhoods to energize data centers.

**Behind-the-Meter Bypass (Nevada, National):** Fleet Data Centers filed for 362 MW of behind-the-meter natural gas generation at TRIC because NV Energy cannot serve them for 2-3 years. Nationally, Cleanview documented 46 data centers building 56 GW of behind-the-meter power, nearly 75% gas-fired. These facilities bypass state renewable portfolio standards, utility oversight, and public review.

**Infrastructure Cost Socialization (Georgia, Pennsylvania):** Georgia Power's $16B data center expansion, backstopped by a $26.5B DOE loan. Three Mile Island restart ($1.6B, DOE $1B loan) funded by a 20-year PPA with Microsoft.

**Grid Reliability Risk (National):** NERC's Level 3 alert (May 4, 2026) -- highest severity, rarely issued -- targeted data centers for unexpectedly dropping or oscillating load, posing "immediate risks" to the bulk power system. Seven mandatory Essential Actions required by August 3, 2026.

**The strongest national-scale numbers:**

- EPRI (May 2026): Data centers projected to consume 9-17% of all US electricity by 2030 -- up 60% from EPRI's own 2024 estimate.
- McKinsey: 11-12% of total US power demand by 2030, a tripling from current levels.
- Goldman Sachs: 165% increase in data center power demand by 2030; 47 GW of new generation needed within 5 years.
- ERCOT: Texas interconnection queue includes 205 GW of large load, approximately 70% data-center-related.

The structural bottleneck that makes conflicts like Lake Tahoe inevitable: data centers can be built in under 2 years. Grid connections take up to 7 years in congested markets. This mismatch guarantees that demand will outstrip infrastructure wherever data centers cluster, and the conflicts will be resolved by the existing regulatory framework -- which, as Lake Tahoe demonstrates, systematically favors the new industrial customer over the existing residential one.

## 6. The Human Cost

The narrative about Lake Tahoe must contend with -- and correct -- the assumption that this is a wealthy ski town where affluent second-home owners face a minor inconvenience.

The census data tells a different story. South Lake Tahoe (population ~21,300) is 26.1% Hispanic/Latino. The largest employment sectors are Accommodation & Food Services (2,276 people), Health Care (1,440), and Retail (1,374). More than half the workforce -- 50.7% -- earns less than $49,000 per year. The homeownership rate is 51.3%, well below the national average of 65.2%, because median home values ($682,400) are double the national median. The 9.3% poverty rate is lower than the national average but masks severe housing cost burden: 62% of workforce housing need is for households below 120% of area median income, and 90% of households earning below 30% AMI have unmet housing needs. Approximately half of Tahoe workers commute from outside the region because they cannot afford to live where they work.

Electricity rates have already risen approximately 77% since late 2022. Liberty's 2024 general rate case sought a 19.1% revenue increase; the CPUC approved 11.4%. Contributing factors include wildfire mitigation costs, insurance premiums exceeding $30 million annually, and infrastructure upgrades. Replacement power procurement will add additional cost pressure on top of already-strained household budgets.

Liberty's grid is physically isolated. It sits entirely within NV Energy's Balancing Authority Area, connects to NV Energy at 38 points, and has no connection to the California ISO grid. Building an independent transmission line from California "would cost hundreds of millions of dollars" with "significant land impacts" (Liberty President Eric Schwarzrock). The only viable alternative pathway is Greenlink West -- the $4.2 billion ratepayer-funded line that terminates behind Google's TRIC campus and has 80% of its capacity already committed to data centers.

Tahoe Spark CEO Danielle Hughes: "Short term, you can commonly get good deals, but it's unstable. The short-term deal gets you through. But then you're in the Western market, competing against PG&E, Southern California Edison, data centers, and mining companies. We're 49,000 customers. We have no leverage."

Sue Hughes (Tahoe Spark/community advocate, separately): "Even though we have low-income communities in both South Lake Tahoe and North Lake Tahoe, Kings Beach, both the Energy Commission and the California Public Utility Commission do not include us in any of their socioeconomic plans."

The incentive math distills the disparity. $457 million in state tax incentives for data centers at TRIC, alongside approximately 300 promised permanent jobs. That is $1.5 million in public subsidy per permanent data center position. The people who clean hotel rooms, serve meals, and run ski lifts in Lake Tahoe are paying higher electricity rates to fund transmission infrastructure that will primarily serve the companies receiving those incentives.

## 8. Net Assessment

### Direction

The weight of evidence supports a single conclusion: the legal architecture of American electricity regulation contains a structural gap that allows wholesale power to be redirected from residential communities to industrial data center customers without any single regulatory body having both the jurisdiction and the mandate to prevent it. Lake Tahoe is the first case where this gap has produced a complete diversion -- an entire community losing its primary power source. It will not be the last.

### Confidence

**Overall assessment confidence: HIGH.**

The core thesis rests on NV Energy's own regulatory filings (the IRP, the FERC amendment, the IRP amendment), verified contract history across CPUC decisions and SEC disclosures, census demographics, and structured financial data from federal disclosure systems. The weakest link is the inability to read the actual text of ER26-534-000, which prevents confirmation that the November amendment set the May 2027 hard stop -- but the convergence of circumstantial evidence from five independent sources places this at 75% probability.

### What This Means

Three implications beyond the immediate Lake Tahoe story:

1. **The wholesale/retail distinction is a systemic vulnerability.** Any community served by a wholesale arrangement -- particularly one crossing state lines -- is exposed to the same dynamic. The obligation to serve protects retail customers within a utility's territory but provides no protection to wholesale customers when capacity becomes scarce. As data center demand tightens capacity nationwide, more wholesale relationships will come under pressure.

2. **FERC is the missing actor.** The agency with jurisdiction over the wholesale relationship (FERC) has not been engaged by any party. FERC Chair Swett has signaled appetite for aggressive action on data center grid conflicts. The Lake Tahoe case is a natural vehicle for FERC intervention under Section 206, but only if someone asks. The absence of a FERC complaint is the most consequential non-event in this story.

3. **The political window is open.** The bipartisan GRID Act, NERC's Level 3 alert, community moratoriums in 5+ states, the NAACP's xAI lawsuit, 120+ organizations lobbying Congress, and Pew polling showing broad public negativity toward data center expansion all indicate that the political conditions for regulatory reform exist. Whether they are sufficient to overcome $1.2B+ in combined utility and data center industry lobbying (BHE plus Data Center Coalition in Q1 2026 alone) is an open question.

### What Would Change the Picture

- FERC initiates a Section 206 proceeding or issues a show-cause order on the NV Energy/Liberty termination.
- Liberty files a formal complaint at FERC rather than relying solely on the CPUC track.
- The June 2026 FERC large-load interconnection rulemaking includes provisions addressing wholesale service termination priorities.
- NV Energy reverses course and offers a further extension (low probability given capacity constraints).
- Greenlink West completion slips past May 2027, leaving Liberty with no wholesale supply and no alternative transmission pathway.
- Congress passes the GRID Act or equivalent legislation mandating residential ratepayer priority.

## 9. Probability Assessments

### Liberty secures replacement power by May 2027

**Probability: 55%**

Evidence for (raises probability):
- Greenlink West expected operational May 2027, providing access to broader supplier pool
- NV Energy spokesperson said Liberty would be "first in the waiting line" for Greenlink access
- Liberty filed CPUC advice letter in March 2026, giving ~14 months for expedited procurement
- Liberty has some self-generation (25% from solar)

Evidence against (lowers probability):
- 4,000 MW of Greenlink West's 5,000 MW capacity already committed to data centers
- Liberty's grid is physically isolated within NV Energy's Balancing Authority; no CAISO connection
- Greenlink West construction timeline is tight and any delay could miss the May 2027 deadline
- Liberty has failed to achieve energy independence in 16 years of attempts
- Western energy market competition from PG&E, SCE, data centers, and mining companies dwarfs Liberty's 49,000-customer base
- Danielle Hughes: "We're 49,000 customers. We have no leverage."

Assessment: Liberty will likely secure some replacement arrangement, but at significantly higher cost and potentially not fully equivalent to the current full-requirements service. Partial replacement (bridge contracts, expanded solar, short-term market purchases) is more likely than a clean, cost-neutral transition. The risk of temporary supply gaps or emergency arrangements is real.

### FERC intervenes to extend service

**Probability: 15%**

Evidence for:
- FERC has clear jurisdiction under FPA Sections 205/206
- FERC Chair Swett has stated appetite to push jurisdictional boundaries on data center conflicts
- El Paso Electric v. FERC (2000) precedent: FERC ordered temporary wholesale sales under Section 202(b)
- Otter Tail Power (1973): Supreme Court upheld orders against utility refusing wholesale sales
- FERC recently found PJM data center co-location rules "unjust and unreasonable" under Section 206
- Maryland OPC Section 206 complaint signals data center cost-shifting is reaching FERC

Evidence against:
- No party has filed a complaint or petition at FERC. FERC does not typically act sua sponte on bilateral wholesale matters.
- 18 CFR 35.15(b) exempts post-1996 contracts from advance termination filing, giving NV Energy procedural cover
- The contract has been repeatedly described as temporary since 2009; forcing extension contradicts contract terms
- Current FERC composition under Trump administration may be less sympathetic to intervention
- Political dynamics: Trump's July 2025 EO fast-tracked data center permitting; intervening against data centers contradicts administration policy

Assessment: FERC intervention requires someone to invoke it. As of May 15, 2026, nobody has. Even if a complaint is filed, the procedural and political barriers are substantial. The most plausible FERC action would be a limited investigation rather than an order compelling continued service. The probability rises significantly if Liberty, California officials, or a Congressional delegation files a formal petition.

### Similar conflicts emerge in other states within 12 months

**Probability: 92%**

Evidence for:
- Conflicts already documented in 10+ states (Virginia, Georgia, Pennsylvania, Texas, Ohio, Oregon, Wisconsin, Indiana, Michigan, plus others)
- PJM 11x capacity price increase already impacting 67 million ratepayers across 13 states
- NERC Level 3 alert confirms grid reliability is already compromised by data center load patterns
- EPRI projects data center share of US electricity doubling by 2030 (conservative) to quadrupling (aggressive)
- Community moratoriums spreading rapidly; more rejections in Q1 2026 than all of 2025
- The structural mismatch (2-year data center build vs. 7-year grid connection) guarantees continued conflicts
- ERCOT queue: 205 GW of large load, ~70% data center

Evidence against:
- Industry self-regulation (Ratepayer Protection Pledge) could reduce friction
- State PUCs in Michigan, Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, and Oregon are proactively creating cost-allocation tariffs
- Some projects may be cancelled or delayed due to supply chain, permitting, or capital market constraints

Assessment: This is the highest-confidence probability in the assessment. Conflicts are not emerging -- they have already emerged. The question is whether they escalate to Lake Tahoe's severity (direct power diversion) or remain at the level of rate increases and transmission disputes. Both outcomes qualify as "similar conflicts."

### Federal legislation addressing data center grid impact passes within 24 months

**Probability: 20%**

Evidence for:
- Bipartisan GRID Act (Hawley R-MO / Blumenthal D-CT) introduced February 2026 -- first Congressional proposal
- 120+ organizations sent letter to Congress in April 2026 opposing data center fast-tracking
- NERC Level 3 alert and EPRI projections provide technical grounding for legislative action
- Pew polling shows broad public negativity toward data center expansion
- State-level action (Michigan, Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, Oregon, Georgia) creates pressure for federal harmonization

Evidence against:
- Trump administration's July 2025 EO fast-tracked data center permitting; legislation restricting data centers contradicts White House policy
- Ratepayer Protection Pledge (signed by Google, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle, xAI, OpenAI, Amazon) gives the administration political cover
- Combined lobbying spend: BHE + Data Center Coalition = $1.2M+ in Q1 2026 alone, plus individual company lobbying
- Congressional gridlock on energy legislation is baseline; even bipartisan bills face committee bottlenecks
- The GRID Act would need to navigate Commerce, Energy, and Finance committees in both chambers
- Data centers are now framed as national security infrastructure (AI competitiveness with China), which raises political cost of restrictions

Assessment: The GRID Act or similar legislation is more likely to be introduced, debated, and used as a messaging vehicle than to pass. The more probable federal action is FERC rulemaking (the large-load interconnection ANOPR expected June 2026) rather than legislation. The 24-month window spans a midterm election cycle, which could either accelerate populist energy (rate shock as campaign issue) or freeze legislative action.

## 10. Open Questions

### What We Do Not Know

1. **The actual text of ER26-534-000.** The November 2025 FERC amendment is the pivotal document. We have strong circumstantial evidence that it set the May 2027 termination date, but we have not read the filing itself. The transmittal letter would explain what changed and why. This is accessible via FERC eLibrary interactive browser access (accession 20251117-5192).

2. **Whether anyone will file at FERC before May 2027.** Liberty, the CPUC, California's Attorney General, the Sierra Club, or a Congressional delegation could invoke FERC's Section 206 authority. As of May 15, none has. The decision tree for this conflict branches fundamentally on whether FERC is engaged.

3. **Greenlink West's actual delivery date.** The May 2027 target is tight. NV Energy's $4.2B, 472-mile project has no public margin for delay. If Greenlink West slips even a few months, Liberty faces a gap with no wholesale supply and no alternative transmission pathway.

4. **How much of Greenlink West's capacity Liberty can actually access.** NV Energy has committed 4,000 MW of 5,000 MW to data center operators. Liberty was told it would be "first in the waiting line" -- but the line forms behind 4,000 MW of prior commitments.

5. **Algonquin's risk disclosure.** The parent company's Q3 2025 earnings (November 7, 2025 -- 10 days before the FERC filing) did not mention the Liberty/NV Energy situation. The Q4 2025 10-Q (expected to contain risk factor disclosure) was not accessible. Whether this omission constitutes a material disclosure gap is an open question.

6. **What NV Energy is charging data center customers.** The Liberty PPA rate was $104.29/MWh at system average cost. NV Energy's data center tariff rates, economic development riders, and any preferential pricing arrangements are not publicly documented in our research.

7. **The behind-the-meter scaling trajectory.** Cleanview documented 56 GW of behind-the-meter data center power nationally, 75% gas-fired. If this model scales, data centers could increasingly bypass the regulated grid entirely -- reducing the pressure on wholesale markets but also reducing utility revenue and regulatory oversight simultaneously.

8. **Whether Liberty's CPUC expedited RFP process will be upgraded to a full proceeding.** The Sierra Club and Tahoe Spark have argued that the scale of the procurement (49,000 ratepayers on an isolated grid in a high wildfire risk area) demands full CPUC proceeding status. The CPUC's decision on this procedural question determines how much public scrutiny the replacement procurement receives.

### What Would Change the Assessment

- **Upward on FERC intervention:** A formal Section 206 complaint filed by any party; Congressional letter to FERC; FERC Chair Swett publicly references the Lake Tahoe case.
- **Downward on FERC intervention:** The June 2026 FERC rulemaking explicitly declines to address wholesale termination priorities; NV Energy successfully argues 35.15(b) exemption in any challenge.
- **Upward on Liberty replacement success:** Greenlink West completes on schedule; Liberty secures a long-term PPA at competitive rates through the CPUC RFP process; NV Energy cooperates on transmission access.
- **Downward on Liberty replacement success:** Greenlink West delayed; RFP produces only expensive short-term offers; NV Energy creates friction on transmission access for Liberty's replacement suppliers.
- **Upward on federal legislation:** Energy rate shock becomes 2026 midterm campaign issue; NERC Level 3 alert triggers Congressional hearings; additional community-level crises emerge before November 2026.
- **Downward on federal legislation:** Ratepayer Protection Pledge is treated as sufficient self-regulation; administration frames data centers as critical national security infrastructure; GRID Act dies in committee.
