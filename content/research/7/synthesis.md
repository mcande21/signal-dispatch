## Signal Convergence

All eight workers converge on the following as established fact:

**The legal chain is real and complete.** *Learning Resources Inc v. Trump* (Feb 20, 2026) is a genuine SCOTUS ruling on the merits — statutory, not merely procedural. IEEPA's "regulate importation" language does not authorize tariffs. The ruling covers all IEEPA-based tariffs universally; it is not limited to the specific orders challenged. Section 232 and Section 301 authority are explicitly preserved.

**A domestic court refund mechanism now exists.** The CIT issued a reliquidation order in *Atmus* (March 4, 2026) directing CBP to reliquidate entries without IEEPA duties. This is the enforcement mechanism — it is what separates this situation from the Bush steel tariff precedent (WTO win + no domestic order = no refunds). Workers 4 and 6 both independently identify this as the critical differentiating variable.

**CBP acknowledged it cannot immediately comply.** CBP filed an affidavit on March 6, 2026 stating non-compliance with the reliquidation order and obtained a stay while building the CAPE refund processing platform, targeting approximately April 20 readiness. The court accepted this. CBP must report progress by March 19, 2026.

**The administration is building Section 301 as a structural replacement.** USTR launched new Section 301 investigations immediately after the IEEPA ruling. This does not moot the refund obligation for duties already collected but signals the administration's intent to maintain tariff-equivalent pressure through IEEPA-immune authority.

**Historical precedent favors refunds when domestic court orders exist.** Nixon's TWEA surcharge (Yoshida, 1974) produced refunds. The Harbor Maintenance Tax (Shoe Corp.) produced refunds with interest. The common variable: a domestic court reliquidation order. The IEEPA situation now has one.

## Signal Divergence

**Kalshi and Polymarket are measuring fundamentally different things.** This is the most important finding in the research corpus and the primary source of analytical confusion:

| Platform | Resolution trigger | Current price | Implication |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|-------------|
| Kalshi (KXTARIFFREFUND) | Court *orders* a refund | 66c | May already be YES — CIT ordered March 4 |
| Polymarket | Importers *actually receive* money | 29% | Genuinely uncertain; requires operational execution |

Workers 1 and 2 identified this independently. The 66c Kalshi price may be pricing a near-certain legal event (court order already issued), not a probability question about actual payment.

**The slow-walk hypothesis is structurally coherent but not confirmed as intentional.** Worker 5 identified five structural vectors through which CBP can delay indefinitely without technically defying the court: no hard compliance deadline (CIT suspended its own order), ACH registration as prerequisite, individual filing required from each of 330,566 importers, 2-year statutory maximum response window on protests, and CAPE as a "working on it" procedural shield. Whether this is deliberate or operational is unresolved — the structure is indistinguishable from intentional delay regardless.

**The 180-day protest clock divergence.** The clock for importers to file protest claims runs concurrent with the CAPE system build. Rights are being extinguished in real time for importers who don't file. This creates a distinction between "importers entitled to refunds" (shrinking as days pass without filings) and "importers who will eventually receive refunds" (a subset requiring successful navigation of the individual filing system). Workers 5 and 6 flag this; Workers 0-4 do not foreground it.

**Barrett's "mess" comment was at oral argument (Nov 5, 2025), not in opinion.** She flagged remedy complexity before the ruling, voted to strike anyway, and the majority said nothing about remedy in the opinion. Kavanaugh quoted her in dissent as evidence of majority irresponsibility. The Court made a deliberate choice: rule on the legal question, leave the $160-175B cleanup to CBP and lower courts. This is not ambiguous — it's a feature, not an oversight.

**Section 122 as a wild card.** The 24-state coalition led by Oregon (filed March 5, 2026) challenges the Section 122 replacement tariffs on separate statutory grounds. If successful, the replacement tariff regime could also fall, compounding the refund exposure substantially. This track is entirely separate from the IEEPA litigation and was underweighted by most workers.

## Preliminary Assessment

### Bear case — No meaningful refunds before June 30 (40%)

The structural slow-walk is the most operational-reality-consistent scenario. CIT suspended its own order. CBP has no hard compliance deadline. The 180-day protest clock running concurrent with CAPE development means importer rights are being extinguished while the system is "being built." The administration has strong political incentive to delay: every day without payment is a day tariff pressure remains economically operative. Even if CAPE launches April 20, processing 327,000+ ACH-enrolled importers through an individual-claim system in 70 days approaches operational impossibility at scale. The 2-year statutory window is fully available without defying any court.

**Trigger for bear resolution:** CAPE launches late, or launches on time but processes trivially small volume before June 30. No hard court deadline imposed by CIT.

### Base case — Partial, token, or definitional refunds (35%)

CBP launches CAPE, processes a meaningful subset of enrolled importers (perhaps 50-100K of the 327K with ACH). Kalshi resolves YES on the "court order" criterion — it may already be YES. Polymarket resolves NO because the contract's "importers actually receive money" criterion is not met at sufficient scale by June 30. The operational failure is real but partial, not total. Litigation over the scope of the stay and what counts as "compliance" drags into Q3 2026.

**Trigger:** CAPE operational, small-to-medium processing volume, platform-dependent resolution.

### Bull case — Substantial refunds before June 30 (25%)

CIT imposes a hard compliance deadline at the March 19 progress hearing or thereafter. CBP — under court order with contempt exposure — expedites CAPE and processes high ACH-enrollment importers at scale. Political calculation shifts: refund payments become a policy tool (importing companies as constituency). Courts reject further stays. The 99% ACH enrollment rate means the technical barrier is lower than the political narrative suggests.

**Trigger:** March 19 CIT hearing produces hard deadline; CAPE launches on schedule and processes volume.

### Overall assessment

**Base estimate for "some importers actually receive money before June 30": 38-42%**

This is consistent with our prior 42% estimate. The research confirms the prior was appropriately calibrated. Kalshi at 66c is significantly overpriced if the contract resolves on actual payment — but may be correctly priced if it resolves on the court *order* (which is already issued). **The most important analytical action is clarifying the Kalshi contract resolution criterion.** If Kalshi = "court orders refund," 66c is cheap. If Kalshi = "importers receive money," 66c is expensive relative to our 42%.

## Research Gaps

1. **Kalshi contract language.** The exact resolution text and source for KXTARIFFREFUND is unconfirmed. This is the highest-priority gap — it changes the analytical conclusion on whether to trade Kalshi vs. Polymarket.

2. **March 19 CIT hearing outcome.** The next scheduled court checkpoint is March 19. What the court requires of CBP at this hearing substantially changes the bear vs. bull probability. This is a near-term binary event.

3. **CAPE system technical status.** Whether CAPE is on track for the ~April 20 target is unknown. CBP operational reporting will be the signal.

4. **Protest filing rate among affected importers.** How many of the 330,566 importers have filed protests within the 180-day window? Low filing rate = smaller eligible refund pool = smaller eventual payment even if system works.

5. **Section 122 litigation trajectory.** The 24-state coalition suit challenging replacement tariffs is entirely untracked in workers 0-4. If Section 122 also falls, refund exposure expands and the administration faces a second front. This has downstream effects on Section 301 timeline and political calculation.

6. **Congressional action.** Workers mapped the theoretical options (retroactive ratification, emergency legislation) but found no active legislative movement as of research date. Is any bill in committee? What is the Senate whip count on any IEEPA ratification measure?

7. **Thomas dissent significance.** Thomas argued unlimited tariff delegation is constitutionally permissible under the foreign commerce power. No other justice joined. This could matter if the administration tries to argue a constitutional override path — but it has zero current Court support.
